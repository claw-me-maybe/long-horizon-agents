# Magazine Feature: The Harper's Pitch and Lede — "The Last Ice Road"

## Occupation
- **SOC:** 27-3043
- **Title:** Writers and Authors

## Scenario
You are a freelance longform narrative journalist, previously published in The Atlantic and Virginia Quarterly Review. After three reporting trips in February and March 2026 to Inuvik and Tuktoyaktuk in Canada's Northwest Territories, you're pitching a 7,500-word feature to Harper's Magazine editor Christopher Beha about the final operating season of the Inuvik–Tuktoyaktuk ice road before climate warming renders it unbuildable for a third consecutive winter. You've spent 11 days with Joseph Kakfwi, a 61-year-old Gwich'in ice road scout, and the trip got complicated: a truck fell through the ice on March 4; the driver survived; Joseph has taken the incident personally. Beha needs the pitch by 9 AM EST April 22, 2026, to pitch the July issue at the next planning meeting.

## Inputs
Reporting inventory in hand:
- 47 hours of recorded interviews (Joseph, his nephew Colin, three other scouts, two drivers, one Hamlet council member, one climatologist at Aurora Research Institute).
- 380 photos, a dozen short videos (for reference, not to publish).
- Field notebooks: 6 moleskines, cross-indexed.
- The March 4 incident: truck owner was a Tuk-based resupply contractor; no fatality; Joseph said "I read it wrong" on the radio before the tow arrived.
- Two scenes you already know will anchor the piece: (1) Joseph augering a test hole at dawn, explaining to you why this winter's ice "sings wrong"; (2) the aftermath of the March 4 near-miss, Joseph in his kitchen unable to eat.
- One lyrical through-line you've been circling: the ice road as a seasonal grammar — verbs appearing only once a year — that is now becoming extinct.
- Harper's conventions: literary first-person narrative nonfirst reported voice, serifed gravitas, structural ambition, no news peg in lede, no "On a cold morning in February" openers, no present-tense historical, no SEO-friendly throat-clearing.

## Artifact specification
Produce a **feature pitch document** with two components:
1. **Editor's pitch letter** (~500-700 words) to Christopher Beha: why this story, why now, why you, proposed length and structure, reporting status (what you have / what remains), access, risks, why it's a Harper's piece specifically (not Atlantic, not NYT Magazine).
2. **Original lede** (900-1300 words) — the actual opening of the piece, in narrative form. Not a summary of the lede. The prose itself.

Voice (for the lede): first-person reported nonfiction, mid-distance (you are present but not the subject). Concrete sensory specificity. No "it's important to note" scaffolding. Let Joseph speak in his own register — don't smooth out his syntax. Avoid the two worst literary-journalism sins: (a) the writer's transformative epiphany in paragraph one, and (b) the weather-as-metaphor opening. The lede should earn its length by doing at least three things at once — introducing Joseph, introducing the road's doomed seasonality, and seeding the March 4 incident without spoiling it.

Voice (for the pitch): conversational-professional, an equal writing to an equal. Assume Beha's time. Don't explain what Harper's is. Don't tell him ice roads are interesting — show you know why this particular ice road, this particular scout, is a story. Name the specific section of the magazine if relevant (Folio, Readings, letter, feature well).

## Output format
Single markdown file. Pitch and lede as two clearly-separated sections. The lede should be continuous prose (paragraphs, not bullets). You may include a one-line "END LEDE" marker. Rough length: 1,500-2,200 words total.

## Iteration targets
1. Kill any sentence in the lede that could open any other magazine feature — cut to something only true of this story.
2. Resist the urge to front-load thesis in the pitch's first paragraph; open with scene or stake, not abstract framing.
3. The lede's ending must create propulsion — the reader should be unable to stop at the paragraph break. Test: does the final beat seed a question or open a door?
4. Calibrate Joseph's dialect — preserve rhythm and idiom without condescension or exoticism.
5. Prune at least one adjective per sentence in the lede's revision pass; literary nonfiction tolerates precision, not ornament.
6. Pitch should say out loud what the piece is *about* at a thematic level (extinction of seasonal knowledge, or whatever you choose) without turning into essay — one earned sentence, deep in the letter.

## Success criteria
- The lede would not be mistaken for a news article — it is literary in register, shape, and ambition.
- Joseph is a person by the end of the lede: specific tics, specific speech, at least one detail no generalization could predict.
- The pitch demonstrates a specific structural plan (3-5 movements, not "chapters TBD") without committing to rigidity.
- Beha could say yes or no on the basis of this document — he has enough to commit, not so much that he's reading a first draft.
- The March 4 incident is present in the lede but not resolved — ledes that give away the incident's emotional resolution fail.
- No cliché openings (weather, quote, dictionary definition, "imagine if..."), no false-immediacy present tense that drops away later, no "little did I know."

## Scope target
A 15-minute v1 should include: the pitch letter in full draft (first pass), and the first ~500-700 words of the lede (the opening scene with Joseph at the auger, stopping before the turn toward the March 4 material). Missing: the lede's second movement, polish pass on the pitch, and the final propulsive sentence of the lede.
