# Intro to Welding Lab: Measuring, Joint Prep, and Fillet Weld Readiness Check

## Occupation
- **SOC:** 25-1194
- **Title:** Career/Technical Education Teachers, Postsecondary

## Scenario
Mr. Alvarez teaches WLD 101, an entry-level welding certificate course at a regional community college. His evening cohort has 18 students, ages 18 to 41, including 4 apprentices returning to school, 3 multilingual learners, and 2 students with documented reading accommodations. The class meets in a 90-minute lecture-lab block followed by open shop time later in the week. This is the first hands-on session after shop orientation. He needs a practical, employer-facing lesson that teaches students to read a tape measure accurately, prep a lap joint, and complete a basic tack-weld setup safely before they attempt a fillet weld.

The program has been hearing the same feedback from local employers: new hires often know safety slogans but struggle with measurement accuracy, sequencing, and cleanup habits. Mr. Alvarez wants the first performance task to screen for those habits early so he can group students for coaching and prevent unsafe or sloppy work from becoming routine.

## Inputs
- **Program and industry framing:**
  - Career readiness expectations: punctuality, PPE compliance, tool care, communication, cleanup, and following directions
  - Program outcomes: shop safety, precision measurement, blueprint basics, weld prep, quality control, and professional behavior
  - Instructor priorities: reduce safety incidents, identify students who need extra measurement practice, and establish lab routines quickly
- **Learner profile:**
  - 18 students with mixed prior experience
  - 4 students have worked in manufacturing or construction and may rush through basics
  - 3 multilingual learners need clear visuals and short oral directions
  - 2 students have reading accommodations and need simplified text or read-aloud support
- **Course context:**
  - First skills-based class after safety orientation
  - Students have already watched a short demo on PPE, housekeeping, and shop rules
  - Next class will introduce weld symbols and the fillet weld bead sequence
- **Materials and equipment available:**
  - Welding booths, welding helmets, gloves, jackets, and safety glasses
  - Steel coupons, clamps, soapstone, tape measures, combination squares, angle gauges
  - Bench grinder and wire brush for joint prep
  - Printed job sheets, observation checklists, and a one-page safety reminder
- **Assessment needs:**
  - Instructor wants a quick performance check that can be scored during lab
  - A short written or oral exit ticket is needed to confirm measurement vocabulary and safety steps
  - Students who miss the standard should be routed to targeted re-teaching before the next weld attempt

## Artifact specification
Create a single-session CTE instruction packet using **backward design** and a **mastery-based skills check**. The artifact should be concrete enough to run in one class period, but modular enough to expand into a larger welding unit.

Required sections:
1. **Lesson metadata**: course title, program level, session length, class size, shop setting, and required equipment.
2. **Learning outcomes**: 3 to 5 measurable outcomes covering knowledge, hands-on skill, and workplace behavior. Each outcome should connect to program goals and safety expectations.
3. **Student-facing agenda**: a short, plain-language description of what students will do during class and what they must bring or wear.
4. **Instructor lesson plan**: minute-by-minute flow for a 90-minute block, including opener, demo, guided practice, independent lab work, and closeout.
5. **Job sheet / lab handout**: step-by-step task directions with key vocabulary, safety reminders, and quality expectations.
6. **Skills assessment**: an observation checklist or performance form with 10 to 12 scorable criteria spanning safety, tool handling, measurement accuracy, sequencing, cleanup, and communication.
7. **Rubric / scoring guide**: a behaviorally anchored rubric with clear pass/fail or 4-point descriptors. Identify any critical safety failures that automatically stop the task.
8. **Differentiation and accommodations**: supports for multilingual learners, readers needing accommodations, and advanced students who are ready for a harder measurement or layout challenge.
9. **Curriculum placement note**: one short paragraph showing where this lesson fits inside a 3- to 4-week welding module and what the next lesson should build on.
10. **Instructor feedback language**: a few sample phrases the instructor can use when coaching students during lab, especially for errors in measuring, sequencing, or PPE.

Pedagogical conventions: backward design, hands-on demonstration, guided practice, deliberate repetition, and mastery-based reattempts. Keep the language industry-appropriate but readable for a first-term student.

## Output format
Single Markdown file, about 1,500 to 2,200 words. Include:
- one table for the lesson flow
- one rubric table with at least 10 criteria
- one short example of completed instructor feedback or an annotated student job sheet

The artifact should read like something an instructor could use immediately, not a policy memo.

## Iteration targets
1. **Right-size the task** - v1 should stay inside one class period and avoid turning into a full course unit.
2. **Make safety decisive** - distinguish minor technique issues from critical safety failures so the checklist is useful in a real shop.
3. **Tighten rubric language** - replace vague terms like "good posture" with observable actions such as "keeps helmet down before arc start" or "verifies clamp placement before tacking."
4. **Improve employer alignment** - add visible references to quality, cleanup, and professional habits that local supervisors actually notice.
5. **Strengthen accommodations** - include at least two concrete supports for multilingual learners and two for students with reading or attention needs.
6. **Support reteaching** - make the reattempt pathway clear so students who miss the check know exactly what happens next.

## Success criteria
- The lesson outcomes are measurable and match the listed program goals.
- The lesson flow is realistic for a 90-minute lab block and includes a demo, practice, and assessment.
- The skills assessment has enough detail to separate safe, ready students from students who need re-teaching.
- Critical safety errors are clearly named and treated differently from routine mistakes.
- Differentiation includes practical shop-based supports, not generic classroom language.
- The curriculum placement note makes the artifact easy to plug into a larger welding sequence.
- The output can be used as a first draft without needing major structural repair.

## Scope target
For a 15-minute v1, produce the lesson metadata, outcomes, lesson flow, job sheet, and the skills assessment rubric. The differentiation, curriculum placement note, and instructor feedback language can be brief but should be present rather than omitted. The aim is a usable first-pass lab packet that can be sharpened later into a more complete welding module.
