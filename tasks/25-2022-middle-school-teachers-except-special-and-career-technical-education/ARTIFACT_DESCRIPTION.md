# 7th Grade Life Science 5E Unit: Cell Structure & Function with Models & Claims

## Occupation
- **SOC:** 25-2022
- **Title:** Middle School Teachers, Except Special and Career/Technical Education

## Scenario
Ms. Hartwell teaches four sections of 7th grade Life Science at Kennedy Middle School, a suburban Title I school of 720 students. Her focus section is 3rd period with 29 students: 5 on IEPs (including 2 with specific learning disabilities in reading, 1 with autism spectrum disorder, 1 with ADHD, 1 with an emotional disability), 7 multilingual learners (WIDA 2-4, with home languages including Arabic, Haitian Creole, and Spanish), and a wide range of science-identity levels (survey data shows 14/29 do not "see themselves as scientists"). The class meets 47 minutes/day, 5 days/week. The unit precedes the district's quarterly science benchmark in early December and follows the district's commitment to three-dimensional NGSS-aligned instruction using the OpenSciEd curriculum framework. The unit should center a **phenomenon**: tardigrades surviving extreme conditions (dehydration, vacuum of space, freezing, radiation). The science coach is available for two co-planning hours and one co-observation during the unit.

## Inputs
- **Standards (NGSS, MS-LS1 bundle):**
  - **MS-LS1-1** (conduct an investigation providing evidence that living things are made of cells; either one cell or many different numbers and types of cells)
  - **MS-LS1-2** (develop and use a model to describe the function of a cell as a whole and ways parts of cells contribute to the function)
  - **MS-LS1-3** (use argument supported by evidence for how the body is a system of interacting subsystems composed of groups of cells)
  - **Science and Engineering Practices:** Developing and Using Models; Constructing Explanations; Engaging in Argument from Evidence
  - **Crosscutting Concepts:** Structure and Function; Systems and System Models
- **Literacy standards (CCSS-ELA-Literacy, Grade 7):**
  - RST.6-8.7 (integrate quantitative or technical info in text with a version expressed visually)
  - WHST.6-8.1 (write arguments focused on discipline-specific content)
- **Prior unit:** Characteristics of life (completed), pre-assessment showed 68% of students hold "cells are only in animals" or "cells = atoms" misconceptions.
- **Materials available:** Class microscope set (16 scopes, student pairs), prepared slides (cheek cell, Elodea, onion skin), methylene blue, yeast, sugar, balloons, cellular respiration sensor probes (Vernier) x 8, Chromebooks, district-adopted OpenSciEd 7.1 anchor unit (partial).
- **Student asset data:** Class has strong drawers/artists (use for modeling), one student with a parent who is a nurse (possible guest speaker).
- **Assessment infrastructure:** District exit-ticket tracker, NGSS-aligned 3D rubric template.

## Artifact specification
Unit plan using the **5E Instructional Model (Engage-Explore-Explain-Elaborate-Evaluate)** and **NGSS storyline pedagogy** (phenomenon -> student questions -> investigations -> figuring out). Must reflect **Universal Design for Learning (UDL)** and disciplinary literacy integration.

Required sections:
1. **Unit overview**: anchor phenomenon (tardigrades / water bears surviving extreme conditions), driving question board seed questions, Performance Expectations targeted (MS-LS1-1, 2, 3), duration (15 instructional days).
2. **Storyline / arc**: how each lesson's learning builds toward explaining the anchor phenomenon — include a visual or described progression.
3. **Daily lesson sequence (15 days)**, each day mapped to a 5E phase and including:
   - Lesson focus question (student-facing)
   - 5E phase
   - SEP / CCC / DCI tags
   - Opening routine (Notice & Wonder, Do-Now)
   - Core investigation or task
   - Discourse structure (think-pair-share, Science Talk, gallery walk)
   - Consolidation / what we figured out
   - Formative check
4. **Anchor investigations**:
   - Microscope lab comparing cheek and onion cells (MS-LS1-1)
   - Cell model construction — students choose medium (physical, digital, analogy) (MS-LS1-2)
   - Yeast respiration investigation with CO2 probe (MS-LS1-3 evidence for systems)
5. **Text-dependent questions** set for at least one informational text (e.g., tardigrade Scientific American article excerpt).
6. **Summative performance task**: students construct a scientific argument (CER: Claim, Evidence, Reasoning) explaining how tardigrades survive extreme dehydration, using cell-level evidence.
7. **Differentiation**:
   - IEP accommodations (graphic organizers, chunked texts, peer reading)
   - MLL supports (frontloaded vocabulary with cognates, sentence frames for CER, visuals, labeling in home language on diagrams)
   - Extensions (comparison of prokaryotic vs. eukaryotic organelles; primary-source reading)
8. **Vocabulary instruction plan** — tiered word list (Tier 2: model, function, evidence; Tier 3: organelle, mitochondria, ribosome) with Frayer models and cognate bridges.
9. **Assessment blueprint** — formative checkpoints, summative rubric aligned to 3D NGSS.

Pedagogical conventions: 5E (BSCS); phenomenon-based learning (NGSS); three-dimensional NGSS (DCI + SEP + CCC); CER argumentation (McNeill & Krajcik); Science Talks (Michaels & O'Connor); ambitious science teaching (Windschitl, Thompson, Braaten).

**Suggested 15-day arc (for the downstream model to expand):**
- Day 1 (Engage): Meet the tardigrade. Video + Notice & Wonder. Build Driving Question Board.
- Day 2 (Engage/Explore): What are living things made of? Microscope introduction; pond water / yeast; generate questions.
- Day 3 (Explore): Cheek cell lab with methylene blue; record observations.
- Day 4 (Explore): Onion skin and Elodea cells; compare animal and plant cells.
- Day 5 (Explain): Synthesize — all living things are made of cells (MS-LS1-1 consolidation).
- Day 6 (Explore): Introduce organelles as functional parts; cell as "cell city" analogy vs. literal models (critique analogies).
- Day 7 (Elaborate): Student-choice cell model construction begins (MS-LS1-2).
- Day 8 (Elaborate): Cell model work day; peer feedback protocol.
- Day 9 (Explain): Model share-out; Science Talk about structure-function.
- Day 10 (Explore): Yeast respiration with CO2 probe; connect to mitochondria function (MS-LS1-3 evidence).
- Day 11 (Elaborate): Multicellular organization — tissues, organs, systems (MS-LS1-3).
- Day 12 (Elaborate): Text-dependent reading on tardigrades (Scientific American excerpt or NPR feature).
- Day 13 (Evaluate): Scaffolded CER draft — how do tardigrades survive dehydration?
- Day 14 (Evaluate): Peer revision of CER; teacher conferences.
- Day 15 (Evaluate): Final CER submission; whole-class debrief and synthesis.

2. **Balance across 3 dimensions** — ensure SEPs and CCCs are genuinely engaged, not just listed (e.g., students actually use a model, not just view one).
3. **Discourse quality** — move beyond "turn and talk" to named Science Talk protocols with sentence stems.
4. **Reading-science integration** — TDQs should require students to cross-reference text evidence with lab observations.
5. **Middle-school developmental fit** — include autonomy, identity, and peer structures (choice of model medium, student-generated questions, peer feedback protocols).
6. **Formative -> adjustment loop** — each formative check includes what the teacher does if most students miss it.

## Common 7th-grade misconceptions to address
- "Cells are only in animals / only in plants."
- "Cells = atoms" or "cells = molecules" (scale confusion).
- "The nucleus is the cell" (part-whole confusion).
- "Plants don't do cellular respiration" (they photosynthesize AND respire).
- "All cells are the same size and shape."
- "Mitochondria are only in animal cells."
- "Cell walls and cell membranes are the same thing."
- "Bigger organisms have bigger cells" (they have more cells, not bigger).

## Science Talk protocol seed (for the downstream model)
- **Before talk**: Teacher poses a focus question ("Based on our data, what does the mitochondrion do?"); 1-minute silent thinking; 2-min partner talk.
- **During talk**: Sentence stems posted ("I noticed ___", "I agree because ___", "I'm wondering about ___", "Can you say more about ___?"). Teacher tracks participation with a class map; revoices key ideas.
- **After talk**: Quick write — one claim you now believe more confidently, one question you still have.

## Pedagogical vocabulary glossary
- **5E model**: Engage, Explore, Explain, Elaborate, Evaluate (BSCS framework for science unit design).
- **NGSS 3D**: Three-dimensional learning = DCI (Disciplinary Core Idea) + SEP (Science & Engineering Practice) + CCC (Crosscutting Concept).
- **DCI/SEP/CCC**: NGSS dimension labels used in PE (Performance Expectation) citations.
- **Anchor phenomenon**: A puzzling, real-world observable event that grounds a unit's storyline.
- **Driving Question Board**: Student-generated questions posted, revisited, and answered across the unit.
- **CER**: Claim-Evidence-Reasoning; scientific argumentation framework.
- **Frayer model**: 4-quadrant vocabulary graphic organizer (definition, characteristics, examples, non-examples).
- **WIDA**: World-Class Instructional Design and Assessment; English-proficiency level framework (1-6).
- **Notice & Wonder**: Routine where students list observations and questions about a phenomenon before instruction.
