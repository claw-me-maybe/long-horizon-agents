## Part B — Teach-Back Education Plan

**How this plan is written:**
- Short sentences. Plain words.
- Any medical word is explained the first time: for example, **diuretic** = *a medicine that helps your body get rid of extra water through urine*.
- Every teach-back prompt is **open-ended**. The nurse carries the responsibility: "I want to be sure I explained this" — not "Did you get it?"
- Numbers are always paired with a concrete picture: 2 lb = about a large water bottle; 5 lb = about a bag of sugar.
- Picture cues are noted for the printed handout so the patient can recognize a topic by the icon, not just the word.

### 2. Low-salt eating (less than 2 grams of salt per day)

**Key message:**
Salt tells your body to hold on to water. Less salt in = less water in your lungs and legs. Your goal is less than 2 grams of salt per day. That is less than 1 teaspoon total from all the food you eat.

**Three easy rules:**
1. **Move the salt shaker off the table.** If it's not there, you won't reach for it.
2. **Check labels before you buy.** Aim for foods with **600 mg of sodium or less per meal**. The number on the label is in milligrams — 1,000 mg = 1 gram.
3. **Watch for the "salty 6":** canned or instant soup, lunch meat, hot dogs and bacon, pickles, cheese, frozen dinners. These have a lot of hidden salt.

**Better options:**
- Low-sodium canned soup, or soup made at home with fresh vegetables.
- Fresh chicken or fish instead of lunch meat.
- Herbs, lemon, pepper, or garlic powder for flavor instead of salt.

> *Picture on the handout:* a traffic-light chart — green foods (fresh veg, fruit, fresh meat, unsalted nuts), yellow (bread, dairy — watch serving size), red (canned soup, bacon, chips, pickles).

**Teach-back prompt:**
*"Tell me what you might have for lunch tomorrow. Picture the plate in your head. Which things on that plate might be high in salt, and what could you swap for them?"*

**Daughter's role:**
- On the next grocery trip, daughter and patient read the labels on his favorite canned soups together. Pick 2 or 3 low-sodium soups he likes.
- Daughter removes the table salt shaker tonight; a pepper shaker and a garlic-powder shaker go in its place.

### 5. Warning signs and emergencies

**Key message:**
Some symptoms are warnings — they mean **call the clinic today**. Others are emergencies — they mean **call 911 now**. The difference is whether you can still breathe and still talk.

**Warnings — call HF Clinic 555-0133 the same business day.** After hours or weekends, call the nurse line **555-0145**.

| Warning sign | What it might look like |
|---|---|
| Shoes or socks tighter than yesterday | You had to loosen the laces |
| You need an extra pillow to breathe at night | You were fine on 1 pillow last week, now you need 2 |
| New dry cough | Cough that started this week and won't stop |
| Felt dizzy once when standing | "Head rush" that took a few seconds to pass |
| Missed a dose and unsure what to do | Woke up and can't remember if you took your morning pills |
| Bruise you can't explain (on apixaban) | A big purple spot you don't remember bumping |

**Emergencies — call 911.** Do not drive yourself.

| Emergency sign | What it looks like |
|---|---|
| Cannot catch your breath even sitting still | Gasping, can't finish a sentence |
| Chest pain or pressure | Squeezing, tight, heavy feeling in the chest |
| You fainted, or felt like you were about to | Blacked out, fell, came back confused |
| Coughing pink foamy spit | Froth that looks pink or red |
| Severe bleeding that won't stop | Cut that keeps pouring after 10 minutes of pressure |
| Fell and hit your head | On a blood thinner — always treat this as an emergency |
| Very shaky, sweaty, confused — sugar doesn't fix it | Possible severe low blood sugar |
