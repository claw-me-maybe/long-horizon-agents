## Part B — Teach-Back Education Plan

**Plain-language rules used below:** Short sentences. Active voice. Pictures where suggested. Any medical word is defined the first time it is used. Every prompt is open-ended so the nurse — not the patient — carries the responsibility for clear teaching.

### 1. Daily weight monitoring

**Key message (for the patient):**
Every morning, step on your scale. Do it after you use the bathroom. Do it before you eat or drink. Wear the same kind of clothes — like a T-shirt and underwear. Write the number on your log sheet right away. Your weight tells us if water is building up inside your body.

> *Picture cue on the patient handout:* simple icon showing a scale, a toilet, and a sun (morning).

**Target (dry) weight:** about **181 lb (82 kg)**.

**Call thresholds — post on the fridge:**
- Up **2 lb in 1 day** (about the weight of a large water bottle) → call HF Clinic **555-0133**
- Up **5 lb in 1 week** (about the weight of a bag of sugar) → call HF Clinic **555-0133**
- Up **more than 5 lb in 2 days and not going down** → call **911**

**Teach-back prompt (nurse says):**
*"I want to be sure I explained this in a way that makes sense. Can you show me — pretend I am you tomorrow morning. Walk me through every step: where you will weigh yourself, what you will wear, what you will write down, and what number would make you pick up the phone."*

**Caregiver role:** Daughter to check the log each evening when she gets home from work and make sure the morning weight was written down. If the number was missed, she helps her dad weigh once — even though evening is not ideal, a number is better than none, and she notes "evening" next to it.

### 2. Low-sodium diet (less than 2 g salt per day)

**Key message:**
Salt tells your body to hold on to water. Less salt means less swelling and easier breathing. Your target is less than 2 grams of salt each day. That is less than 1 teaspoon total — from all foods combined.

**Easy rules:**
- Do not add salt at the table. Take the salt shaker off the table.
- Canned soup and instant soup are usually very high in salt. Look for "low-sodium" on the label, or make soup at home with fresh vegetables.
- Lunch meat, bacon, hot dogs, pickles, cheese, and frozen dinners are high in salt.
- Try herbs, lemon, pepper, or garlic instead of salt.
- Check labels — aim for 600 mg sodium or less per meal.

> *Picture cue:* a traffic-light chart on the 2 g sodium handout (v3.1) showing green/yellow/red foods.

**Teach-back prompt:**
*"Tell me about a meal you might eat tomorrow. What is on your plate? Which parts might have a lot of salt, and what could you swap in instead?"*

**Caregiver role:** Daughter to help check canned-soup labels on the next grocery trip and pick 2–3 lower-sodium options the patient likes.

---

**No fluid restriction is ordered right now.** Drink when you are thirsty. Do not force extra water. If your weight starts going up, the HF clinic may ask you to limit fluids later — we will tell you the exact amount if that happens.

### 5. Symptom red flags

**Key message:**
Some symptoms are warnings — they mean call the clinic today. Others are emergencies — they mean call 911. Knowing which is which keeps you out of the hospital.

**Warnings — call HF Clinic 555-0133 the same business day** (after hours: nurse line 555-0145):
- Your shoes or socks feel tighter than yesterday
- You need an extra pillow to breathe at night
- New dry cough
- You felt dizzy once when standing up
- You missed a dose and are not sure what to do
- Bruising you cannot explain (you take a blood thinner)

**Emergencies — call 911 right now:**
- You cannot catch your breath even sitting still
- Chest pain or pressure
- You fainted
- You are coughing pink foamy spit
- Severe bleeding that will not stop
- Fell and hit your head (you take a blood thinner)
- You are very shaky, sweaty, confused — and eating sugar does not fix it (low blood sugar on metformin)

**Teach-back prompt:**
*"Let's pretend it is Saturday night and something doesn't feel right. Give me two examples of things that would make you call the clinic, and two things that would make you call 911 instead. I want to see how we'd decide together."*

### 6. Fall prevention

**Key message:**
You are at higher risk of falling right now. You fell once last year. You use a cane. Your new water pill can make you feel light-headed — especially if you stand up fast.

**What to do:**
- Use your cane every time you walk — even at night to the bathroom.
- Sit up on the edge of the bed and count to 10 before you stand. Then stand and count to 10 before you walk. This gives your blood pressure time to catch up.
- Turn on a light at night. Use the grab bars in the bathroom (you already have them).
- Keep a clear path from bed to bathroom — no rugs, no cords.
- Take the water pill in the morning — not at bedtime — so you are not walking to the bathroom half-asleep.
- If you feel dizzy, sit down right away. Call the HF clinic **555-0133** if it happens more than once.

> *Picture cue:* pictogram of "sit → count → stand → count → walk."

**Teach-back prompt:**
*"Show me how you are going to get out of bed tomorrow morning. Talk me through it step by step. What does the cane do? What if you feel wobbly?"*
