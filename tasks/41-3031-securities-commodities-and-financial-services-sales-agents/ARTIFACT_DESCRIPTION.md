## Occupation
- **SOC:** 41-3031

## Scenario
You are a senior institutional equity salesperson on the Healthcare desk at Hartwell Petrie & Co., a mid-sized sell-side broker-dealer headquartered in New York with offices in Boston, San Francisco, and London. It is Tuesday, April 14, 2026, 7:15 AM ET, the morning after Cortevia Therapeutics (NASDAQ: CVTX) reported Q1-2026 results and updated guidance on their lead Phase III asset, CVX-417 (oncology, second-line non-small-cell lung cancer). Your 8:00 AM sales meeting reads out the desk's "Morning Idea Note" to roughly 40 buy-side accounts, including three key holders you personally cover — Meridian Grove Capital (long-only, $38B AUM, current 2.1% position in CVTX), Polaris Equity Partners (hedge fund, $4.2B, flat in CVTX), and Allenwright Asset Management (insurance general account, $112B, no position). The note must be clearly framed as a sales/research distribution artifact — not personalized investment advice — and must comply with Hartwell Petrie's Reg AC, Reg FD, and institutional-client-communications framework.

## Artifact specification
A distribution-ready institutional "Morning Idea Note" written in a research-sales voice — tight, persuasive, but compliant. Required sections, in this order:

1. **Headline and one-line thesis** (≤25 words), with ticker, price, target, and time horizon. Example target form: "CVTX (Buy, PT $94 vs. spot ~$78): catalyst pulled forward; own into readout."
2. **What's new / why now** — 3–5 bullets keyed to yesterday's print and the pulled-forward catalyst.
3. **Our desk's differentiated view** — where Hartwell Petrie's analyst (Ahuja) differs from Street consensus, with specific numbers (PoS, peak sales, price target). Should explain the *reason* for the delta (e.g., the Eliant Phase II miss as a read-through into CVX-417's benefit-risk profile).
4. **Trade construction / ways to express the view** — outright long; paired vs. ELB; options overlay (reference the observed April $80 call flow) — each with a one-sentence rationale and risk. Include approximate sizing guidance in shares or notional where helpful.
5. **Account-relevance callouts** — short, tailored sentences for Meridian Grove, Polaris, and Allenwright, grounded in each account's known context (without disclosing other clients' positions or orders in the note itself).
6. **Key risks and what would change our view** — CVX-417 failure scenario (implied price $48–$52 per Ahuja bear case), competitive readouts (a pending Phase II from Maridelle Pharma expected late May), guidance credibility (is the Q1 beat inventory-stocking or sustainable end-demand?).
7. **Catalysts calendar** — next 6 months, with dates or windows (ASCO abstract drop late May; Maridelle Phase II late May; PFS readout window late June–early July; Q2 print late July; R&D day August 14).
8. **Required disclosures** — Reg AC language, market-making, analyst ownership, IB relationship; a clear "not personalized advice; institutional audience only" framing placed so a reviewer can find it without scrolling.

Tone: professional, confident, sell-side-sales cadence (active voice, numbers up front), but compliant and non-advisory. No exclamation marks, no emojis, no retail-style pitch language. Use "we" for the desk view; attribute analyst numbers explicitly to Dr. Ahuja. Must read as a research-sales artifact intended for an institutional audience — not as a retail pitch and not as personalized advice.

## Output format
Markdown, rendered length ~1.5 pages / 700–1,000 words. One catalysts table and one "ways to play" table. Headline capped at one line. Disclosures labeled under a clear heading at the end (not buried as footnote microcopy).

## Iteration targets
1. **Sharpen the differentiated view.** v1 often reads as a recap of the print. Iterate so Section 3 makes the 58% vs. ~45% PoS delta — and *why* our desk gets there — the centerpiece, with the Eliant Phase II read-through made explicit (weaker competitor comparator data implies a larger addressable population if CVX-417 hits, and a lower bar on HR).
2. **Tailor the account callouts with real specificity.** v1 tends to write generic "long-only accounts should consider" lines. Better: reference Okafor's prior "de-risk" comment for Meridian Grove (and position that the catalyst pull-forward is itself the de-risking moment); Polaris's existing ELB short as a natural paired-trade extension; Allenwright's new specialty-pharma sleeve, committee cycle, and lower-beta initial sizing preference as fits for a scaled-in core entry rather than a catalyst trade.
3. **Make the trade-construction section actionable.** v1 lists "buy the stock." Iterate to at least three distinct expressions with sizing guidance and explicit risk for each:
   - (a) outright long with ~$78 entry, scale on pullback to $72; risk is a pre-readout drift lower.
   - (b) pair long CVTX vs. short ELB, beta-weighted; risk is ELB bid-to-cover / short-squeeze dynamics post-miss.
   - (c) April $80 call overlay referencing the observed 4,200-contract flow; risk is vol mean-reversion from 62% back toward 48%.
4. **Add genuine risks, not fig-leaf risks.** v1 may list "market risk." Replace with: PFS miss implied downside to $48–$52 per Ahuja bear case; guidance raise may reflect channel-stocking not end-demand (need to reconcile Relvintil TRx trend vs. revenue); Maridelle Pharma competitor Phase II late May may re-rate the category regardless of CVTX.
5. **Tighten disclosures without burying them.** v1 either over-disclaims in a wall of text or hides compliance at the bottom. Iterate to a clean, labeled disclosure block — Reg AC attribution, market-making disclosure, analyst ownership, IB relationship, institutional-audience framing — that a compliance officer would approve on first read.
6. **Get the catalysts calendar right.** v1 often lists vague "upcoming milestones." Iterate to dated or windowed events: ASCO abstract drop late May; Maridelle Phase II late May; PFS readout window late-June to early-July; Q2 print late July; R&D day August 14.

## Success criteria
(For evaluator use only.)
1. The note is framed unambiguously as a sales/research distribution artifact for institutional accounts — not personalized investment advice — and includes Reg AC-style attribution and market-making disclosure visible without hunting.
2. Section 3 makes a specific, quantified differentiated call (58% vs. ~45% PoS; $94 vs. Street average) and tells the reader *why* the desk view diverges, citing the Eliant Phase II miss read-through.
3. At least three distinct trade expressions are given (outright, pair vs. ELB, options overlay), each with a one-line risk. The options overlay references the observed April $80 call flow and the vol regime (62% vs. 48%).
4. Each of the three named accounts receives a callout grounded in that account's specific prior context (Okafor "de-risk" comment; Polaris ELB short; Allenwright specialty-pharma sleeve and sizing preference) without leaking other clients' positions or orders.
5. The risks section names at least one scenario that would invalidate the thesis, with an implied price level or magnitude (e.g., PFS miss → $48–$52).
6. The catalysts table has at least four dated or narrowly windowed events across the next 6 months, not vague milestones.

## Compliance quick-check for the writer
Before distribution, the note must satisfy Hartwell Petrie compliance's 10-point checklist:
1. Is the audience clearly marked as institutional?
2. Is there a clear non-advice framing?
3. Is there a Reg AC-style analyst attestation via Ahuja's published note (or reference to it)?
4. Is market-making disclosed?
5. Is analyst ownership disclosed?
6. Is IB-relationship disclosure present (none in last 12 months)?
7. Are price-target and probability numbers attributed to Ahuja, not presented as "our" numbers without attribution?
8. Are comparable trades anonymized and client positions not reproduced?
9. Are the specific client callouts generic enough to not disclose a position/order?
10. Is the "not personalized advice" language visible on first screen without scrolling?

Failing any one of these sends the note back for rewrite before it can go out — the writer should plan for this gate, not treat it as an afterthought.
