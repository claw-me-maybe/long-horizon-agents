# Account Plan: Brightshelf Market Holdings — FY26 Private-Label Coffee Expansion ($1.8M Opportunity)

## Occupation
- **SOC:** 41-4012
- **Title:** Sales Representatives, Wholesale and Manufacturing, Except Technical and Scientific Products

## Scenario
You are the Senior Account Executive for Midwest Grocery at Fernhollow Roasters, a mid-size specialty coffee roaster and private-label manufacturer ($112M revenue, based in Grand Rapids, MI). Brightshelf Market Holdings — a 38-store regional grocery chain across Michigan, Indiana, and Ohio — is expanding its private-label "Brightshelf Reserve" coffee program from 4 SKUs to 10 SKUs for the FY26 reset, with planogram lock on June 26, 2026. The incumbent supplier, Westbrook Coffee Co., holds 3 of the 4 current SKUs; Fernhollow holds 1 (a single-origin Ethiopia). Your VP Sales, Marta Benedict, expects a written account plan by Monday, April 27, 2026 to secure internal alignment across Ops, Marketing, and Finance before your April 30 buying meeting with Brightshelf's Category Manager Darnell Pike. Annual opportunity if we take 6 of the 10 SKUs: ~$1.8M at run-rate (year 1 ramped), with follow-on cold-brew concentrate add at ~$420K in FY27. Losing the entire program would put $340K of existing Ethiopia revenue at risk.

NPS from Pike's predecessor Sarah Chen was 9/10 on her exit interview (March 2025). No service-credit or chargeback incidents in the last 18 months.

## Artifact specification
A written Account Plan for internal distribution to the Fernhollow deal team (VP Sales, VP Ops, CMO, CFO) used to align on asks and commitments before the April 30 Brightshelf buying meeting. Required sections, in this order:

1. **Executive summary** — the $1.8M opportunity framed against FY26 quota; the specific SKU ask (6 of 10 SKUs, which ones, and why); the June 26 decision date; the walk-away position.
2. **Account snapshot** — Brightshelf business, category context, where private-label coffee sits for them, relevant macro tailwinds (e.g., private-label penetration gains).
3. **Stakeholder map** — at minimum Pike, Yoon, Shah, Teague; each with role, stance today (champion / neutral / skeptic / blocker), what they care about, and our next action with date and owner.
4. **Opportunity sizing** — SKU-by-SKU mix, unit forecasts, revenue and gross margin, with a base / upside / downside case that reconciles to the $1.8M base.
5. **Competitive displacement strategy** — why we can take SKUs from Westbrook, where we will not try (and why), and where we add whitespace (e.g., compostable pods).
6. **Commercial proposal framing** — the rebate tier structure we will lead with, MDF/slotting we will commit, and the margin floor we will not cross. Include a "walk-away" position and a "what we will not concede" list.
7. **Next-best-actions** — a dated 6–10 step plan through June 26 planogram lock, with owner and dependency for each step.
8. **Risks and mitigations** — at least four, grounded in this account (not generic).
9. **Internal asks** — specific commitments needed from Ops (freight capacity), CMO (MDF co-fund), CFO (tier approval and credit terms), with date-by-when for each.
10. **One-page summary** — a top-of-document summary suitable for a 10-minute internal standup.

Tone: professional, commercially confident, specific. Written for an internal audience that will hold you accountable for the numbers — so no puffery. Uses account language ("planogram," "reset," "MDF," "OTIF," "slotting," "category captain") correctly. Persuasive but grounded — this is an internal plan, so candor about risks and objections is valued over bravado.

## Output format
Markdown document, ~4–6 pages rendered. At least three tables (SKU/pricing, stakeholder map, next-best-actions). Include a one-page summary at the top suitable for screen-sharing in a 10-minute internal call.

## Iteration targets
1. **Turn the stakeholder map into a real map, not a list.** v1 typically lists four names with one sentence each. Iterate to include stance (champion/neutral/skeptic/blocker), decision weight (1–5 where 5 is blocker-or-approver), and the *specific* next touch for each (e.g., "send Shah a COGS sensitivity chart by 4/22 via Pike; Marta Benedict owns").
2. **Make the opportunity sizing defensible SKU-by-SKU.** v1 tends to present one $1.8M number. Better: a SKU-by-SKU table with unit velocity assumption, FOB price, extended revenue, applied margin, and a base / upside / downside that reconciles to $1.8M in the base. Name the velocity source.
3. **Ground the displacement plan in real levers.** v1 will write "better quality." Iterate to Westbrook's Brazil-sourcing risk tied to the 30% 2026 Minas Gerais shortfall, their 91.3% OTIF vs. our 97.4%, and the fair-trade-certification gap against the 134-index shopper data.
4. **Give the commercial proposal a defensible floor.** v1 quotes list price. Iterate to: lead offer, a step-down, and a documented margin floor below which we walk, with justification.  Reference the specific margin floors by product line (22%/26%/19%).
5. **Date the next-best-actions and sequence them.** v1 writes a bulleted list. Iterate to a table with date, action, owner, dependency, and what it unlocks — sequenced to the June 26 planogram lock. Minimum 8 actions.
6. **Cover the Yoon objection head-on.** Yoon values "margin-neutral innovation" and dislikes mid-program supplier change. v1 ignores this. Iterate to a specific tactic — e.g., phased SKU add-in, not displacement, in Districts 1–2 and displacement only in District 3 where category is underperforming by 9%.
7. **Leverage the Grand Rapids / Michigan-local angle for Teague.** v1 ignores the CEO. Iterate to include a specific touchpoint or talking point for Teague that invokes the regional-supplier preference and the co-op marketing opportunity around "Michigan Roaster" shelf call-outs.

## Success criteria
(For evaluator use only.)
1. The plan identifies Pike, Yoon, Shah, and Teague by role, names a champion hypothesis, and gives a dated next-touch for each.
2. SKU-level sizing reconciles to the $1.8M base case with visible unit/price/margin math; an upside and downside case are also present and internally consistent.
3. At least two of the three displacement arguments (Westbrook OTIF gap, Brazil sourcing risk, fair-trade certification gap) are present with specifics.
4. Commercial proposal includes both a lead offer and a walk-away margin floor; rebate tiers from the input are referenced correctly.
5. The next-best-actions plan is sequenced toward the June 26 planogram lock with at least 6 dated actions and named owners.
6. The plan proposes a specific tactic to manage Yoon's "no mid-program change" concern (e.g., add-in in strong districts, displace only in underperforming District 3).
7. The plan explicitly addresses Teague using the Grand Rapids / regional-supplier angle (not a generic "meet the CEO" line).

## Non-goals (explicit)
- This is an internal account plan, not the proposal document delivered to Brightshelf on April 30. A separate customer-facing deck is downstream and out of scope for this artifact.
- Not a pricing sheet. Pricing appears only where needed to demonstrate the commercial logic and margin floor.
- Not a product brief. Product specs appear only where they matter for the decision (e.g., cert status, MOQ implications for reset timing).
- Do not disclose Westbrook's observed FOB price ($5.55) or their 91.3% OTIF figure in any language that would go back to Brightshelf; these are for internal calibration only.
- Margin floors are confidential. Do not state them verbatim in language a Brightshelf stakeholder should see.
- Do not design the in-store marketing circular or the endcap creative. That is CMO scope and flows from, but is not part of, this account plan.
