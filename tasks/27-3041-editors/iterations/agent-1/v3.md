Dear Naledi,

The technique that carries this novel is one I want to name before anything else, because the revision depends on you knowing it is the engine. Across the 1960s sections you return to Abeni's hands — beading for her mother's shop in Ch. 1, cradling the kerosene lamp during the Ikoyi blackout in Ch. 3, knotting the wrapper around infant Folasade in Ch. 6 — and by the fourth return a reader has been given a full interior life through one body part. The salt lantern does the same work at scale: I expected a decorative title-drop and instead, at Ch. 9, p. 112, Abeni refuses to light it for her husband's guests, and the object becomes a moral instrument. Image accruing into ethics — that is the technique this book is built on, and nothing in your comps set is doing it at this register. Not Gyasi, who works in tableau. Not Evaristo, who works in voice. Not Nelson, who works in lyric compression. You are doing something older and slower and harder, and it is working.

The second thing worth naming before we turn to the structural notes: the novel you delivered has a sharper political intelligence than the one I acquired. Abeni's exchange with the British nurse in Ch. 7 — "she spoke to me the way one speaks to a lamp one has already decided is out of oil" — is the most exact rendering of colonial condescension I have read in fiction this year. Folasade's Peckham chapters, particularly the church-politics scene in Ch. 11 and the Ankara fabric argument in Ch. 14, carry a specificity about 1990s British-Nigerian class friction I had no right to expect from the submission. The notes that follow are all in service of letting that edge cut further; none of them are about softening it.

## Big-picture diagnosis

If you fix only one thing in this revision, fix the architecture of the Bristol thread. Everything else below is real, but it is secondary. I want to be plain about that priority so you don't spread the revision budget evenly and finish with four half-fixes.

### 1. The Bristol thread is underbuilt, not underwritten

**Diagnosis.** Temi's sections feel thin not because the prose is thin — the damp-patch passage at Ch. 17, p. 201 is the finest small-scale interior writing in the manuscript — but because Temi does not make enough *decisions that cost her*. Abeni makes a consequential choice in nearly every chapter she appears in. Folasade's 1990s arc is built around the slow decision of whether to return to Lagos for her father's funeral. Temi, across 2023, is receiving: the flat, the audio letters, the diagnosis, eventually the history. Inheritance is a theme; it is not an engine. The thread is currently running on theme alone, and theme cannot carry thirty thousand words.

**What might unlock it.** Three genuinely distinct directions. These are not the same fix in different outfits — A is a withholding move, B is a relational move, C is a pacing move — and the revision should commit to one.

*Option A — give Temi an active secret.* Something she is hiding from Marcus, from Folasade, or from the reader, whose consequences land inside the 2023 thread rather than after the novel ends. A secret generates decisions by force; it also gives Temi the double interiority (what she thinks, and what she refuses to think) that Abeni has and Temi currently lacks.

*Option B — build Temi and Aunty Fola into a sustained antagonism.* Make the 2023 thread structurally about a contest over what gets told, what gets kept, what gets inherited. This reframes the Aunty Fola question (addressed below) and gives Temi a scene partner who forces her to act.

*Option C — move the diagnosis to Ch. 15 or earlier.* Right now it arrives in Ch. 22 as a late complication. Earlier, it becomes the engine that gives urgency to every subsequent Temi scene. This is the most structurally invasive option and I would only go to it if A and B both fail to spark.

### 2. The braided structure is not yet braiding

**Diagnosis.** You are alternating POVs cleanly at the chapter level, but the three threads are not yet in conversation at the paragraph level. The salt lantern is the one object that crosses all three timelines and changes meaning in the crossing. Almost nothing else does. A reader finishes an Abeni chapter and enters a Temi chapter carrying nothing across the gap except general thematic mood, and for a braided novel that is the gap where the book either earns its structure or resolves into three novellas sharing a binding.

**What might unlock it.** Three genuine alternatives.

*Option A — seed three additional cross-thread objects.* The Ankara wrapper from Ch. 2, the audio cassette *hardware* (not its content) from Ch. 19, the word "ìgbàgbé" (forgetting) that Abeni uses in Ch. 4 — any of these could receive the salt lantern's treatment and recur across generations with shifting valence. Image-level braid.

*Option B — use structural rhyme rather than object rhyme.* Have Folasade in Ch. 12 and Temi in Ch. 20 make structurally parallel decisions — say, whether to answer a call from Lagos — without the novel drawing attention to the parallel. The braid becomes formal rather than imagistic. Colder, more modernist.

*Option C — let one character read another's chapter.* Not literally, but have Temi in Ch. 19 discover a document — a letter, a diary page — whose content the reader has already lived through in an earlier Abeni chapter. The reader feels the gap between what the document says and what happened. Hardest to execute, strongest braid effect when it works.

### 3. The third-act reversal happens off-page

**Diagnosis.** Ch. 24 is where the novel's deepest secret — what happened to Abeni's sister Adaeze in 1967 — finally surfaces, and you deliver it in roughly 400 words of exposition from Aunty Fola. The whole novel has been coiling around this moment for twenty-three chapters, and its weight is load-bearing. As currently written, the revelation feels like a plot event. It should feel like a break in the weather.

**What might unlock it.** Two directions, different in where they place the dramatic weight.

*Option A — dramatize Adaeze's story in 1967.* Write it as a new Abeni chapter placed just before Ch. 24, so the reader learns it in the body, and Aunty Fola's present-day telling becomes confirmation of something the reader already knows but Temi does not. Dramatic irony carries the scene.

*Option B — dramatize the *telling*.* Keep the revelation in the present, but rebuild Ch. 24 as a full scene — Aunty Fola resisting, Temi pressing, the physical setting (a drive to Heathrow, a kitchen at 2 a.m., take your pick) doing thematic work. Not a monologue but a prying-open.

Option A protects chronology and is structurally safer. Option B keeps the weight of discovery on Temi, where the 2023 thread needs it most. These favor different versions of the novel you are writing.

### 4. The ending ends, rather than concludes

**Diagnosis.** Ch. 27 gives us a beautiful final image — Temi in the flat, the light through the window, the lantern on the sill — but the image is not earned by a reckoning in the preceding twenty pages. A reader closes the book holding the prose but not the shape. The issue is causal: the final chapters need Temi to do something that could only have happened because of everything she has now learned, and what she currently does is contemplate.

**What might unlock it.** *Option A:* have Temi in Ch. 26 make a decision that structurally reverses a decision Folasade made in the 1990s thread. Action-level braid, generations in dialogue. *Option B:* end with Temi writing rather than receiving — a letter, a message, a record that will be inherited in turn. Closes the inheritance arc by inverting it. *Option C:* let the ending be partial — close on an unresolved action rather than a resolved image. These are three different endings for the same book; you will know which one is yours.

## Thread-by-thread notes

### Temi / 2023 Bristol — the thread carrying the revision

I am giving this section more space than the other two because Lagos is working and Bristol is not, and an edit letter that allocated space evenly across threads would flatter the book you wrote rather than help the book you are revising.

**What is working.**

- The flat as character. The damp patch behind the wardrobe (Ch. 17, p. 201) that Temi keeps meaning to mention to the landlord is a perfect objective correlative for everything she is postponing. This is the mode the Bristol thread should be in more often.
- The texture of her working life. The specificity about the MyHR system, the Monday 9 a.m. team huddle, the particular fluorescent loneliness of NHS middle-administration — none of your comps do this. It is a competitive advantage, not a weakness; the impulse to smooth it toward universality is the wrong impulse.
- The argument with Marcus in Ch. 21 about whether they can afford a child. The dialogue has friction. The chapter ends on the right beat. Do not touch it.
- The audio-letters discovery (Ch. 19, p. 224) is genuinely moving, though overbuilt — see below.

**What is not yet.**

- Temi's interiority is consistently one layer thinner than Abeni's and Folasade's. When Abeni thinks, we get what she thinks *and* what she is refusing to think — the double interiority that makes her chapters rereadable. With Temi we mostly get the first layer only. Ch. 15, p. 178 (her reaction to the diagnosis) is the clearest case: the feelings are rendered but not refracted. What is Temi refusing to admit she feels? That missing register is what makes the thread feel thin.
- The voice slips toward literary-contemporary default in the prose itself. "She felt unmoored" (Ch. 20, p. 238) could appear in any London novel from the last decade. Your Abeni prose is never interchangeable with any other Nigerian novelist; your Temi prose sometimes is. Push the register until it cannot be substituted.
- The thread ends rather than concludes — see big-picture item 4.
- Ch. 25 is, I suspect, skippable. Try cutting it entirely and see whether anything fails. It currently reads like scaffolding left in a finished building.

**Chapter by chapter.**

You are asking a single scene to be a revelation, a grief moment, a bridge to the 1990s thread, *and* an establishing scene for Temi's relationship with her mother's voice. Split it. Keep the discovery in Ch. 19; move the listening itself to its own chapter — a new Ch. 19.5, or a rebuilt Ch. 25 — where it can breathe.

- Ch. 25: cut.

## Targeted questions

Sit with these before the revision. They are to be answered on the page, not in the letter back to me.

1. What does Temi want that she would be ashamed to name aloud to Folasade? Not "what does Temi want" — the shame version is the one that matters.
2. What does Aunty Fola know that she will never, under any circumstances, tell Temi? What is she protecting by not telling — Temi, herself, Abeni, Adaeze, or something else?
3. If Abeni and Temi met as adults (they cannot; pretend), what would Abeni actually think of her? Not the sentimental answer. The real one.
4. Where does Folasade lie to herself? There should be at least three places in the manuscript. If you can only find one, the thread is being too gentle with her.
5. The salt lantern means three different things across three generations. Name the three, in one sentence each.
6. What happens to Temi in 2024, the year after the novel ends? You do not have to write it, but you have to know it. Your ending will land differently depending on what you believe.
7. Is the novel's politics primarily about colonialism, class, or gender? It cannot be equally about all three and still hit hard. Choose a primary, a secondary, an atmosphere.
8. What would make this a worse version of itself? The answer tends to surface what a book is secretly afraid of.
9. If Temi's chapters stood alone as a novella, what would the plot be? If the honest answer is "not much," that is the diagnosis for big-picture item 1.
10. What is the book's relationship to hope? I cannot yet tell whether The Salt Lantern is hopeful or bleak, and you need to know before revision, because the endings of all three threads resolve differently depending on the answer.

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her. Change her job.

She currently does two things. In the Lagos sections she supplies warmth and Yoruba-idiom texture, which is working. In the Bristol sections she is an exposition-delivery mechanism, and that is the structural weakness dragging down the 2023 thread. The temptation will be to cut her to solve the Bristol problem. Don't. Cut her function, not her presence.

Recast her as the person Temi has to *get* things from rather than the person who tells Temi things. If Aunty Fola knows what happened to Adaeze and is choosing, for her own reasons — shame, grief, promise-keeping, self-protection — not to say, she becomes a character with agency in the present tense rather than a conduit for backstory. The 2023 thread then carries two antagonisms instead of one (Temi versus the thing she is postponing; Temi versus Aunty Fola's silence), which is exactly the density the Bristol sections currently lack.

The test I set myself while reading: can you write a scene in which Aunty Fola lies to Temi? Not a grand betrayal. A small, plausible, motivated lie. If you can — and I think you can; the woman who cooks jollof for the grieving neighbor in Ch. 18 has the interiority for it — she stays and the novel becomes structurally sounder. If the scene genuinely will not come, she is a device rather than a character, and devices belong in revision's cutting pile.

My strong intuition: you can write that scene.

Budget, roughly: cut a third of her current scenes, rebuild the remaining two-thirds around withholding rather than telling. The Ch. 24 revelation becomes the climactic point of her arc rather than an expository beat in Temi's.
