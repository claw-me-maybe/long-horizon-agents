Dear Naledi,

I've now read The Salt Lantern twice, and the opening pages in 1960s Lagos are doing something I haven't seen a debut do in years. Abeni's hands — the way you return to them across Chapters 1, 3, and 6, first threading beads for her mother's shop, then cradling the kerosene lamp during the blackout, then knotting the wrapper around the baby who will become Folasade — give us a whole interior life through a single body part. That's the kind of control most novelists don't find until book three. The salt lantern image itself, which I expected to work as a one-time title-drop, earns its recurrence in Ch. 9, p. 112 when Abeni refuses to light it for her husband's guests; it becomes a moral object, not a decorative one.

The second thing I want to name before we get into structural work: the novel you've written is angrier than the novel we bought, and that's good. The acquisitions memo pitched this as Homegoing meets Small Island; what you've delivered has a sharper political intelligence than either. Abeni's conversation with the British nurse in Ch. 7 — especially the line "she spoke to me the way one speaks to a lamp one has already decided is out of oil" — is the clearest articulation of colonial condescension I've read in fiction this year. Hold that edge in the revision. Don't let anyone (including me) sand it down.

## Big-picture diagnosis

### The Bristol thread is underbuilt, not underwritten

Temi's sections are doing real work — the scene in Ch. 19 where she finds Folasade's old audio letters to Abeni is genuinely moving — but the thread feels shorter not because it has fewer pages (it does, by about 30%) but because it has fewer *decisions*. Abeni makes a choice with consequences in nearly every chapter she appears in. Folasade's 1990s thread is built around the slow decision about whether to go back to Lagos for her father's funeral. Temi, across 2023, is mostly *receiving* — inheriting the flat, inheriting the letters, inheriting the diagnosis. She needs at least two decisions that cost her something, and one of them probably needs to be a decision she later regrets.

### The braided structure isn't braiding

You're alternating POVs cleanly, but the three threads don't yet speak to each other at the chapter-to-chapter level. An object, a phrase, or a gesture introduced in a Lagos chapter should be doing something different by the time it reaches Bristol. The salt lantern itself mostly does this; almost nothing else does. Consider where a reader, having just read a Folasade chapter, opens the next Temi chapter — what should they be carrying across the gap?

### The third-act reversal happens off-page

I want to flag Ch. 24 specifically. The revelation about Abeni's sister — which the whole novel has been coiling around — is delivered in a single paragraph of exposition from Aunty Fola. For the load this moment bears, we need to be in a scene.

## Thread-by-thread notes

### Temi / 2023 Bristol

Working:
- The flat itself as a character. The description of the damp patch behind the wardrobe in Ch. 17, p. 201, which Temi keeps meaning to tell the landlord about, is one of the best small-scale interior-life moves in the book.
- The texture of her work life — the NHS administrative job, the specific grinding quality of the MyHR system — is rendered with an authority the other contemporary novels on our comps list don't have.
- Her relationship with Marcus, particularly the argument in Ch. 21 about whether they can afford to have a child, reads true.

Not yet working:
- Temi's interiority is consistently one layer thinner than Abeni's or Folasade's. When Abeni thinks, we get what she thinks *and* what she is refusing to think. With Temi, we mostly get the first. Ch. 15, p. 178 is the clearest example: her reaction to the diagnosis is rendered but not refracted.
- The voice tips toward literary-contemporary default in places where it should be specifically Temi. "She felt unmoored" (Ch. 20, p. 238) could be any thirtysomething protagonist in any London novel from the last decade.
- The thread ends, rather than concluding. Ch. 27 gives us an image but not a reckoning.

Chapter-specific flags:
- Ch. 13: opens too close to the previous Folasade chapter tonally; needs a harder tonal shift.
- Ch. 17: the damp patch scene is gold. Build out from it.
- Ch. 19: the audio letters scene is doing too much work in too little space.
- Ch. 25: I think this chapter is skippable. Test cutting it and see if anything breaks.

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her. But cut her scenes by roughly a third, and give her a reason to be in the present-day thread that isn't expository.

Right now Aunty Fola is doing two jobs: she's a source of warmth and Yoruba-idiom texture in the Lagos sections, which is working, and she's a delivery mechanism for family history in the Bristol sections, which is not. The problem isn't her presence — it's that you've cast her as the person who tells Temi things. Make her the person Temi has to *get* things from. If Aunty Fola knows what happened to Abeni's sister and is choosing, for her own reasons, not to say, she becomes a character with agency in the present tense. Right now she's a conduit.

The test: can you write a scene where Aunty Fola lies to Temi? If yes, she stays and the novel gets better. If no, she's a device, and devices should be cut.
