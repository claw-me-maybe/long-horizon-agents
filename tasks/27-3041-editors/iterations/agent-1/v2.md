Dear Naledi,

I've read The Salt Lantern twice now, and the thing I want to name first is what Abeni's hands are doing across the 1960s sections. You return to them in Ch. 1, Ch. 3, and Ch. 6 — beading for her mother's shop, cradling the kerosene lamp during the blackout, knotting the wrapper around the infant Folasade — and by the fourth return a reader feels she has been given a whole interior life through one body part. The control required to do that without it becoming a motif-for-motif's-sake is the kind of control most novelists don't find until their third book. The salt lantern itself earns its recurrence the same way: I expected a decorative title-drop, and instead at Ch. 9, p. 112 Abeni refuses to light it for her husband's guests and it becomes a moral object. Objects that start as image and accrue into ethics — that's the technique this book is built on, and nobody else in your comps set (not Gyasi, not Evaristo, certainly not Nelson) is doing it at this scale.

The other thing I want to say before we go into structural work: the novel you delivered is angrier and more politically literate than the one I bought. Abeni's exchange with the British nurse in Ch. 7 — "she spoke to me the way one speaks to a lamp one has already decided is out of oil" — is the sharpest rendering of colonial condescension I've read in fiction this year, and Folasade's Peckham chapters have a specificity about 1990s British-Nigerian class friction (the church-politics scene in Ch. 11, the argument about the Ankara fabric in Ch. 14) that I didn't know to expect from the submission. Hold that edge. The revision notes below are all in service of letting that voice reach further, not softening it.

## Big-picture diagnosis

If you only fix one thing in this revision, fix the Bristol thread's architecture. Everything else below is secondary to that.

### 1. The Bristol thread is underbuilt, not underwritten

**Diagnosis.** Temi's sections feel thin not because the prose is thin — it isn't, the damp-patch-behind-the-wardrobe passage in Ch. 17, p. 201 is some of the best small-scale interior writing in the manuscript — but because Temi doesn't make enough *decisions that cost her*. Abeni makes a consequential choice in nearly every chapter she appears in. Folasade's whole 1990s arc is the slow accretion of a decision about whether to return to Lagos. Temi, by contrast, spends 2023 inheriting: the flat, the audio letters, the diagnosis, eventually the history. Inheritance is a theme, not a plot engine, and the thread currently runs on the theme alone.

**What might unlock it.** Three genuinely different directions, not three versions of the same fix:

*Option A — give Temi an active secret.* Something she is hiding from Marcus, or from Folasade, or from the reader, that is answered in the novel's present tense. A secret generates decisions by force. (Precedent: the way Nelson uses the protagonist's withholding in Open Water, but load-bearing.)

*Option B — collapse Temi and Aunty Fola into an antagonism.* Make the 2023 thread fundamentally about a conflict between them — about what gets told, what gets kept, what gets inherited. This reframes Aunty Fola (see below) and gives Temi a scene partner who forces her to act rather than receive.

*Option C — push the diagnosis earlier and make it the engine.* Right now the diagnosis in Ch. 22 functions as a late complication. If it lands in Ch. 15 or earlier, Temi's subsequent choices (about Marcus, about the flat, about whether to go to Lagos) acquire the urgency the thread currently lacks. This is the most structurally invasive option and I'd only recommend it if A and B don't spark.

These are not the same fix in three outfits. A is a withholding move, B is a relational move, C is a pacing move. Pick one and commit.

### 2. The braided structure isn't yet braiding

**Diagnosis.** You're alternating POVs cleanly at the chapter level, but the three threads aren't yet in conversation at the paragraph level. The salt lantern is the one object that crosses all three timelines and changes meaning; almost nothing else does. Right now a reader finishes an Abeni chapter and enters a Temi chapter carrying nothing across the gap except general thematic mood. For a braided novel, that's the gap where the book either earns its structure or just looks like three novellas in trenchcoat.

**What might unlock it.** Three alternatives:

*Option A — seed three more cross-thread objects.* The Ankara wrapper from Ch. 2, the audio cassette hardware (not the content) from Ch. 19, the specific word "ìgbàgbé" (forgetting) that Abeni uses in Ch. 4 — any of these could be given the salt lantern's treatment and recur across generations with shifting valence.

*Option B — use structural rhyme rather than object-rhyme.* Have Folasade in Ch. 12 and Temi in Ch. 20 make structurally parallel decisions (say, whether to answer a phone call from Lagos) without drawing attention to the parallel. The braid becomes formal, not imagistic.

*Option C — let one character read another's chapter.* Not literally — but let Temi, in Ch. 19, discover a document (a letter, a diary page) whose content the reader has already lived through in an earlier Abeni chapter. The reader feels the gap between what the document says and what actually happened. This is harder to execute but produces the strongest braid effect.

### 3. The third-act reversal happens off-page

**Diagnosis.** Ch. 24 is where the novel's deepest secret — what happened to Abeni's sister Adaeze in 1967 — finally surfaces. You currently deliver it in a paragraph of exposition from Aunty Fola across roughly 400 words. The whole novel has been coiling around this moment, and it needs a scene to bear its weight. As written, the revelation feels like a plot event; it should feel like a break in the weather.

**What might unlock it.** Two directions:

*Option A — dramatize it in 1967.* Write the Adaeze scene as a new Abeni chapter, placed just before Ch. 24, so the reader learns it in the body, and then Aunty Fola's telling becomes a confirmation of something the reader already knows but Temi doesn't. The dramatic irony does the work.

*Option B — dramatize the *telling*.* Keep the revelation in the present, but rebuild Ch. 24 as a full scene — Aunty Fola resisting, Temi pressing, the physical setting (the kitchen? the drive to Heathrow?) doing thematic work. Not a monologue but a prying-open.

Option A is safer and preserves chronology; Option B is riskier but keeps the weight of discovery on Temi, where the 2023 thread needs it.

### 4. The ending ends, rather than concludes

**Diagnosis.** Ch. 27 gives us a beautiful final image — Temi in the flat, the light through the window, the lantern on the sill — but the image isn't earned by a reckoning in the preceding twenty pages. A reader closes the book feeling the prose but not the shape. The issue is one of *causality*: the novel's final chapters need Temi to do something that could only have happened because of everything she has now learned, and what she does currently is contemplate.

**What might unlock it.** *Option A:* have Temi make a decision in Ch. 26 that reverses a decision Folasade made in the 1990s thread, so the generations are in dialogue at the level of action. *Option B:* end with Temi writing rather than receiving — a letter, a message, a record that will be inherited in turn. *Option C:* let the ending be partial. End on an unresolved action rather than an image. I don't have a strong preference between these; they're genuinely different endings for the same novel and you'll know which one is yours.

## Thread-by-thread notes

### Temi / 2023 Bristol — the thread that most needs you

I'm giving this section more space than the other two because Lagos is working and Bristol isn't, and an edit letter that treats all three threads evenly would be flattering the book you wrote rather than helping the book you're revising.

**What's working.**

- The flat as character. The damp-patch passage (Ch. 17, p. 201) that Temi keeps meaning to mention to the landlord is a perfect small-scale objective correlative for everything she is postponing. This is the mode the Bristol thread should be in more often.
- The texture of her work life. The specificity about the MyHR system, the Monday 9 a.m. team huddle, the particular fluorescent loneliness of NHS middle-administration — none of your comps do this. This is a competitive advantage you should lean into, not edit out.
- The argument with Marcus in Ch. 21 about whether they can afford a child. The dialogue has real friction; the chapter ends on exactly the right beat.
- The audio-letters discovery (Ch. 19, p. 224) is genuinely moving, though overbuilt — see below.

**What isn't yet.**

- Temi's interiority is consistently one layer thinner than her mother's and grandmother's. When Abeni thinks, we get what she thinks *and* what she is refusing to think — the double interiority that makes her chapters rereadable. With Temi we mostly get the first layer. Ch. 15, p. 178 (her reaction to the diagnosis) is the clearest case: the feelings are rendered but not refracted. What is Temi refusing to admit she feels? That's the missing register.
- The voice slips toward literary-contemporary default in the prose itself. "She felt unmoored" (Ch. 20, p. 238) could appear in any London novel from the last decade. This isn't a Temi sentence. Your Abeni prose is never interchangeable with any other Nigerian novelist; your Temi prose sometimes is. Push until it can't be.
- The thread ends rather than concludes. Ch. 27 gives an image but not a reckoning — see big-picture item 4.
- Ch. 25 is, I suspect, skippable. Try cutting it entirely and see what, if anything, fails. Right now it reads like scaffolding left in the finished building.

**Chapter-by-chapter.**

- Ch. 13: opens too close tonally to the Folasade chapter that precedes it. A harder tonal shift at the transition would let the reader feel the time jump in the body rather than in the date header.
- Ch. 17: the damp-patch passage is gold. More of this mode, please — physical detail that carries psychological freight without being told to.
- Ch. 19: the audio-letters discovery is doing too much work in too little space. You're asking the scene to be a revelation, a grief moment, a bridge to the 1990s thread, *and* an establishing scene for Temi's relationship with her mother's voice. Split it. Keep the discovery in Ch. 19, move the listening itself to its own chapter (new Ch. 19.5 or a replacement for Ch. 25) where it can breathe.
- Ch. 21: the argument with Marcus ends right. Don't touch it.
- Ch. 25: consider cutting.
- Ch. 27: see ending note above.

### Folasade / 1990s Peckham — the thread that's working and could be doing even more

**What's working.** The church-politics scene in Ch. 11, the Ankara fabric argument in Ch. 14, and Folasade's long internal monologue on the 436 bus in Ch. 16 are all exact and specific and nothing like anything in the comps. The 1990s texture — the phone cards, the VHS tapes of Nollywood films, the particular cadence of Nigerian-Pentecostal English as spoken in a Peckham front room — is rendered with authority.

**What isn't.** Folasade's relationship with her own ambition is under-examined. She gives things up across these chapters, but the novel is gentler with her sacrifices than it is with Abeni's, and I think Folasade can bear more scrutiny. Ch. 16 flirts with it and then pulls back. I'd let it not pull back.

**Chapter flags.** Ch. 10's opening is a beat slow; Ch. 12 could absorb material if you move Temi's diagnosis earlier (see big-picture item 1, Option C); Ch. 16 is the chapter I'd push hardest on in revision.

### Abeni / 1960s Lagos — the thread that's load-bearing and nearly finished

**What's working.** Everything structural. The Abeni chapters are the spine of this novel and the reason it sold. Keep the braided object technique (the hands, the lantern, the wrapper) and trust it.

**What isn't.** Two small things, not structural. First, Ch. 5 has a point-of-view wobble on p. 61 where we briefly hear Abeni's husband's interiority; I think this is accidental. Second, the political material in Ch. 8 around the 1967 eve-of-war atmosphere is compressed to the point of shorthand — a reader who doesn't know the history will miss what's at stake. Expand by half a page; no more.

**Chapter flags.** Ch. 5, p. 61 POV wobble. Ch. 8 political compression. Otherwise this thread is line-edit territory, not developmental.

## Targeted questions

These are questions to sit with before the revision — not to answer in the letter back to me, but to answer on the page.

1. What does Temi want that she would be ashamed to name aloud to Folasade? (Not "what does Temi want" — the shame version.)
2. What is the one thing Aunty Fola knows that she will never, under any circumstances, tell Temi, even if pressed? What is she protecting by not telling?
3. What would Abeni think of Temi if they met as adults? Not romantic-reconciliation-answer: the actual answer.
4. Where in the manuscript does Folasade lie to herself? (There should be at least three places. If you can only find one, the thread is too gentle with her.)
5. The salt lantern means three different things across three generations. What are the three things, in one sentence each?
6. What happens in 2024 — the year after the novel ends — to Temi? You don't have to write it, but you have to know it. The ending will land differently depending on what you believe.
7. Is the novel's politics about colonialism, about class, or about gender? It can't be equally about all three and still hit hard. Which is primary, which is secondary, which is atmosphere?
8. What would make this book a worse version of itself? (The answer here often reveals what the book is secretly afraid of.)
9. If Temi's chapters were a standalone novella, what would it be about — not thematically, but in terms of plot? If the answer is "not much," that's the diagnostic.
10. What is the book's relationship to hope? I can't yet tell whether The Salt Lantern is a hopeful book or a bleak one, and I think you need to know before the revision, because the revision choices at the endings of all three threads depend on that answer.

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her. But change her job.

Right now she has two functions: warmth-and-Yoruba-texture in the Lagos sections, which is working, and exposition-delivery in the Bristol sections, which is the structural weakness dragging down the 2023 thread. The temptation will be to cut her to solve the Bristol problem. Don't. Cut her function, not her presence.

Recast Aunty Fola as the person Temi has to *get things from* rather than the person who tells Temi things. If she knows what happened to Adaeze and is choosing, for her own reasons — shame, grief, promise-keeping, self-protection — not to say, she becomes a character with agency in the present tense rather than a conduit. The novel then has two antagonisms in the 2023 thread (Temi vs. the thing she is postponing; Temi vs. Aunty Fola's silence), which is exactly the density the Bristol sections currently lack.

The test I gave myself while reading: can you write a scene in which Aunty Fola lies to Temi? Not a grand betrayal — a small, plausible, motivated lie. If you can (and I think you can — the woman who cooks jollof for the grieving neighbor in Ch. 18 has the interiority for it), she stays and the novel gets structurally better. If you can't write that scene, she's a device and she should go. My strong intuition is you can.

Budget roughly: cut about a third of her current scenes, rebuild the remaining two-thirds around withholding rather than telling.
