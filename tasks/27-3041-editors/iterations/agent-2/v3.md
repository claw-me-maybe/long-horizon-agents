Dear Naledi,

Second read finished last night, and before anything else I want to name the two scenes in which your book does something I have not seen another novel do in quite this way. The first is the refused coin in Ch. 2 (p. 19-23): Abeni, orphaned that week, offers a kobo for salt, and the seller tells her "a girl with no mother buys nothing on a Tuesday." That single sentence teaches the reader your book's cosmology — a Yoruba grief-economy in which days of the week have weight and commerce is moral — without a paragraph of exposition, without an elder-character explainer, without the anthropological register that sinks so many diasporic debuts. You trusted the refusal to carry the world. It does.

The second is what that refusal becomes thirty years later, in Ch. 11 (p. 142), when Folasade counts fifty-pence pieces into the electricity meter in a Peckham hallway and the prose registers — quietly, without italics, without a flashback — that she is her mother's daughter handling her own refused coin, her own denominated grief, in a different empire's currency. The Lagos market and the Peckham meter are not two scenes that rhyme; they are one gesture performed twice, and because you staged it that way the braided structure stops being organizational and becomes argumentative. Your book's claim is that money remembers what bodies forget, and the form of the book already knows this. That is a rare thing in a first novel, and it is most of the reason we bought it.

What the revision needs to do is pull the third strand — Temi's 2023 Bristol — up to the level those two scenes set, and, more urgently, convert your thematic braid into a mechanical one so the generations don't only rhyme; they bind.

## Big-picture diagnosis

**If you only fix one thing, fix #2.** The Bristol thinness (#1) is the more visible weakness and the one marketing will ask about, but causal connective tissue (#2) is what prevents the final movement from landing, and it's the note I would be most embarrassed not to have given you.

### 1. Bristol does not yet carry third-wall weight

Temi has 21% of the draft's word count against Abeni's 43% and Folasade's 36%, and the deficit is not only proportional — it is textural. Temi's chapters summarize where the historical threads dramatize. The PhD, the breakup with Seun, the father's silence: these are told. Abeni's wedding night and Folasade's geriatric-ward shift are shown. The consequence is that the book's contemporary claim — that a granddaughter who can google her own grandmother is not therefore free of her — arrives as assertion rather than enactment. The contemporary reader, whose relation to inheritance is the whole political point of the novel, is the reader the novel is not currently earning.

**What might unlock it.** Three genuinely distinct routes, not three framings of one fix:

- *Conservative: expand Temi in place.* Give her two additional chapters in Part III (she has three; the historical threads have five each) and rewrite Ch. 14 and Ch. 18 as scenic rather than reflective — the hung-up phone call and the archive discovery are both currently paraphrase of emotion where they need to be event. Preserves your current architecture; minimizes risk; probably insufficient on its own.
- *Middle: re-weight the braid.* Cut Ch. 7 and Ch. 15 from the historical threads (both reprise rather than advance — see thread notes) and redistribute that ~7k words to Temi. Keeps the book at 92k; shifts the center of gravity toward the present; requires that Temi's new material be scenic and causally load-bearing, not atmospheric.
- *Radical: let Temi frame the novel.* Open and close in Temi's first person, with the 1960s and 1990s sections presented as her reconstruction from archives and her mother's testimony. This is the Azumah Nelson-shaped route. It solves the Bristol problem by making Bristol the book's situation rather than one of its three settings; it also risks losing the equal-weight pleasures of pure third-person immersion in Abeni's world. I would not commit to this without a call first.

### 2. The threads rhyme but do not cause

The braid is doing metaphor-work but not machine-work. Abeni's 1967 choices — the marriage to Tunde, the severing of contact with her sister Ronke, the migration in 1971 — do not visibly determine Folasade's specific 1994 losses, which in turn do not visibly determine Temi's specific 2023 paralysis. A careful reader finishing the novel can say "these women inherit each other's grief"; she cannot say "because Abeni did X in 1967, Folasade in 1994 had no alternative but Y, and Temi in 2023 cannot do Z until she undoes both." The political claim of the book — that diasporic inheritance is structural, not sentimental — requires that structure, and the structure is mostly absent.

**What might unlock it.** Three genuinely alternative moves:

- *Object route.* Let the salt lantern be a physical thing the reader tracks — broken, repaired, hidden, sold, returned — rather than a recurring image. Where is it in 2023? Who holds it? Can it still be lit? An object that survives three generations can carry causal weight a motif cannot.
- *Sororal-shadow route.* Bring Ronke forward. She is Abeni's severed sister and almost absent from the draft. Let her descendants appear in present-day Lagos and have Temi find a cousin through her research. The causal spine becomes: Abeni cut Ronke off, and because of that Folasade grew up without an auntie, and because of that Temi is reconstructing from archives what ought to have been given to her at a kitchen table. This is the Homegoing-shaped route in a more intimate two-branch form.
- *Withheld-information route.* There is a specific thing Abeni never told Folasade. Folasade therefore cannot tell Temi. The Ch. 25 letters are the explicit rupture of that silence — not a discovery of continuity but the breaking of a chain. This preserves your current plot bones and reframes your reversal; it is the cheapest option and probably the most dramatically potent.

### 3. The Ch. 25 reversal is a revelation, not a reversal

The tin of letters reframes what the reader knows but does not force any character to act. Temi reads, is moved, the novel ends. A third-act reversal should cost the viewpoint character a decision — something refused, something chosen, something that could not be chosen before the revelation. Right now the book resolves at the level of the reader's understanding, not the characters'.

**What might unlock it.**

- *Force a choice.* After Temi reads the letters, make her choose between two incompatible fidelities: her mother's version of the family story or her grandmother's. Publish or don't. Call or don't. The novel ends on the act, not the reading.
- *Move the discovery earlier.* Shift the letters to the end of Part III so the final movement becomes the consequences of knowing, not the fact of it. Structurally small; may be sufficient.
- *Withhold a letter.* Temi finds the tin and discovers, by the end, that one letter was destroyed. The revelation is also a loss; the reversal is her acceptance that the record she wanted will not be available. Formally the most interesting; also the riskiest, because it depends on the reader trusting that the absent letter is not authorial evasion.

## Thread-by-thread notes

### Temi / 2023 Bristol — most work needed

**What's working.** Ch. 6 (p. 74-78), Temi in the archive, is the book's best contemporary writing. She photographs microfiche with her phone because the scanner is broken; she registers the small shame of knowing her supervisor would disapprove; she admits — to herself, not to the reader, which is the right kind of admission — that she is researching her grandmother partly to avoid her mother. Scholarship as evasion is the spine of this character, and Ch. 6 trusts it. The other moment I kept returning to is Ch. 16 (p. 211), where Temi catches herself pronouncing "Abeni" her mother's way rather than her supervisor's, and chooses the supervisor's pronunciation for the remainder of the meeting. That is a sentence that contains a thesis. Do not touch it.

**What's not working.**

- *Interiority flattens after Ch. 6.* From Ch. 10 the close-third defaults to naming Temi's feelings rather than staging the gap between what she feels and what she will admit. The call-and-hang-up in Ch. 14 (p. 183) is a structurally beautiful beat that the surrounding prose then explains away — "she wasn't ready" where the texture of un-readiness should be. Trust the gesture; cut the gloss. This is a pattern note, not a single-line note; it applies to Ch. 14, Ch. 19, Ch. 22, and the first half of Ch. 23.
- *Marcus is not earning his space.* He appears in Ch. 9, Ch. 16, and Ch. 22, and in each scene he is a sounding board. He should want something from Temi that complicates her research — not necessarily romantic; perhaps that she stop writing, that she write differently, that she name what she is using him to stabilize. If no want can be found for him, cut him. That space is better spent on Seun, who is currently too thin for the weight the Ch. 20 breakup is asked to carry.
- *Bristol itself is under-rendered.* Lagos has heat, salt, specific birds; Peckham has specific buses, specific fishmongers, a specific cold. Bristol in 2023 is mostly interiors — Temi's flat, the archive, one pub — and reads as generic British-academic. This is a thematic failure, not a sensory one. Bristol's slave-trade history is latent in your book and never lands on the page. Temi walking past the Colston plinth or the negative space where it stood, without the narration registering it, is a missed structural beat given that your book is about what a coin remembers. The city needs to do work that only this city can do.
- *The Ch. 20 breakup arrives too late and costs too little.* By Ch. 20 the reader has accepted Temi as alone; the breakup reads as backfill rather than event. Move it to Part II so that her isolation is earned by event rather than stipulated, or cut it and let her loneliness be pre-existing.

Underneath all four of these notes is one diagnosis: Temi's thread is written from the posture of a character looking back at her own life, and the other two threads are written from the posture of characters inside theirs. Collapsing that narrative distance — even in a close-third — is the single largest craft move available to the revision.

### Folasade / 1994 Peckham — nearly there

**What's working.** Folasade is the most fully realized character in the book. The Ch. 11-12 sequence — the electricity meter, the folded shirts the morning after Tunde leaves, the phone call to Lagos she does not make — holds domestic interiority at narrative distance with a control that many mid-career novelists never reach. The decision to give her an unglamorous job (care work on the Denmark Hill geriatric ward) and to let her be competent and bored there, rather than saintly or exploited, is the ethical center of the book. Do not soften it.

**What's not working.**

- *The Tunde marriage is under-dramatized before his departure in Ch. 15.* The chapter tells us he leaves; it does not show us why *this* marriage, of all the marriages you could have written, was the one that ended. One scene — a fight, a refused meal, a small cruelty either way — would make his departure feel chosen rather than narratively convenient. A paragraph would not be enough; a scene, probably somewhere in Part II.
- *Ch. 17's warmth.* The Aunty Fola reappearance. See below.

### Abeni / 1960s Lagos — consider trimming

**What's working.** Essentially everything. Ch. 2, Ch. 4, Ch. 8, Ch. 13, and Ch. 21 are the book's strongest chapters and are the reason the auction went the way it did. I have nothing to suggest about them.

**What's not working.** Ch. 7 and Ch. 15 reprise rather than advance. Ch. 7's wedding-preparation sequence repeats the kinship material Ch. 3 already established; Ch. 15's post-war market scene echoes Ch. 13 without moving Abeni forward. If you take option B of diagnosis #1 — re-weighting the braid — these are the candidates.

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her. Revise her second appearance; do not cut her presence.

You asked directly; I will answer directly. Cutting Aunty Fola would solve a word-count problem you do not have and create a thematic problem you do. She is the only character who physically inhabits two threads — Lagos in her first appearances, Peckham in Ch. 17 — and is therefore the only piece of human machinery currently braiding the generations in anything stronger than metaphor. Given that diagnosis #2 above is precisely that the braid needs more mechanical connection, not less, Aunty Fola is a structural asset wearing the costume of a darling. Cutting her would make your biggest revision harder, not easier.

What I think is actually troubling you about her is not her presence but her tonal register. Her Lagos chapters (Ch. 4, Ch. 8, Ch. 13) carry heavy work — the lantern motif, the civil-war material, the moral counterweight to Abeni's mother-in-law — while the 1994 reappearance in Ch. 17 is warm and memorial in a book that has otherwise earned the right to be difficult. Ch. 17 currently reads as an author loving a character. It needs to read as a character imposing a cost on another character. Ask what Fola is refusing to say in Ch. 17 and build the chapter around that refusal. The lantern should change hands with weight, not gift-wrap. If you do this, she more than earns her space; if you cannot find the refusal, then the problem is Ch. 17, not Aunty Fola, and you will know you have diagnosed the real issue.

So: firm keep. The question is not whether she earns her space in the book; it is whether Ch. 17 earns hers.

## Targeted questions

For your notebook, not for me. Answer them before you begin the revision.

1. What does Temi want that she would be ashamed to name aloud to Folasade? Not "connection" — something smaller and more damning.
2. What does Folasade know about Abeni that she has decided Temi will never be told, and what is the specific scene — where, when, in response to what — in which she decided it?
3. In the twelve months after Tunde leaves, what is the worst thing Folasade does to herself, or to her daughter? The draft gives us her competence; where does the competence fail?
4. If Abeni could read Temi's PhD dissertation, which sentence would most offend her, and why?
5. What is the salt lantern, physically, in 2023? Who owns it? Can it still be lit? If yes, who has matches?
6. The civil war is present in Abeni's thread and absent as *consequence* in the later threads. What does Folasade in 1994 or Temi in 2023 not do — or not know how to do — because of Biafra?
7. Ronke is barely present. Is that a structural choice or an avoidance? If the former, what in the text marks it as chosen rather than omitted?
8. Aunty Fola knows something about Abeni's marriage that Abeni does not know Fola knows. What is it? Does the reader ever learn? Does Fola ever use it?
9. What is the book's position on Temi's research: vocation, defense mechanism, or both? Does the prose currently know which, or is it deferring the question?
10. If the novel had to end on a single image rather than the current scene of reading, what would it be — and what does that tell you about the ending you actually want?

## Scope and next steps

**In scope for this revision.** Address the three big-picture issues above, prioritizing #2. Raise the Bristol thread (one of three routes in #1). Revise Ch. 17. Cut or reconceive Ch. 7 and Ch. 15 if you pursue option B of #1. Rewrite the Ch. 25 reversal so that it costs Temi a decision.

**Out of scope for this revision.**

- Line-level prose polish. Your sentences are already better than most of the books on our list; we will address phrasing in the copyedit pass in July.
- A fourth POV. I can feel the temptation — a Tunde chapter, a Ronke chapter — and I am asking in advance that you resist it. A fourth voice would dissolve the architecture we are trying to strengthen.
- Expansion of the historical research. The civil-war material is precisely dosed. More would tilt the book toward saga and away from intimacy, which is where its power lives.
- The opening. Ch. 1 and Ch. 2 are the reason we bought the novel. They do not need anything. If you find yourself wanting to touch them during the revision, that is a signal that you are avoiding harder work elsewhere.
