Dear Naledi,

I've just finished my second read of "The Salt Lantern," and before anything else I want to name what this book is doing that I haven't seen done quite this way before. The refused-coin scene in Ch. 2 (p. 19-23), where the salt-seller tells Abeni that "a girl with no mother buys nothing on a Tuesday," is a book teaching me its own cosmology through a withheld transaction — not exposition, not a flashback, a refusal. And when that refused coin echoes thirty years later in Folasade's 1994 Peckham electricity meter (Ch. 11, p. 142) — same gesture, same denominations, different empire — your braided structure stops being an organizing principle and becomes the argument. This is a novel about what money remembers that bodies forget, and its form knows it.

The other thing the book is doing, and doing well, is trusting silence. Aunty Fola on the verandah in Ch. 4 (p. 51), lighting the lantern without speaking to the child who is watching her; Folasade in Ch. 12 folding her husband's shirt the morning after he leaves, counted movements instead of a thought; these are scenes where a lesser draft would have given us interior monologue and you have given us choreography. The Lagos and Peckham threads already earn the comparisons to Gyasi and Evaristo that we put in the acquisitions memo. What this revision needs to do is raise the Bristol thread to meet them, and — more urgently — convert your book's thematic braid into a mechanical one so that the generations don't merely rhyme; they cause each other.

## Big-picture diagnosis

Before the specifics: **if you only fix one thing in this revision, fix issue #2 below.** The Bristol thinness (issue #1) is more visible, but the causal weakness is what keeps the book from landing its final argument, and it's the note I'd be most embarrassed not to have given you.

### 1. Bristol doesn't yet carry third-wall weight

Temi gets 21% of the word count against Abeni's 43% and Folasade's 36%, and the deficit is not only proportional — it's textural. Temi's chapters summarize where the historical threads dramatize. We are told about her PhD, her father's silence, her breakup with Seun; we are *shown* Abeni's wedding night and Folasade's hospital waiting room. The consequence is that the novel's contemporary claim — that the granddaughter who can google her grandmother is not therefore free of her — arrives as assertion rather than enactment.

**What might unlock it.** Three genuinely different routes, pick one:

- *Expand Temi structurally.* Give her a fourth and fifth chapter in Part III (currently she only has three there), and make at least two of her existing chapters scenic where they are currently reflective — the call to Folasade in Ch. 14, the archive discovery in Ch. 18. This is the most conservative fix and preserves your current architecture.
- *Re-weight the braid.* Cut one Abeni chapter and one Folasade chapter (candidates: Ch. 7 and Ch. 15, both of which reprise rather than advance) and redistribute that real estate to Temi. This keeps the book at 92k while shifting the center of gravity forward in time. More aggressive but more elegant.
- *Let Temi narrate the frame.* Open and close the book in Temi's first person, with the 1960s and 1990s sections as her reconstruction — a move closer to what Azumah Nelson does in "Open Water." This would be a major architectural revision and I'd want to talk before you committed to it, but it solves the Bristol problem by making Bristol the book's *situation* rather than one of its three settings.

### 2. The threads rhyme but don't cause

Right now Abeni's 1967 choices (the marriage to Tunde, the severed contact with her sister Ronke, the migration) don't visibly determine Folasade's specific 1994 losses, which in turn don't visibly determine Temi's specific 2023 paralysis. The braid is doing metaphor-work but not machine-work. A careful reader finishing the book can say "these women inherit each other's grief" but cannot say "because Abeni did X in 1967, Folasade in 1994 had no choice but Y, and Temi in 2023 cannot do Z without first undoing both." The novel's political claim — that diasporic inheritance is not sentimental but structural — requires that structure.

**What might unlock it.** Three alternatives, not three framings of the same fix:

- *Plant a mechanical through-line object.* The salt lantern itself is a candidate but is currently underused as object — it appears in Ch. 4 and Ch. 17 and is then referenced but not handled. Make it something that gets broken, repaired, sold, hidden, returned; a thing whose physical fate the reader tracks across three generations and whose location in 2023 is the resolution of the plot.
- *Build a sororal shadow-plot.* Ronke, Abeni's severed sister, is almost absent from the current draft. Bring her forward: her descendants in present-day Lagos, a cousin Temi discovers through research. Let the book's causality be "Abeni cut Ronke off, and because of that Folasade grew up without an auntie, and because of that Temi has to reconstruct from archives what should have been given to her at a kitchen table." This is the Homegoing-shaped route but with a more intimate two-branch structure.
- *Make the silence causal, not atmospheric.* What if there is a specific thing Abeni never told Folasade, which Folasade accordingly cannot tell Temi, and which the revelation in Ch. 25 is the explicit rupture of? This preserves your current plot bones and reframes the letters-in-the-tin as the breaking of a chain rather than the discovery of a continuity.

### 3. The Ch. 25 reversal is a revelation, not a reversal

The tin of letters reframes what the reader knows but doesn't force any character to act. Temi reads, feels moved, and the novel ends. A third-act reversal should cost the viewpoint character a decision — something refused, something chosen, something that could not be chosen before the revelation — and right now the book resolves at the level of the reader's understanding rather than the characters'.

**What might unlock it.**

- *Make Temi choose between two incompatible fidelities* after she reads the letters: her mother's version of the family story, or her grandmother's. Have her publish, or not publish, or call, or not call. An act, not a feeling.
- *Move the discovery earlier* (end of Part III rather than Part IV) so that the final movement of the book is the consequences of knowing, not the fact of knowing. This is the simpler fix and might be sufficient.
- *Withhold a letter.* Let Temi find the tin but discover, by the end, that one letter was destroyed — so the revelation is also a loss, and the reversal is her acceptance that the record she wanted is not available. This is the most formally interesting option and also the riskiest.

## Thread-by-thread notes

### Temi / 2023 Bristol — the thread that needs the most work

**What's working.** The opening of Ch. 6 (p. 74-78), Temi in the archive, is the best contemporary writing in the draft. Photographing microfiche with her phone because the scanner is broken; the small shame of knowing her supervisor would disapprove; the admission that she is researching her grandmother partly to avoid her mother — that triangulation (scholarship as evasion) is the spine of a character, and the chapter trusts it. I also loved the tiny moment in Ch. 16 (p. 211) where Temi catches herself pronouncing "Abeni" the way her mother does rather than the way her supervisor does, and chooses the supervisor's pronunciation for the rest of the meeting. That is a sentence that contains a thesis.

**What's not working.**

- *Interiority flattens after Ch. 6.* From Ch. 10 onward the close-third defaults to telling us Temi's feelings rather than staging the gap between what she feels and what she will admit. The Ch. 14 call-and-hang-up (p. 183) is a structurally beautiful beat that the prose then explains: "she wasn't ready" arrives where the texture of un-readiness should. Trust the gesture; cut the gloss.
- *Marcus isn't earning his space.* He appears in Ch. 9, Ch. 16, and Ch. 22, and in each appearance he is a sounding board rather than a person with friction of his own. He should want something from Temi that complicates her research — not necessarily romantic; perhaps he wants her to stop, or to write differently, or to acknowledge that her project uses him as stabilizer. If he can't be given his own want, cut him; the space is better spent on Seun, who is currently too thin for the weight the breakup is asked to carry.
- *Bristol itself is under-rendered.* The Lagos thread has heat, salt, specific birds; Peckham has specific buses, specific cold, specific fried-fish shops. Bristol 2023 is mostly interiors — Temi's flat, the archive, one pub. The city that represents the diasporic present reads as generic British-academic, and this is a thematic failure, not a sensory one. Bristol's slave-trade history is *latent* in your book and never lands on the page. Temi walking past the Colston plinth (or the hole where it was) without the narration registering it is a missed structural beat given your book's concerns. The city needs to do work.
- *The Seun breakup (Ch. 20) arrives too late and costs too little.* By Ch. 20 the reader has accepted that Temi is alone; the breakup reads as backfill. Either move it to Part II so that the reader's sense of Temi's isolation is earned by event rather than stipulation, or cut it and let her loneliness be pre-existing.

### Folasade / 1994 Peckham — close to done

**What's working.** Folasade is the book's most fully realized character. Her Ch. 11 and Ch. 12 sequence — the electricity meter, the folded shirts, the phone call she doesn't make to Lagos — is a masterclass in domestic interiority held at narrative distance. The decision to give her an unglamorous job (care work at the Denmark Hill geriatric ward) and to let her be competent and bored there, rather than saintly or exploited, is the ethical center of the book.

**What's not working.** Two notes, both small:

- *Her relationship with Tunde is under-dramatized before he leaves in Ch. 15.* We know he is leaving because the chapter tells us; we don't know why *this* marriage, across all the marriages you could have written, was the one that ended. One additional scene — a fight, a refused meal, a small cruelty in either direction — would make his departure feel chosen rather than narratively convenient.
- *The Aunty Fola reappearance in Ch. 17 is too warm.* See below.

### Abeni / 1960s Lagos — working, but consider trimming

**What's working.** Almost everything. Ch. 2, Ch. 4, Ch. 8, Ch. 13, and Ch. 21 are the novel's strongest chapters and the reason the book sold at auction.

**What's not working.** Ch. 7 and Ch. 15 reprise material already established — Ch. 7's wedding-preparation sequence repeats the kinship exposition of Ch. 3, and Ch. 15's post-war market scene echoes Ch. 13 without advancing Abeni's arc. If you need the real estate for Temi (see diagnosis #1, option B), these are the candidates.

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her. Revise her second appearance, don't cut her presence.

I'll answer directly because you asked directly. Cutting Aunty Fola would solve a word-count problem you don't have and create a thematic problem you do. She is the only character who physically inhabits two threads — the Lagos of her first appearance and the Peckham of Ch. 17 — and she is therefore the only piece of human machinery currently braiding the generations in anything stronger than metaphor. Given that diagnosis #2 above is precisely that the braid needs more mechanical connection, not less, she is a structural asset wearing the costume of a darling. Cutting her would make your biggest revision harder, not easier.

What I suspect is actually troubling you about her is not her presence but her tonal register: her Lagos chapters (Ch. 4, Ch. 8, Ch. 13) are doing heavy work — establishing the lantern motif, voicing the civil-war material, providing the moral counterweight to Abeni's mother-in-law — while her 1994 reappearance (Ch. 17) is warm and memorial in a book that has otherwise earned the right to be difficult. Ch. 17 currently reads as an author loving a character; it needs to read as a character imposing a cost on another character. Ask what Fola is refusing to say in that scene, and build the chapter around that refusal. The lantern should change hands with weight, not gift-wrap.

So: firm keep. The question is not whether she earns her space but whether Ch. 17 does.

## Targeted questions

These are for your notebook, not for me. Answer them for yourself before you begin the revision.

1. What does Temi want that she would be ashamed to name aloud to Folasade? (Not "connection" — something smaller and more damning.)
2. What does Folasade know about Abeni that she has decided Temi will not be told, and what is the specific scene in which she made that decision?
3. In the twelve months after Tunde leaves, what is the worst thing Folasade does to herself or her daughter? The book currently gives us her competence; where does the competence fail?
4. If Abeni could read Temi's PhD dissertation, what is the sentence she would be most offended by?
5. What is the salt lantern, physically, in 2023? Who owns it? Can it still be lit?
6. The civil war is present in Abeni's thread but absent as *consequence* in the later threads. What does Folasade in 1994 or Temi in 2023 not do, or not know how to do, because of Biafra?
7. Ronke — Abeni's sister — is barely present. Is that a structural choice or an avoidance? If the former, what in the text marks it as chosen rather than omitted?
8. Aunty Fola knows something about Abeni's marriage that Abeni does not know Fola knows. What is it, and does the reader ever learn?
9. What is the book's position on Temi's research? Is her archival impulse a vocation, a defense mechanism, or both — and does the prose currently know which?
10. If the novel had to end on a single image rather than the current scene of reading, what would it be, and what does that tell you about the ending you actually want?

## Scope and next steps

**What this revision should do.** Address the three big-picture issues above, with priority on #2 (causality). Raise the Bristol thread. Revise Ch. 17. Cut or reconceive Ch. 7 and Ch. 15 if you pursue option B of issue #1. Rewrite the Ch. 25 reversal so that it costs Temi a decision.

**What this revision should NOT do.**

- Do not chase line-level prose polish. Your sentences are already better than most of the books we publish; we will address phrasing in the copyedit pass in July.
- Do not add a new POV. I can feel the temptation (a Tunde chapter, a Ronke chapter) and I am asking you in advance to resist it. A fourth voice would dissolve the architecture we are trying to strengthen.
- Do not expand the historical research. The civil-war material is precisely dosed; more would tilt the book toward saga and away from intimacy.
- Do not rewrite the opening. Ch. 1 and Ch. 2 are the reason we bought the book. They do not need anything.
