Dear Naledi,

I've just finished my second read of "The Salt Lantern," and before I say anything else I want to tell you what the 1960s Lagos sections are doing that I haven't seen a debut novelist do in a long time. The scene where Abeni walks the market after her mother's burial (Ch. 2, p. 19-23), watching the salt-seller refuse her coin because "a girl with no mother buys nothing on a Tuesday" — that is a book teaching me its own cosmology, and doing it through a refused transaction rather than exposition. I believed the economy, the grief, and the superstition simultaneously, and I believed them because you trusted the reader to sit in the unexplained. Aunty Fola's first appearance in Ch. 4 (the lantern-lighting on the verandah, p. 51) gave me the same feeling — a character who arrives already weighted with a history the prose refuses to flatten.

The braided structure is doing real thematic work, too. When Folasade in 1994 Peckham counts out coins for the electricity meter (Ch. 11, p. 142) and the reader hears the echo of that refused Lagos coin thirty years and four thousand miles earlier — that is the book's argument about inheritance written into its form, not its dialogue. This is the novel I bought, and it is mostly on the page. Now I want to talk about what the revision needs to do to bring the whole architecture up to the level of its best sections.

## Big-picture diagnosis

### 1. The Bristol thread doesn't yet carry equal weight

The acquisitions memo I wrote fourteen months ago pitched this as a three-voice book, but in the current draft Temi's 2023 Bristol sections read as a frame around the "real" historical material rather than a third load-bearing wall. Temi gets 21% of the word count (I counted) while Abeni gets 43% and Folasade 36%. More importantly, Temi's chapters are where the prose gets most summarized and least scenic — we are *told* about her PhD, her breakup, her father's silence, but the chapters that dramatize these things (Ch. 19, Ch. 23) are shorter and less embodied than anything in the Lagos thread. The consequence is that the book's contemporary argument — what does any of this inheritance *mean* now, for a woman who can google her own grandmother — lands as assertion rather than dramatization.

### 2. The causal chain between generations is under-articulated

Right now the three threads rhyme thematically but don't quite *cause* each other. Abeni's choices in 1967 (the marriage, the migration, the severed contact with her sister) don't visibly shape Folasade's specific losses in 1994, which in turn don't visibly shape Temi's specific 2023 paralysis. The braided structure is doing metaphorical work but not mechanical work. A reader finishing the book can say "these women carry each other's grief" but cannot say "because Abeni did X, Folasade had to do Y, and Temi cannot do Z."

### 3. The third-act reversal is currently a revelation, not a reversal

The Ch. 25 discovery (the letters in the tin) reframes what we know but doesn't force any character to act differently. Temi reads them, feels moved, and the book ends. A third-act reversal should cost someone something — a decision, a refusal, a door closed — and right now the plot resolves at the level of the reader's understanding rather than the characters'.

## Thread-by-thread notes: Temi / 2023 Bristol

This is the thread that needs the most work, so I'm going to spend the most time here.

**What's working:** The opening of Ch. 6 (Temi in the archive, p. 74-78) is the best contemporary writing in the book. The detail of her photographing microfiche with her phone because the library's scanner is broken — and the small shame of knowing her supervisor would disapprove — gives me a Temi who is specific: a PhD student who cuts corners, who knows she cuts corners, and who is researching her own grandmother partly as a way to avoid her mother. That triangulation (scholarship as avoidance) is the spine of a character, and it's there in Ch. 6 and nowhere else with the same sharpness.

**What's not working:**

- **Temi's interiority flattens after Ch. 6.** From Ch. 10 onward her chapters default to a close-third that tells us her feelings rather than dramatizing the gap between what she feels and what she admits. The Ch. 14 scene where she calls Folasade and hangs up before it rings (p. 183) is structurally a great beat but the surrounding prose over-explains it — we get "she wasn't ready" when we should get the texture of un-readiness. Trust the gesture.
- **The Marcus relationship isn't earning its space.** He appears in Ch. 9, Ch. 16, and Ch. 22, but in each appearance he functions as a sounding board for Temi's feelings about her family rather than a person with his own friction against her. If he's going to stay, he needs to want something from Temi that complicates her research, not just listen to her process it.
- **The Bristol setting is under-rendered.** Compared to the sensory density of Lagos (the heat, the salt, the specific birds) and Peckham (the specific buses, the specific cold), Bristol in 2023 is mostly interiors — Temi's flat, the archive, one pub. The city that is supposed to represent the diasporic present reads as generic British-academic, and that's a thematic problem, not just a sensory one. Bristol has a slave-trade history that is latent in the book but never lands on the page; Temi walking past the Colston plinth (or its absence) without the narration registering it is a missed structural opportunity.
- **The breakup with Seun (Ch. 20) arrives too late and does too little.** By Ch. 20 the reader has accepted that Temi is alone; the breakup reads as backfill rather than event.

**What might help:** Consider whether Temi's research *itself* can generate plot — a discovery in the archive that forces her to call her mother, a document whose existence Folasade doesn't want acknowledged. Right now the archive is a setting; it could be an antagonist.

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her. Not as she currently is, but keep her.

You asked me directly so I'll answer directly: cutting Aunty Fola would solve a word-count problem you don't have and create a thematic problem you do. She is the only character in the book who exists in two threads (Lagos 1967 and Peckham 1994, via the letters) and who therefore physically braids the generations. Cutting her would leave the threads touching only through metaphor, and my first big-picture note above is precisely that the threads need more mechanical, not less metaphorical, connection. Aunty Fola is a structural asset wearing the costume of a darling.

What I think is actually bothering you about her is that her Lagos chapters (Ch. 4, Ch. 8, Ch. 13) are doing a lot of work — establishing the lantern motif, voicing the novel's most overt political material about the civil war — while her 1994 reappearance (Ch. 17) is quieter and risks feeling like fan-service for a character the reader has grown to love. The fix isn't to cut her; it's to give her 1994 scene a cost. Right now she arrives, she sees Folasade, she gives the lantern. Ask yourself what she is refusing to say in that scene, and whether the chapter can be built around that refusal.

Provisional yes on keeping her. Firm yes after revision.
