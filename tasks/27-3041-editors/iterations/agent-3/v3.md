Dear Naledi,

I have finished my second read of "The Salt Lantern," and before anything else I want to tell you what the book is doing that nothing on our comp list is doing. Gyasi in "Homegoing" lets history arrive as event. Evaristo in "Girl, Woman, Other" lets history arrive as voice. You have written a novel in which history arrives through the senses — through the grain of a wick, the sound of a rally audible through a kitchen window, the weight of a lantern held at a threshold. In Ch. 2, Abeni notices the grain of the wick before she notices her own shaking hands. Two chapters later, in Ch. 4 (p. 47), the Ibadan rally becomes audible through the kitchen window at the exact instant Mama Iyabo asks about the christening money. You never once narrate that the political is domestic; you arrange the furniture so the reader cannot miss it. That is the craft move the novel is organized around, and it is the reason Jemima and I went as far as we did in the auction.

The second thing I want to name, because a letter that is all problems is a bad letter: Ch. 8, when Abeni realizes she has been mispronouncing her own daughter's name for three weeks and has not been corrected. One paragraph, no commentary, the whole thesis of the novel compressed into a domestic oversight. Keep it exactly as it is. And Ch. 15, Folasade on the night bus with the sleeping child on her shoulder, counting the stops between who she was and who she has become — that is a scene I will still be thinking about in five years. There are at least a dozen passages I could add to this list, and I will flag more as we go. I want you to know the floor of this novel is high.

## The big-picture issues

I see three structural problems the revision must address. They are nested — issue 1 is the ground, issue 2 is the architecture, issue 3 is the roof — and solving them in that order is the efficient path. If you only fix one thing, fix issue 1. Everything else gets easier.

### Issue 1: Temi has a voice problem because she has a desire problem

Abeni wants the compound, the name, the lineage protected: stakes named by Ch. 3. Folasade wants out of her mother's house and into a country that will not keep asking her where she's from: stakes named by Ch. 10. Temi wants — and here the prose goes careful, and the carefulness is the tell — to "understand her mother," to "feel less sad," to "figure things out." The verbs are abstract where the other two women's verbs are concrete. You wrote in your letter that you struggled with her voice. I think what you struggled with is her desire, and voice cannot be solved until desire is. Everything thin about the Bristol thread traces back to this.

**What might unlock it.** Three genuinely alternative paths. Pick one; they do not combine.

*Path A — the inheritance frame.* Temi is given a concrete object of desire that only the novel's family history can grant her: the deed to the Lagos compound, the final sentence her mother stopped mid-utterance in 2007, the true name she has been carrying. Her arc becomes an inheritance arc, the braid starts paying off mechanically, and the novel becomes an inheritance novel in the classical sense.

*Path B — the refusal frame.* Temi wants something that has nothing to do with her family — the city, the girlfriend, the freelance life — and the novel's engine is the gravitational pull of the family dragging her back toward a story she is trying not to be in. This is a book about the cost of escape, not the rewards of return. It would require cutting some of the grief scaffolding (the hospice arc becomes shorter, sharper) but it solves the "character study inside a multigenerational novel" problem by making that tension the subject.

*Path C — the shame frame.* Temi wants something she would be ashamed to say aloud to Folasade — to leave the family entirely, to convert, to not have children, to hate the grandmother we are being asked to love — and the novel is organized around the pressure of her not saying it. This is the hardest path because it requires the reader to feel the shape of the unsaid thing, but Ch. 17 with Ayo proves you can do it at the scene level. Sustain that mode across nine chapters and you have a novel nobody else is writing.

A is the safe revision. B is the most commercially legible. C is the Graywolf-list book. I would not choose for you, but if you want my read: C.

### Issue 2: The braid promises convergence at the level of imagery, not knowledge

Three POVs, three eras, alternating chapters — the form itself is a promise that these three women will end in the same conceptual room. You deliver on that promise at the level of the lantern and at nothing else. At the end of Ch. 27, Temi does not know more about Abeni than she did in Ch. 1. Folasade does not know more about her mother than she did in Ch. 10. The reader, meanwhile, has been given information across the braid that none of the characters possess. The effect is that the reader performs the convergence work the characters should be performing, and the ending reads as curated rather than earned.

**What might unlock it.** Three paths that actually diverge.

*Path A — the traveling document.* One physical object moves across the three eras. A letter Abeni wrote in 1967 and did not send. A photograph. A deed. Each woman encounters it with different information and reads it differently. By Ch. 26, all three of them have touched the same piece of paper, and the reader watches the meaning accumulate. This is the lowest-risk revision: you are adding a single object, not restructuring.

*Path B — the withheld scene, shown three times.* Pick one moment — I would suggest the 1967 departure from Lagos, currently handled in summary in Ch. 9 — and write it fully, from Abeni's POV in Part 1, from Folasade's childhood memory in Part 2, and from Temi's discovered-evidence angle in Part 3. The convergence becomes epistemic: the reader and Temi arrive at the whole scene together. This plays directly to your strengths; the Lagos thread's scene-making is where your prose is most confident.

*Path C — collapse the timeline.* Temi meets Folasade in narrative present, and the 1990s thread becomes a flashback structure inside their conversation. The most structurally invasive option — roughly 18,000 words need to move or disappear — and the one that refuses the braid rather than delivering on it.

B is what I would try first. It solves issue 2 and makes issue 3 cheaper to fix, because the third iteration of the scene lives in Part 3 and arrives at Temi.

### Issue 3: The third-act reversal lands on the wrong generation

The reveal in Ch. 25 — Abeni's first marriage, the child who did not survive, the name Folasade was given — is placed as the novel's turn. It turns Folasade. She reacts, she recontextualizes, she weeps in the kitchen. But Temi is the present-tense narrator, the reader-surrogate, and the character holding the last three chapters. A third-act reversal that pivots the middle generation and not the present-day one leaves Ch. 26 and Ch. 27 in the hands of a protagonist who does not know what the novel has just revealed.

**What might unlock it.** Three options that are not cousins.

*Path A — relocate agency.* Temi becomes the one who uncovers the reveal, not Folasade's audience to it. The scene moves to her, physically: she is in the Lagos house, she is reading the letter, she is the one who calls her mother rather than being called. This changes who the reversal belongs to without changing what is revealed.

*Path B — split the reveal.* Folasade learns one thing in Ch. 25 — the first marriage, the lost child. Temi learns a different, costlier thing in Ch. 26 — something that only her generation has the frame to recognize as devastating. What Folasade discovers about the past is different in kind from what Temi discovers about her own life. The reveal becomes two reveals of different weight, each landing on the character best equipped to feel it.

*Path C — move the reveal earlier.* End-of-Part-2 placement for the first-marriage reveal. The true third-act reversal is then something about Temi's present that only makes sense in light of what we learned in Part 2. This reorganizes the novel's center of gravity toward Temi and costs you the current ending; the exchange may be worth it.

I am most drawn to B. It is the option that honors the braided form — three women, three revelations, each in her own register.

## Thread-by-thread notes

The Bristol thread gets the most space here because that is where the revision lives. Lagos is largely working. Peckham needs one structural move. Bristol needs rebuilding.

### Bristol (Temi, 2023)

*What is working.* Temi at the sentence level is alive. The opening of Ch. 14 — the bus stop in Stokes Croft, the list of her ex's mispronunciations of her name — is some of the sharpest prose in the novel; the rhythm of that list does for Temi what the lantern does for Abeni. The Lebanese-restaurant scene with her cousin Ayo in Ch. 17 is the first time in the Bristol thread I felt two people in a room rather than one consciousness performing at the reader. More Ayo. He is the catalyst this thread is missing; he is Temi's Folasade.

*What is not working — in priority order.*

*(1) The desire problem (issue 1 above) is the Bristol problem.* Fixing it fixes roughly two-thirds of what is thin here. This is where to start.

*(2) The Peckham flashbacks inside Temi's chapters are stealing authority from Folasade.* At Ch. 16 (p. 201), Ch. 20 (p. 241), and Ch. 24 (p. 288), Temi "remembers" things she cannot plausibly remember: the smell of her mother's first flat before Temi was born, the texture of her grandmother's wrapper in 1994, the radio program playing the day Folasade took her first citizenship test. Narrative distance collapses in a way the reader registers as authorial reach. These memories belong to Folasade's POV, or they belong nowhere. Right now they do double duty and both threads lose.

*(3) The 2023 plot is three plots doing one plot's work.* The job loss, the breakup, and the hospice arc each carry weight and compete for the same emotional real estate. Only the hospice arc connects to the braid. The breakup is useful characterization for the first third and outlives its utility by Ch. 18 — by Ch. 21 Temi is still processing a relationship the reader has stopped caring about. The job loss does almost no visible work. Demote both to backstory, let the hospice arc structure the present, and the thread will gain velocity without losing texture.

*(4) Interiority without judgment.* Temi's chapters are dense with sensation and thin on conclusion — she notices everything and decides nothing. The stylistic intent is legible and I respect it, but Abeni's and Folasade's chapters let us hear them think, and Temi's let us hear her only perceive. The asymmetry starves the reader of her judgment, which is the instrument by which we feel the stakes of her choices. A few pages per chapter of earned reflection — not interior monologue, not italicized thought, but the character reaching conclusions in the prose itself — would rebalance her voice against the other two.

*(5) The ending.* Ch. 27 closes on an image — the lantern, the Bristol flat, the winter light through the kitchen window. It is a beautiful image, and it is the wrong ending beat. Temi is supposed to be the character who has moved; the closing gesture should be hers to take, not yours to describe. She should do something or refuse to do something, in a sentence that belongs to her and not to the narrator.

### Lagos (Abeni, 1960s)

This thread is working. These are fine-tuning notes for the line edit pass, not revision work.

*What is working.* Almost all of it. The political-domestic interleaving, the object-level attention, the compound as character, the marriage scenes in Ch. 3 and Ch. 6. The rhythm is doing Achebe-inflected work without derivative footprint.

*What is not working.* Two small things. Ch. 9 compresses six months into three pages and the reader loses the weight of what happens there — the one place in Part 1 where I felt the prose summarizing past its best material. And the last page of Ch. 12, the transition out of Lagos, is reaching for a thematic flourish the scene has not earned. Let the departure be ordinary; let the reader do the thematic work.

### Peckham (Folasade, 1990s)

*What is working.* Folasade is the novel's most quietly complex creation — the ambivalence toward her mother, the refusal to perform diaspora, the sharp comic voice in the workplace scenes at the council office. Ch. 15 (the bus ride home) is a scene I will still remember in five years.

*What is not working.* Folasade is structurally underused. She has the authority to narrate Peckham — see issue re: Temi's misplaced memories — she is the generation that bridges the other two, and she has the fewest chapters of the three (eight, versus ten for Abeni and nine for Temi). If you go with issue 2 path B, Folasade's middle telling of the 1967 scene will carry the most weight, and she needs the room. Add one chapter: specifically, Folasade and her mother in the late 1990s, a scene currently only referred to. That chapter does not exist in the manuscript and its absence is audible.

## Targeted questions for the revision

I want you to be able to answer these before you rewrite. Some you have probably asked; a few I am guessing you have not.

1. What does Temi want that she would be ashamed to name aloud to Folasade?
2. What is the sentence Folasade has been refusing to say to her mother for thirty years? Write it out. You do not have to use it in the novel, but you need to know it.
3. What does Abeni, in 1967, know that she has decided her daughter will never know — and is that decision the novel's inciting incident?
4. If the lantern motif were cut from the book entirely, what structural connective tissue would be left? If the answer is "nothing," the motif is doing work the architecture should be doing instead.
5. Which of the three women does the novel believe is right about the family? "All of them" is not an answer; a novel that refuses to judge its own characters refuses to have a thesis.
6. What is the one piece of information that, if Temi learned it in Ch. 26, would change her action in Ch. 27 — and is she currently learning it?
7. Who is the reader you are writing for? Not demographically: what does that reader know about 1967 Lagos that they do not know about 2023 Bristol, and is the prose calibrated to that asymmetry? (I suspect it currently over-orients the reader to Lagos and under-orients them to the specific Bristol Temi inhabits.)
8. Why is Aunty Fola unmarried? The novel assumes it and the reader notices the assumption. Make it a decision she made or a thing that happened to her, but do not leave it as ambient fact.
9. Who, in Temi's life, is she performing for — and what does the novel cost her for that performance?
10. If the book ended with Temi in Lagos rather than Bristol, would it be a better novel or only a more expected one?

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her. Cut her subplot.

You asked directly, so I am answering directly. Aunty Fola is not cut-able — she is the only character present across all three eras, her scenes with Abeni in Ch. 7 and with Temi in Ch. 23 are doing connective work no one else in the novel can do, and her comic timing in Ch. 18's kitchen scene is one of the book's pleasures. Cut her and you cut a load-bearing wall.

But the church-fund arc in Ch. 11 and Ch. 13 is the part of her you are unsure about, and your instinct is sound: it is a self-contained short story stapled to the novel. You gave Fola a plot of her own because you were worried she did not have enough to do. She does not need a plot. She is strongest as the character who moves through other people's scenes and refuses to recede. Cut the church fund (roughly 3,400 words) and redistribute Fola into three additional scenes in the Bristol thread — a phone call, an unannounced visit, a confrontation — where she is not carrying plot but acting as catalyst. She will sharpen. Characters like her are weakened, not strengthened, by having their own subplot; they need the oxygen of other people's.

If after the revision she still feels unearned, we revisit. I do not think she will.
