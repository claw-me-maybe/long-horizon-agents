Dear Naledi,

I have finished my second pass through "The Salt Lantern," and I want to lead with the thing I keep coming back to: the novel has a physical imagination that almost nobody working in the literary-debut space right now has. The salt lantern itself — Abeni holding it at the threshold of her father's compound in Ch. 2, the way she notices the grain of the wick before she notices her own shaking hands — is the kind of object-level attention that carries the whole first section. You are writing people who live inside weather and cookware and the specific grain of wood, and the reader believes them because you believed them first.

The second thing I want to name is how the 1960s Lagos section earns its politics. The move in Ch. 4, where the rally outside the Ibadan gate becomes audible through the kitchen window exactly when Abeni's mother-in-law asks about the christening money — that is the book's thesis stated through furniture. You never once tell us "the political was domestic." You arrange the scene so we cannot miss it. That is real craft, and it is the reason Jemima and I fought for the book in the auction.

## The big-picture issues

### The Bristol thread is a character study inside a novel that is otherwise doing multigenerational inheritance

Temi in 2023 is rendered as a consciousness — a sharp, grieving, post-breakup consciousness — rather than as a node in the family's unfinished business. The other two threads are doing load-bearing work: Abeni's choices in 1967 generate Folasade's 1994, and Folasade's silences generate Temi's 2023. But Temi's sections, particularly Ch. 19 through Ch. 22, could be lifted out and published as a novella and the causal chain of the novel would not notice. She is reacting to her life, not to her inheritance.

### The braided structure is promising a convergence it hasn't yet earned

Three POVs, three eras, alternating — the form itself makes a promise that by the end these three women will be in the same conceptual room. Right now the book delivers that only at the level of imagery (the lantern appears in all three), not at the level of plot or knowledge. Temi at the end of Ch. 27 does not know more about her grandmother than she did at the start, and the reader does not know more about why this family ended up in Bristol than they could have guessed from the first fifty pages.

### The third act reversal is landing on the wrong character

The reveal about Abeni's first marriage in Ch. 25 is structurally placed as the novel's turn — but it turns Folasade's story, not Temi's. Given that Temi is the present-tense narrator, the contemporary reader-surrogate, and the character holding the novel's final pages, the reversal needs to hit her hardest, and right now she is mostly a witness to it.

## The Bristol thread — what's working, what's not

**What's working.** Temi's voice at the sentence level is alive. The opening of Ch. 14 — her at the bus stop in Stokes Croft, counting the ways her ex mispronounced her name — is some of the sharpest prose in the book. The scene in Ch. 17 at the Lebanese place with her cousin Ayo is the first time in the Bristol thread I felt two people in a room rather than one person performing consciousness at the reader. More of that, please.

**What's not working.** Three things, in order of priority.

First, Temi does not want anything with the specificity that Abeni and Folasade want things. Abeni wants the compound, the name, the lineage protected. Folasade wants out of her mother's house and into a country that will not keep asking where she's from. Temi wants... to be less sad? To understand her mother? The verbs are fuzzy, and the reader feels it. Your own note says you struggled with her voice — I think you struggled with her desire, which is the thing the voice needs to be organized around.

Second, the Peckham flashbacks that show up inside Temi's chapters (Ch. 16, Ch. 20, Ch. 24) are doing Folasade's work for her. They are the wrong character's memories in the wrong character's head, and they dilute both threads. Folasade's section has the authority to narrate Peckham; Temi does not, and when she "remembers" things she could not remember (the smell of her mother's first flat before Temi was born, Ch. 20 p. 241), the reader notices the author reaching.

Third, the 2023 plot — the job loss, the breakup, the grandmother-in-hospice arc — is three plots where the book needs one. The hospice arc is the one that connects to the other two threads. The other two are competing for the same emotional real estate.

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her, but cut the B-plot.

Aunty Fola is not cut-able as a presence — she is the only character in the novel who has been in all three eras, and her scenes with Abeni in Ch. 7 and with Temi in Ch. 23 are doing work no other character can do. She is the novel's connective tissue. But the subplot about her and the church fund in Ch. 11 and Ch. 13 is the part you are unsure about, and you are right to be unsure: it is a self-contained short story stapled to a novel. Cut the church fund arc, keep Fola. She becomes sharper, not thinner, when she has less plot of her own and more presence in other people's scenes.
