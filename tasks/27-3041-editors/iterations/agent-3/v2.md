Dear Naledi,

I have finished my second pass through "The Salt Lantern" and I want to lead with what the book is doing that nothing else on our list is doing. You have written a novel in which the political is never an idea — it is weather, it is kitchen noise, it is the grain of a wick. In Ch. 2, Abeni holds the lantern at the threshold of her father's compound and notices the grain of the wick before she notices her own shaking hands. Four pages later, in Ch. 4, the rally outside the Ibadan gate becomes audible through the kitchen window at the exact instant her mother-in-law asks about the christening money. You never once tell us the political was domestic; you arrange the furniture so we cannot miss it. That is the craft move the book is organized around, and it is doing something neither Gyasi nor Evaristo is trying to do — a historical novel where history arrives through the senses rather than through narrated event.

The other thing I want to name before anything else: the moment in Ch. 8 where Abeni realizes she has been mispronouncing her own daughter's name for three weeks and has not been corrected — that broke me open. It is a one-line scene and it contains the novel's thesis about language, lineage, and the small erasures people accept. Keep it exactly as it is. A letter that is all problems is a bad letter, and I want you to know the book has at least a dozen moments like that; I will flag more as we go.

## The big-picture issues

I see three structural problems that I believe the revision must address. They are nested — fixing the first makes the second easier, and the third is downstream of both — but I want to name them separately because the fixes are different.

### Issue 1: Temi does not have a desire line, and the braided structure is exposing that

Abeni wants the compound, the name, the lineage protected — stakes that are named by Ch. 3. Folasade wants out of her mother's house and into a country that will not keep asking where she's from — stakes named by Ch. 10. Temi wants... the prose is careful here, and the carefulness is the tell. She wants to "understand her mother," to "feel less sad," to "figure things out." The verbs are abstract where the other two women's verbs are concrete. Your own revision letter says you struggled with her voice; I think you struggled with her desire, and voice cannot be solved until desire is. This is the "if you only fix one thing" issue.

**What might unlock it.** Three genuinely different options, not three framings of the same fix.

*Option A — the inheritance frame.* Give Temi a concrete object of desire that only the novel's family history can give her: she wants her grandmother's land in Lagos, or she wants to finish the sentence her mother stopped mid-way in 2007, or she wants to know whose name she actually carries. Her plot becomes an inheritance plot, and the braided structure starts paying off mechanically.

*Option B — the refusal frame.* Temi wants something that has nothing to do with her family and is fighting the novel's gravitational pull toward them. Her desire is the city, the girlfriend, the job — and the novel's action is the family dragging her back. This is a different book than Option A, and it would require cutting some of the grief-scaffolding, but it solves the "character study inside a multigenerational novel" problem by making the tension between those two things the point.

*Option C — the withheld-desire frame.* Temi wants something she would be ashamed to say aloud to Folasade — to leave, to convert, to not have children, to hate the grandmother we are being asked to love — and the novel is organized around her not saying it. This is the hardest of the three because the reader has to feel the shape of the unsaid thing, but you are already good at this at the sentence level; Ch. 17 with Ayo shows you can do it.

I would pick one and commit. They do not combine.

### Issue 2: The braided structure is promising convergence at the level of imagery but not at the level of knowledge

Three POVs, three eras, alternating chapters — the form itself promises the reader that by the end, these three women will be in the same conceptual room. Right now you deliver that only through the recurring lantern motif. At the end of Ch. 27, Temi does not know more about Abeni than she did in Ch. 1; Folasade does not know more about her mother than she did in Ch. 10; the reader, crucially, has been handed information across the braid that none of the characters possess. The result is that the reader is doing the convergence work the characters should be doing, and the ending feels curated rather than earned.

**What might unlock it.** Again, three genuinely different moves.

*Option A — the document.* One physical artifact travels across the three eras — a letter, a photograph, a deed — and each woman reads it differently because she reads it with different information. The convergence is literal: by Ch. 26 all three of them have touched the same piece of paper, and the reader watches the meaning accumulate.

*Option B — the withheld scene, shown three times.* Pick one moment — the 1967 departure from Lagos, say — and show it from Abeni's POV in Part 1, from the outside in Folasade's memory in Part 2, and from Temi's discovered-evidence angle in Part 3. The convergence is epistemic: the reader and Temi arrive at the full scene together.

*Option C — collapse the timeline.* Temi in 2023 meets Folasade in the narrative present, and the novel makes the 1990s thread a flashback structure inside their conversation. This is the most structurally invasive option and would require cutting roughly 18,000 words, but it solves convergence by refusing the braid.

I am most drawn to B for your strengths, but A is the safest revision and C is the most ambitious.

### Issue 3: The third-act reversal is landing on the wrong character

The reveal in Ch. 25 about Abeni's first marriage is structurally placed as the novel's turn. Right now it turns Folasade — she is the one who reacts, who recontextualizes, who weeps in the kitchen. But Temi is the present-tense narrator, the reader-surrogate, and the character holding the novel's final three chapters. A third-act reversal that turns the middle-generation character and not the present-day character leaves the reader stranded in Ch. 26 and Ch. 27 with a protagonist who does not know the thing the novel has just revealed.

**What might unlock it.** Either (a) Temi is the one who uncovers the reveal — she is the active agent of the third act, not Folasade's audience — and the scene shifts to her, or (b) the reveal is split, with Folasade learning one thing in Ch. 25 and Temi learning a different, costlier thing in Ch. 26 that only her generation could understand the weight of, or (c) the reveal is moved earlier, to end-of-Part-2, and the real third-act reversal is something about Temi's own life that only makes sense in light of it. These are genuinely alternative, not three dressings of the same idea.

## Thread-by-thread notes

### The Bristol thread (Temi, 2023) — the revision's center of gravity

This is where the revision lives, so I am going to spend the most time here.

*What is working.* Temi at the sentence level is alive. The opening of Ch. 14 — her at the bus stop in Stokes Croft, counting the ways her ex mispronounced her name — is some of the sharpest prose in the book; the rhythm of that list is doing what the lantern does for Abeni. The Lebanese-restaurant scene with Ayo in Ch. 17 is the first time in the Bristol thread I felt two people in a room rather than one consciousness performing at the reader. More of Ayo; he is the catalyst Temi needs.

*What is not working — in priority order.*

*(1) The desire problem named above is primarily a Bristol problem.* Fixing issue 1 fixes most of what is thin here.

*(2) The Peckham flashbacks inside Temi's chapters are stealing authority from Folasade.* Ch. 16 (p. 201), Ch. 20 (p. 241), Ch. 24 (p. 288) — Temi "remembers" things she cannot possibly remember: the smell of her mother's first flat before Temi was born, the texture of her grandmother's wrapper in 1994. Narrative distance collapses in a way that reads as the author reaching rather than the character reaching. These belong to Folasade's POV or they belong nowhere; right now they do double duty and both threads lose.

*(3) The 2023 plot is three plots.* The job loss, the breakup, and the grandmother-in-hospice arc are each carrying emotional freight, and they are competing for the same real estate. Only one of them — the hospice arc — connects to the braid. The breakup is doing useful characterization work in the first third but outlives its utility by Ch. 18. The job loss is doing almost no work I can see. I would consider demoting both to backstory and letting the hospice arc structure the thread.

*(4) Interiority without reflection.* Temi's chapters are dense with sensation and thin on reflection — she notices everything and concludes nothing. This is a stylistic choice I respect, but it starves the reader of the character's judgment, which is the thing we need to feel the stakes of her choices. Abeni's and Folasade's chapters let us hear them think. Temi's chapters let us only hear her perceive. A few pages of earned reflection — not internal monologue, but conclusion — would rebalance her voice.

*(5) The ending.* Ch. 27 closes on an image (the lantern, the Bristol flat, the winter light) rather than on an action or a decision. Given that Temi is supposed to be the character who has moved, the closing beat should be hers to take, not yours to describe.

### The Lagos thread (Abeni, 1960s)

This thread is largely doing its job; these are fine-tuning notes, not revision notes.

*What is working.* Almost everything. The political-domestic interleaving, the object-level attention, the marriage scenes in Ch. 3 and Ch. 6, the slow-build of the compound as a character. The rhythm of the prose here is doing Ch'inua Achebe-inflected work without being derivative.

*What is not working.* Two small things. The timeline in Ch. 9 compresses six months into three pages and the reader loses the weight of what happens — this is the one place in Part 1 where I felt you were summarizing past the good stuff. And the last page of Ch. 12 (the transition out of Lagos) is doing a thematic flourish the scene did not earn; I would let the departure be ordinary and let the reader do the thematic work.

### The Peckham thread (Folasade, 1990s)

*What is working.* Folasade as a character is the book's most quietly complex creation — her ambivalence toward her mother, her refusal to be a symbol of diaspora, her sharp comic voice in the workplace scenes. Ch. 15 (the bus ride home with the child Temi asleep on her shoulder) is a scene I will remember.

*What is not working.* Folasade is the structurally underused thread. She has the authority to narrate Peckham (see issue re: Temi's flashbacks), she is the generation that bridges the other two, and she is given the fewest chapters (eight, versus ten for Abeni and nine for Temi). If issue 2 is resolved via the "scene shown three times" option, it will be Folasade's middle telling that carries the most weight, and she needs the room to do it. I would consider giving her one more chapter — specifically a chapter that shows her relationship with her own mother in the late 1990s, which is currently only referred to.

## Targeted questions for the revision

I want you to be able to answer these before you start rewriting. Some of them are questions you have almost certainly asked yourself; a few I am guessing you have not.

1. What does Temi want that she would be ashamed to name aloud to Folasade?
2. When Folasade thinks about her mother, what is the specific sentence she has been avoiding saying for thirty years?
3. What does Abeni, in 1967, know that she has decided her daughter will never know — and is that decision the novel's inciting incident?
4. If the lantern motif were cut from the book entirely, what would the braid have left as its structural connective tissue? (If the answer is "nothing," the motif is doing too much work.)
5. Which of the three women does the novel believe is right about the family? The answer cannot be "all of them"; a novel that refuses to judge its characters refuses to have a thesis.
6. What is the one piece of information that, if Temi learned it in Ch. 26, would change what she does in Ch. 27 — and is she currently learning it?
7. Who is the reader you are writing for? Not demographically — I mean, what does that reader know about Lagos in 1967 that they do not know about Bristol in 2023, and is the prose calibrated to that asymmetry?
8. Why is Aunty Fola unmarried? (I am asking because the novel assumes it and the reader notices the assumption; either make it a choice she made or a thing that happened to her, but do not leave it as ambient fact.)
9. If the book ended with Temi in Lagos rather than Bristol, would it be a better novel or just a more expected one?

## The Aunty Fola question

Keep her. Cut her subplot.

You asked me directly, so I am answering directly. Aunty Fola is not cut-able as a presence — she is the only character who has been in all three eras, her scenes with Abeni in Ch. 7 and with Temi in Ch. 23 are doing connective work no other character can do, and her comic timing in the kitchen in Ch. 18 is one of the book's genuine pleasures. To cut her is to cut a load-bearing wall.

But the church-fund arc in Ch. 11 and Ch. 13 is the part of her you are unsure about, and your instinct is correct: it is a self-contained short story stapled to a novel. It gives Fola a plot of her own because you were worried she did not have enough to do, but the truth is that she is most powerful as the character who moves through other people's scenes and refuses to stay in the background. Cut the church fund entirely (roughly 3,400 words), and let Fola be present in three additional scenes in Temi's thread — a phone call, a visit, a confrontation — where she is not the plot but the catalyst. She will get sharper, not thinner. Characters like her do not need their own subplot; they need the oxygen of other people's.

If after the revision Fola still feels unearned, we can revisit. I do not think she will.
