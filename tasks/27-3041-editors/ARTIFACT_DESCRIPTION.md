# Developmental Edit Memo: "The Salt Lantern" by Naledi Okonkwo

## Occupation
- **SOC:** 27-3041
- **Title:** Editors

## Scenario
You are a senior acquiring editor at Graywolf Press. You've acquired Naledi Okonkwo's literary debut novel "The Salt Lantern" (92,000 words), a multigenerational story following three generations of a Nigerian-British family, moving between 1960s Lagos, 1990s Peckham, and 2023 Bristol. Pub date is scheduled for Spring 2027, and you owe Naledi — who is on a tenure-track teaching appointment and working nights — a developmental edit letter by end of day April 25, 2026, so she has a full 8 weeks to revise before the copyedit pass. The book sold at auction on the strength of a gorgeous 1960s Lagos opening; your and the marketing team's concern is whether the contemporary Bristol thread, which was shorter and rougher in the submission, now pulls its weight.

## Inputs
What you have in front of you:
- Full manuscript read twice; 312 margin notes in Google Docs.
- Acquisitions memo you wrote 14 months ago, which pitched the book as "Homegoing meets Small Island, with a sharper political edge."
- Author's own revision letter (included with this pass) where Naledi says: "I know the Bristol sections still feel thinner. I struggled with Temi's voice. Please tell me if Aunty Fola is cut-able — I love her but I don't know if she earns her space."
- Agent (Jemima Forrester at Curtis Brown) flagged via email that Naledi has had "some difficult notes in the past and responds best to specificity over vibe."
- Comparable titles from your marketing plan: Caleb Azumah Nelson's "Open Water," Yaa Gyasi's "Homegoing," Bernardine Evaristo's "Girl, Woman, Other."
- Manuscript structure: 4 parts, 27 chapters, alternating POVs (Grandma Abeni 1960s, Mother Folasade 1990s, Daughter Temi 2023).

## Artifact specification
Produce a **developmental edit letter** (not line edits) addressed directly to the author. Structure:
1. **Opening** (2 paragraphs): Specific, earned praise — name what the book is doing that nothing else is doing. No throat-clearing.
2. **Big-picture diagnosis** (1-2 pages): The 2-4 structural issues you believe the revision must address. Each issue gets a named heading, a diagnosis paragraph, and a "what might unlock it" paragraph (options, not prescriptions).
3. **Thread-by-thread notes** (1 section per POV): What's working, what's not, with chapter-specific examples.
4. **Targeted questions** section: 6-10 questions the author should be able to answer before revising (character interiority, thematic intent, causality).
5. **The Aunty Fola question**: Direct answer to the author's direct question, with rationale.
6. **Scope and next steps**: What this revision should NOT try to do (prevents scope creep), rough timeline, what's in scope for the line edit pass vs. this pass.

Voice: warm but direct. You're a colleague, not a teacher. Cite page/chapter numbers. Name craft moves with craft vocabulary (interiority, narrative distance, braided structure, third-act reversal). Never "I didn't like X" — always "X did/didn't do Y for me" or "the reader I imagined." Name at least two moments that moved you; a pure-problems letter is a bad letter.

## Output format
Single markdown file, letter format (Dear Naledi,). Rough length: 2,200-3,200 words. Use markdown headers for sections; cite manuscript locations as "Ch. 14, p. 183" format. No tracked changes — this precedes line editing.

## Iteration targets
1. Kill the generality — replace any sentence that could apply to a different book with a sentence that could only apply to this book.
2. Answer the Aunty Fola question decisively; drafts that punt with "that's your call" are a failure of editorial courage.
3. Strengthen the "what might unlock it" paragraphs — options should be genuinely alternative, not three framings of the same fix.
4. Rebalance praise and critique — if the opening praise is under 120 words, it's thin; if it's over 400, it's flattery.
5. Make the thread-by-thread notes uneven on purpose: the Bristol thread needs more space than the Lagos thread, because Lagos is working.
6. Add at least 2 questions in the Targeted Questions section that the author probably hasn't asked herself (not "what does Temi want" but e.g., "what does Temi want that she would be ashamed to name aloud to Folasade").

## Success criteria
- The letter gives Naledi a clear mental model of her own manuscript's architecture that she did not have before reading it.
- Structural issues are prioritized (an explicit "if you only fix one thing" moment).
- Chapter-specific citations appear at least 8 times.
- The direct question about Aunty Fola receives a direct, reasoned yes/no/third-option with at least one paragraph of rationale.
- A reader familiar with Graywolf's list can tell this is a Graywolf letter — serious, craft-forward, no marketing pressure language.
- Tone passes the "would Naledi send her partner an angry screenshot of this" test — warm enough to receive, specific enough to act on.

## Scope target
A 15-minute v1 should include: the opening praise paragraphs, a skeleton of 2-3 big-picture issues (diagnosed, not yet with unlock paragraphs), one full thread-by-thread section (Bristol/Temi), and a provisional answer to the Aunty Fola question. Missing: the full questions section, the other two thread sections, the scope/timeline section, and the second-pass polish on the diagnostic language.
