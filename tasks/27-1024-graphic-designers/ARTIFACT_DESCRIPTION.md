# Design Brief + Visual System Spec: Kindling Coffee Roasters Rebrand

## Occupation
- **SOC:** 27-1024
- **Title:** Graphic Designers

## Scenario
You are the senior designer at Field & Ore, a Portland-based brand studio. Your client is Kindling Coffee Roasters, a 7-location specialty coffee chain in the Pacific Northwest (Portland, Seattle, Eugene, Bend) preparing to launch their first Bay Area location (San Francisco's Mission District) in September 2026 and expand into direct-to-consumer bagged coffee at Whole Foods nationwide in Q1 2027. Their current identity — a woodcut-flame logo and cabin-core brown/green palette from 2017 — reads as "local roastery" in a market where they now compete with Blue Bottle, Intelligentsia, and Sey. The internal creative director, Marcus Wen, wants a rebrand that keeps the name's warmth but scales to a national shelf presence. You owe a design brief and visual system spec to the Kindling leadership team (CEO Ana Delgado, CMO Tomás Rivera) by April 30, 2026, ahead of design kickoff May 4.

## Inputs
What's on the table:
- Brand research deck from Kindling (attached earlier): customer interviews flag "feels like a hug," "the only chain where the barista remembers me," and, negatively, "I wouldn't be surprised to see them at a farmer's market in a rural town."
- Existing logo (woodcut flame + "KINDLING" serif wordmark), primary palette: #4A3A2A (bark), #6B8E4E (moss), #F3E8D3 (oat).
- Packaging constraint: DTC bag format is a 12 oz side-gusset pouch, with legally required origin/roast-date/weight zones.
- Budget/timeline signal: full system in 10 weeks, 2 rounds of revision, one in-market test at the new SF location.
- Competitor shelf reference (written description): Sey reads modernist-serif minimal-white; Onyx reads maximalist-illustrative; Blue Bottle reads monoblue iconic; Intelligentsia reads heritage-monogrammed.
- Stakeholder dynamic: CEO Delgado is attached to the flame mark (her cofounder drew the original); CMO Rivera privately thinks it needs to go.

## Artifact specification
Produce a **design brief + visual system spec** (written document, no actual art). Structure:
1. **Problem statement** — one paragraph, the design problem stated in business terms.
2. **Strategic pillars** — 3 brand attributes the system must express (e.g., "warm but not rural," "crafted but not precious," "specific but scalable"). Each pillar gets one sentence of implication.
3. **Visual territories** — 3 distinct directional territories described in words (mood, typographic register, color temperature, imagery approach, logo posture). These are the three design routes you'd present in R1.
4. **Logo evolution thesis** — handle the flame diplomatically; present options from "refine the flame" to "retire the flame" with pros/cons.
5. **Typographic system** — named faces, hierarchy (display/subhead/body/caption/micro), specimen settings. Include one ASCII specimen mockup showing hierarchy.
6. **Color system** — hex values, roles (brand/signal/neutral/supporting), accessibility notes (WCAG AA on coffee-bag shelf contexts).
7. **Packaging ASCII wireframes** — text-mockup of the 12 oz bag, one for each of the 3 territories, with annotations. Use monospace layout showing lockup placement, origin callout, mandatory-info zones.
8. **Application matrix** — one table: rows are applications (in-cafe menu, bag front, bag back, Instagram grid, cup sleeve, vehicle wrap), columns are the three territories, cells describe the treatment.
9. **Risks and trade-offs** — 3 honest risks in each direction.
10. **Recommendation** — not required, but if included, must be defended in terms of the strategic pillars.

Voice: confident, client-facing but honest. Name things. Use typographic vocabulary correctly (never "font" when "typeface" is meant; never confuse weight and style). Avoid designer clichés ("iconic," "elevated," "curated") unless you define them.

## Output format
Single markdown file. ASCII mockups in fenced code blocks using monospace box-drawing or plain ASCII. Packaging mockups should include zones labeled by letter with a legend below. Rough length: 300-550 lines including the ASCII art.

## Iteration targets
1. Make the three territories genuinely distinct — if any two could credibly share a packaging system, collapse them.
2. Diplomatically but clearly present the "retire the flame" option — don't bury the recommendation the CMO needs.
3. Tighten the ASCII bag mockups — legally required zones (net weight, origin, roast date) must be labeled and sized plausibly.
4. Replace any adjective-only brand pillar with one that has a consequence (e.g., not just "warm" but "warm, therefore we don't use all-caps display").
5. Add a WCAG contrast check against the oat neutral for at least 2 of the 3 palettes.
6. Cut the application matrix rows that don't differentiate — if all three territories resolve similarly on "vehicle wrap," drop that row.

## Success criteria
- A non-designer reader (CEO Delgado) can read this and understand what each of the three routes will feel like without seeing art.
- A designer reader (a junior on your team) could start comping R1 from this doc without asking further questions about system-level decisions.
- The flame-logo question is addressed with at least two named options and a defensible point of view.
- ASCII bag mockups include the 4-5 mandatory info zones (brand/origin/roast date/net weight/roaster) in plausible layout positions; differentiate meaningfully across the three territories.
- Typography and color specifications are complete enough to implement (named faces with weights, hex values with roles).
- The document is internally consistent — pillars connect to territories connect to type/color/packaging decisions.

## Scope target
A 15-minute v1 should include: problem statement, 3 pillars (possibly rough), 3 territories sketched at the paragraph level, 1 ASCII bag mockup (for the lead territory), partial type/color spec, and a draft position on the flame logo. Missing: the other 2 bag mockups, the full application matrix, accessibility notes, and polish on the visual-territory prose.
