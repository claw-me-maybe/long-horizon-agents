## I. Question Presented

Whether the 12-month post-employment non-competition covenant executed by Priya Narayanan on June 1, 2024, in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales and a $90,000 retention bonus, is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, to restrain her from commencing employment as "Head of Revenue" at Kettlepoint AI on April 20, 2026; and, derivatively, whether Halston Analytics is likely to obtain preliminary injunctive relief in Suffolk Superior Court against (i) continued breach of the covenant and (ii) threatened or actual misappropriation of Halston's trade secrets evidenced by the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events.

## II. Brief Answer

The covenant is likely enforceable in substantially its current form as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business, subject to two qualifications. First, the "United States and Canada" geographic scope is facially broader than Halston's commercial footprint (29 U.S. states and one Canadian province over the preceding 24 months) and presents a substantial reformation risk, but Massachusetts courts strongly prefer blue-penciling to voiding, *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773, 779 (1974), so the likeliest outcome is narrowing rather than denial. Second, the 40% garden-leave figure in § 3.1 falls below the 50% statutory default of § 24L(b)(vii) and must be justified as "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration"; this is the covenant's most textually exposed element, and a protective top-up payment before any PI hearing is advisable.

The stronger and faster near-term injunctive theory rests on trade-secret misappropriation under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq. ("DTSA"), and the Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G ("MUTSA"), grounded in the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events to directories labeled "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning." The recommended procedural path is a combined preliminary-injunction motion in Suffolk Superior Court that pairs covenant enforcement (with a blue-pencil fallback) with parallel DTSA and MUTSA claims, so that the court has two independent paths to relief.

## III. Statement of Facts

Halston Analytics, Inc. ("Halston") is a Delaware-incorporated B2B demand-forecasting and revenue-intelligence software company headquartered in Cambridge, Massachusetts. Priya Narayanan joined Halston on February 1, 2022, as Senior Director, Enterprise Sales. On June 1, 2024, Halston promoted her to Vice President of Enterprise Sales; her base salary increased from $215,000 to $285,000 (and to $295,000 effective January 1, 2026), and she received a one-time $90,000 retention bonus. The non-competition agreement at issue (the "Covenant") was executed on June 1, 2024. The offer letter attaching the Covenant was delivered on May 17, 2024, and Narayanan signed ten business days later.

The Covenant's operative provisions read:

> **§ 2.1 — Restricted Period.** For a period of twelve (12) months following termination of Employee's employment for any reason, Employee shall not, directly or indirectly, engage in, own, manage, operate, consult for, or be employed by any Competing Business within the Restricted Territory.
>
> **§ 2.2 — Competing Business.** "Competing Business" means any business that offers demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers.
>
> **§ 2.3 — Restricted Territory.** "Restricted Territory" means the United States and Canada.
>
> **§ 3.1 — Garden Leave Consideration.** During the Restricted Period, the Company shall pay Employee, on a monthly basis, an amount equal to forty percent (40%) of Employee's highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination.
>
> **§ 7.2 — Choice of Law / Forum.** Massachusetts law; exclusive jurisdiction in Suffolk County Superior Court or the U.S. District Court for the District of Massachusetts.

Schedule A to the Covenant identifies as Halston trade secrets: customer pipeline reports (current-quarter and forward-looking); standalone-selling-price ("SSP") allocation models; the win/loss database and competitive-intelligence files; and the proprietary "ForecastIQ" algorithm parameters and training datasets.

Narayanan resigned on April 3, 2026, providing ten business days' notice; her last day was April 17, 2026. Kettlepoint AI, a direct competitor in the retail-demand-forecasting vertical, announced her hire by press release on April 15, 2026; her Kettlepoint start date is April 20, 2026. Throughout her Halston tenure Narayanan was a Massachusetts resident; she worked three days per week from Halston's Cambridge office and two days per week remotely from her home in Providence, Rhode Island. Her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record.

On April 7, 2026, Halston's in-house IT Security team, acting under in-house-counsel direction, forensically imaged the laptop Narayanan returned at exit. The imaging identified two USB-copy events on March 28, 2026 — six days before her resignation — to directories named "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning." The USB device serial numbers do not correspond to any Halston-issued device. Two former direct reports have stated that Narayanan convened three "pipeline strategy" sessions in February and March 2026 focused on the retail-vertical accounts now targeted by Kettlepoint.

## IV. Discussion

The analysis proceeds in three ordered steps. Step 1 tests the Covenant against the threshold validity requirements of § 24L — without which the Covenant is unenforceable as written, though reformation may rescue it. Step 2 applies the overlay of common-law reasonableness under *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635 (2004), and *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280 (1974); § 24L did not displace this inquiry. Step 3 analyzes preliminary-injunction viability under *Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609 (1980), with a standalone trade-secret theory under DTSA and MUTSA.

### A. Statutory Validity Under G.L. c. 149, § 24L

**1. Timing and notice — § 24L(b)(i).** Section 24L(b)(i) requires that the agreement be provided "in writing to the employee" at least ten business days before the effective date or the commencement of employment, whichever is earlier, and that it expressly state the right to consult counsel. Halston delivered the offer letter attaching the Covenant on May 17, 2024, and Narayanan signed on June 1, 2024 — precisely ten business days later. Two confirmations remain. First, the 5/17/2024 cover letter must identify the Covenant as a material term of the promotion; a covenant slipped into a promotion package without affirmative disclosure can fail § 24L(b)(i). Second, the Covenant should contain (or be accompanied by a communication containing) the statutory right-to-counsel notice. *Subject to those two confirmations, the notice requirement is satisfied.*

**2. Independent consideration for a mid-employment agreement — § 24L(b)(ii).** The Covenant was executed in 2024, more than two years into Narayanan's employment, triggering § 24L(b)(ii)'s requirement of "fair and reasonable consideration independent of the continuation of employment." The $90,000 retention bonus alone discharges that requirement; it is non-trivial, identifiable, and bargained-for. The promotion from Senior Director to VP, with a $70,000 base-salary increase and enlarged strategic responsibilities, reinforces the conclusion. Pre-MNAA authority, *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797, 809–11 (2020), reached an analogous result under the common-law "material change" doctrine. *Automile* is not controlling for post-10/1/2018 agreements because § 24L(b)(ii) codified and supplanted the common-law test with an explicit statutory standard; *Automile*'s reasoning is therefore informative but not dispositive, and the correct citation practice is to ground the conclusion in § 24L(b)(ii) with *Automile* as supporting rather than as controlling authority. *This element is satisfied.*

**3. Garden-leave or "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" — § 24L(b)(vii).** Section 24L(b)(vii) provides in substance that a covenant "shall be supported by a garden leave clause or other mutually-agreed-upon consideration." The garden-leave default requires payment of at least 50% of the employee's highest annualized base salary over the two years preceding termination, paid on a pro-rata basis during the restricted period. The Covenant's 40% figure is below the statutory default and therefore cannot qualify as compliant garden leave in the statute's technical sense.

Halston's position must be that the 40% payment, coupled with the $90,000 retention bonus and the promotion itself, constitutes "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration." Four observations bear on that position:

(i) The statute's disjunctive structure ("garden leave clause or other mutually-agreed-upon consideration") plainly contemplates a category distinct from pure garden leave; otherwise the "or other" clause would be surplusage.

(ii) The 24-month lookback window used in § 3.1 tracks the statutory formulation and is not a source of vulnerability.

(iii) Post-MNAA caselaw is thin; no reported decision has interpreted "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" in a manner that squarely answers whether a 40%-of-salary monthly payment, combined with upfront bonus, qualifies. This is a genuine interpretive question.

(iv) The risk of a court reading the 40% figure as a non-compliant attempted garden leave — rather than as "other" consideration — is real and asymmetric: if the court reads § 3.1 as attempted garden leave, the Covenant fails § 24L(b)(vii) absent reformation. A protective top-up payment raising § 3.1 to 50% (either via contract amendment or via unilateral undertaking in advance of the PI hearing) would eliminate this risk. A parallel undertaking in a protective declaration would be inexpensive insurance at trivial risk-adjusted cost.

*This element presents a substantial risk that should be mitigated by a top-up payment or a supplemental protective undertaking before any PI filing.*

**4. Duration — § 24L(b)(iv).** The 12-month Restricted Period tracks the statutory cap. G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)(iv). The cap sets a ceiling, not a safe harbor; reasonableness as applied remains a separate question under *Boulanger*. For Halston's sales cycle — typical enterprise SaaS cycles of 6–9 months, with forecast-model refresh cadences running roughly quarterly — 12 months is within the reasonable band. *This element is satisfied.*

**5. Geographic scope — § 24L(b)(v).** "The United States and Canada" is broader than Halston's actual commercial footprint: client revenue data confirms bookings in only 29 U.S. states and one Canadian province (Ontario) over the preceding 24 months. Section 24L(b)(v) requires the geographic reach to be "reasonable in relation to the interests protected." Halston has no colorable legitimate interest in jurisdictions where it has not operated. The formulation is therefore facially overbroad.

The remaining question is the consequence. Massachusetts courts have long preferred reformation to voiding. *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773, 779 (1974), authorizes courts to enforce a covenant "to the extent that [it is] reasonable," and post-MNAA practice has continued that approach. The reasonable reformed scope is the set of U.S. states and Canadian provinces in which Halston has booked revenue during the 24 months preceding termination (or, alternatively, in which it has targeted named accounts for Q2 2026). A reformed scope of that nature is likely to be enforced; the Covenant will not be voided wholesale. *This element presents a substantial reformation risk but is unlikely to be fatal.*

**6. Activity scope — § 24L(b)(vi).** "Demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers" (§ 2.2) is narrow and closely tied to Halston's actual operations. It does not extend to healthcare, financial services, or other verticals unrelated to Halston's book. Kettlepoint's public positioning as a retail-demand-forecasting provider places it squarely within the defined Competing Business. *This element is satisfied.*

**7. Choice-of-law and forum — § 24L(e); *Oxford Global*.** Section 24L(e) provides that the Act applies to any employee who is a resident of Massachusetts or who has been employed in Massachusetts for at least 30 days immediately preceding termination, and renders unenforceable a choice-of-law provision that would deprive such an employee of the Act's protections. *Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462, 473–75 (2018), confirms that a Massachusetts choice-of-law clause cannot circumvent more employee-protective law of the employee's state of primary residence or employment.

Narayanan's Cambridge office of record, her three-days-per-week Cambridge presence, her Massachusetts residency, and her listing of Cambridge on personnel records all support MNAA coverage. The two-days-per-week Providence remote-work pattern is a factor on the other side but is unlikely to be dispositive given the weight of the Cambridge-anchored facts. The evidentiary record for the 30-day lookback should be documented prior to filing using (a) badge-swipe records, (b) VPN logs, (c) timesheets or time-tracking records, and (d) expense reports for travel between Providence and Cambridge. Narayanan's $295,000 base salary exempts her from Rhode Island's low-wage non-compete prohibition under R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-59-3(a), so the Rhode Island statute would be materially less favorable to her than the MNAA in any event, reducing the stakes of the primary-employment question. *This element presents a manageable risk that should be closed out with factual documentation before filing.*

### B. Common-Law Reasonableness: *Boulanger* and *Marine Contractors*

Section 24L does not displace the common-law reasonableness inquiry; a covenant that passes the statutory thresholds must still be reasonable under *Boulanger*. The two analyses are sequential, not interchangeable: validity first, reasonableness second.

**Legitimate business interest.** *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280, 287 (1974), recognizes three protectable categories — trade secrets, other confidential business information, and employer goodwill — and excludes protection from ordinary competition. Halston's interest falls squarely within all three categories. Narayanan had access to the ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and training datasets, SSP allocation models, the win/loss database, and forward-looking pipeline reports (all enumerated on Schedule A to the Covenant). She was the enterprise-sales face for the three named accounts at issue (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech) over a sustained multi-year period, generating personal goodwill classically protectable under *Marine Contractors*. *The legitimate-business-interest prong is satisfied.*

**Time and space.** Time: 12 months aligns with typical enterprise-sales cycles and forecast-model refresh cadences and is reasonable as applied. Space: as discussed, "United States and Canada" requires reformation to Halston's actual operational footprint. *Time is reasonable; space requires blue-penciling.*

**Public interest.** Massachusetts recognizes a dual public interest in reasonable covenant enforcement and labor mobility, which the MNAA codifies. A reformed injunction coextensive with Halston's actual operations, paired with a clearly scoped trade-secret prohibition, serves both prongs of that interest. *Consonance with the public interest is established.*

### C. Preliminary Injunction Under *Packaging Industries*

Under *Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609, 617 (1980), preliminary injunctive relief in Massachusetts requires (1) likelihood of success on the merits, (2) substantial risk of irreparable harm absent relief, (3) balance of harms favoring the movant, and (4) consonance with the public interest. The test is applied on a sliding scale: a stronger showing on likelihood of success can tolerate a somewhat weaker showing on the other factors. *Packaging Indus.*, 380 Mass. at 617.

**1. Likelihood of success.** Two independent theories support the likelihood showing. On the Covenant theory, the Step-1 and Step-2 analyses above predict enforcement in narrowed form: the principal uncertainties are (i) the § 24L(b)(vii) garden-leave characterization (mitigable by a top-up payment) and (ii) the geographic-scope reformation mechanics (substantial risk but not fatal, given *All Stainless*). On the trade-secret theory (Section IV.D below), the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events to "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" provide direct evidence supporting misappropriation or, at minimum, threatened misappropriation. In combination, the likelihood showing is strong.

**2. Irreparable harm.** The irreparable-harm analysis must distinguish two distinct harms, a point often blurred in covenant-plus-trade-secret PI motions.

*Covenant-based harm.* If Narayanan begins selling at Kettlepoint against Halston for the three named accounts (Brinkerhoff, Meridian, Quellstream), Halston loses customer goodwill that is difficult to reconstruct ex post and not readily quantifiable in damages. The Q2 forecast assumption that these three accounts do not churn in the next 90 days is the specific factual anchor for the harm.

*Trade-secret harm.* Disclosure or use of the ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and training datasets, and the forward-looking pipeline reports, cannot be undone once disclosed and operates wholly independently of the Covenant. This harm is sufficient to support injunctive relief standing alone.

The two-track structure matters procedurally: if the court declines to enforce the Covenant (or narrows it substantially), the trade-secret harm still supports an injunction limited to the specific files and their derivative use.

**3. Balance of harms.** Narayanan's burden is deferral of the Kettlepoint role, or narrow scope restrictions within it; her economic downside is mitigated by the § 3.1 garden-leave payment. Halston faces potentially permanent loss of customer relationships and trade-secret value. A narrowly tailored injunction — confining Narayanan's Kettlepoint role to non-retail-forecasting work and requiring return and forensic remediation of the "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" materials — imposes limited burden while preventing substantial harm. *The balance favors Halston.*

**4. Public interest.** As noted above, a narrowly tailored injunction honors the dual interests the MNAA reflects. *The public-interest factor is satisfied.*

*The four factors, taken together, favor preliminary relief, particularly if the motion is structured to permit — indeed invite — narrowing by the court.*

### D. Parallel Trade-Secret Theory: DTSA and MUTSA

The March 28, 2026 USB-copy events provide the factual predicate for a freestanding misappropriation claim under DTSA, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq., and MUTSA, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G. The two statutes substantially align; MUTSA is Massachusetts's 2018 codification of the Uniform Trade Secrets Act, enacted simultaneously with § 24L. The three elements tracked below apply to both.

**1. Trade-secret status — 18 U.S.C. § 1839(3); G.L. c. 93, § 42.** Information qualifies as a trade secret if (i) it derives independent economic value from not being generally known or readily ascertainable, and (ii) the owner has taken "reasonable measures" to protect its secrecy. The contents of "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning," by their labels and the personnel who had access, presumptively qualify: Halston's forward-looking pipeline information drives its sales strategy and is not publicly available, and ForecastIQ's algorithm parameters and training data are the product of substantial investment. Halston's reasonable measures include the Schedule A identification, the Covenant's confidentiality provisions, access controls on the relevant systems, and the forensic-imaging protocol used at exit. Definitive characterization will require review of the copied content once recovered, but the presumption is strong.

**2. Misappropriation — 18 U.S.C. § 1839(5); G.L. c. 93, § 42.** Copying to a non-company USB device, six days before resignation, of directories labeled to match the retail-vertical accounts and algorithm at issue, supports a strong inference of misappropriation — either acquisition by improper means or use/disclosure of information acquired by improper means. The proximity to resignation, the non-company device, and the topical match to Kettlepoint's target accounts combine to shift the inferential weight to Halston.

**3. Remedies.** DTSA and MUTSA both authorize injunctive relief. 18 U.S.C. § 1836(b)(3)(A); G.L. c. 93, § 42A. DTSA additionally authorizes an ex parte civil seizure under "extraordinary circumstances," 18 U.S.C. § 1836(b)(2), but the seizure remedy is rarely granted and is likely unavailable here: there is no evidence Narayanan will evade process, the location of the USB devices is unknown, and a conventional PI with an expedited return-and-imaging order will suffice. The trade-secret theory also supports an independent damages claim not dependent on the Covenant.

The trade-secret theory is viable independent of the Covenant and, given the forensic findings, is the single strongest near-term ground for injunctive relief.

## V. Recommendation

Three options, ranked by expected value to the client:

**1. Combined PI motion — covenant enforcement plus DTSA/MUTSA claims (recommended).** File in Suffolk Superior Court seeking enforcement of the Covenant as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business with blue-pencil fallback on geographic scope, combined with DTSA and MUTSA claims seeking (i) return and forensic remediation of the "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" materials and (ii) an injunction against use or disclosure. Before filing, execute a protective top-up payment raising § 3.1 to 50% (or a supplemental undertaking to the same effect) to eliminate the § 24L(b)(vii) risk. *Probability of meaningful relief: ~65–75%.* *Estimated fees: $75K–$90K through hearing.* *Client-relationship impact: assertive but measured.*

**2. Trade-secret-only TRO/PI.** File a narrower TRO grounded solely in DTSA and MUTSA, deferring the Covenant question for later plenary briefing. This avoids the geographic-scope and garden-leave fights at the PI stage and takes the partner's preferred use of the forensic findings at their strongest. *Probability of TRO entry: high.* *Scope of relief: narrower — barring use of the copied materials and requiring forensic remediation.* *Estimated fees: $45K–$55K.* *Recommended if the partner prefers a cleaner record and narrower relief.*

**3. Negotiated standstill toward a customer-specific non-solicit.** Open counsel-to-counsel dialogue with Kettlepoint, proposing a narrower customer-specific non-solicit covering Brinkerhoff, Meridian, and Quellstream for 90–180 days, a modest forensic-review protocol, and a mutual release. *Probability of success: moderate, contingent on Kettlepoint's posture.* *Estimated fees: $15K–$25K.* *Recommended if litigation cost exceeds protected value.*

**4. Stand down and monitor.** Defer filing; monitor Narayanan's conduct and Kettlepoint's positioning in the three named accounts; reserve PI and trade-secret claims for a later and better-documented record. This preserves the claims but cedes the injunctive timing advantage and risks loss of Q2 customer relationships. *Recommended only if further factual development casts substantial doubt on § 24L(e) coverage or on the forensic findings.*
