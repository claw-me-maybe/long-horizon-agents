## I. Question Presented

Whether the 12-month post-employment non-competition covenant executed by Priya Narayanan on June 1, 2024, in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales and a $90,000 retention bonus, is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, to restrain her from commencing employment as "Head of Revenue" at Kettlepoint AI on April 20, 2026, and, derivatively, whether Halston Analytics is likely to obtain preliminary injunctive relief in Suffolk Superior Court against (i) continued breach of the covenant and (ii) threatened or actual misappropriation of Halston's trade secrets evidenced by the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events.

## II. Brief Answer

The 12-month covenant is likely enforceable in substantially its current form as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business, though the "United States and Canada" geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk and the 40% garden-leave figure requires further factual development to confirm it qualifies as "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" under § 24L(b)(vii). The stronger and faster near-term injunctive theory rests on trade-secret misappropriation under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq., and the Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G, given the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events to directories labeled "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning."

The recommended procedural path is a combined preliminary-injunction motion pursued in Suffolk Superior Court, pairing covenant enforcement (with a blue-pencil fallback under *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974)) with parallel DTSA and MUTSA claims. A covenant-only theory faces more reformation uncertainty than a trade-secret theory; the trade-secret theory is cleaner and, on its own, likely justifies a narrowly tailored TRO.

## III. Statement of Facts

Halston Analytics, Inc. ("Halston") is a Delaware-incorporated B2B demand-forecasting and revenue-intelligence software company headquartered in Cambridge, Massachusetts. Priya Narayanan joined Halston on February 1, 2022, as Senior Director, Enterprise Sales. On June 1, 2024, she was promoted to Vice President of Enterprise Sales; her base salary increased from $215,000 to $285,000 (and later to $295,000 effective January 1, 2026), and she received a $90,000 retention bonus. The non-competition agreement at issue (the "Covenant") was executed on June 1, 2024. The offer letter attaching the Covenant was delivered on May 17, 2024, and the Covenant was signed ten business days later on June 1, 2024.

The Covenant includes the following operative provisions:

> **§ 2.1 — Restricted Period.** For a period of twelve (12) months following termination of Employee's employment for any reason, Employee shall not, directly or indirectly, engage in, own, manage, operate, consult for, or be employed by any Competing Business within the Restricted Territory.
>
> **§ 2.2 — Competing Business.** "Competing Business" means any business that offers demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers.
>
> **§ 2.3 — Restricted Territory.** "Restricted Territory" means the United States and Canada.
>
> **§ 3.1 — Garden Leave Consideration.** During the Restricted Period, the Company shall pay Employee, on a monthly basis, an amount equal to forty percent (40%) of Employee's highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination.
>
> **§ 7.2 — Choice of Law / Forum.** Massachusetts law; exclusive jurisdiction in Suffolk County Superior Court or the District of Massachusetts.

Schedule A to the Covenant identifies the following as Halston trade secrets: customer pipeline reports (current-quarter and forward-looking); standalone-selling-price ("SSP") allocation models; the win/loss database and competitive-intelligence files; and the proprietary "ForecastIQ" algorithm parameters and training datasets.

Narayanan resigned on April 3, 2026, providing ten business days' notice; her last day was April 17, 2026. Kettlepoint AI, a direct competitor in the retail-demand-forecasting vertical, announced her hire by press release on April 15, 2026, with a start date of April 20, 2026.

At all relevant times Narayanan was a Massachusetts resident; she worked three days per week out of Halston's Cambridge office and two days per week remotely from her home in Providence, Rhode Island. Her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record.

On April 7, 2026, Halston's in-house IT Security team, acting under in-house-counsel direction, forensically imaged the laptop Narayanan returned at exit. The imaging disclosed two USB-copy events dated March 28, 2026 — six days before her resignation — to directories named "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning." The USB device serial numbers do not correspond to any Halston-issued device. Two former direct reports have stated that Narayanan convened three "pipeline strategy" sessions in February and March 2026 focused on retail-vertical accounts now targeted by Kettlepoint.

## IV. Discussion

The analysis proceeds in three steps. First, the Covenant must satisfy the threshold statutory validity requirements of § 24L. Second, an agreement that is statutorily valid must still be reasonable under the common-law framework of *Boulanger* and *Marine Contractors* — the statute does not displace the pre-existing reasonableness inquiry. Third, preliminary injunctive relief is analyzed independently under *Packaging Industries*, with a parallel DTSA/MUTSA theory available whether or not the Covenant is enforced.

### A. Statutory Validity Under § 24L

**1. Timing and notice — § 24L(b)(i).** The statute requires that the agreement be provided to the employee at least ten business days before the effective date or before acceptance, whichever is earlier. Halston delivered the offer letter attaching the Covenant on May 17, 2024, and Narayanan signed on June 1, 2024 — exactly ten business days later. Assuming the offer letter identified the Covenant as a material term of the promotion (to be confirmed from the 5/17/2024 cover letter), notice is satisfied. *This element is satisfied.*

**2. Independent consideration for a mid-employment agreement — § 24L(b)(ii).** Because the Covenant was executed in 2024, more than two years into Narayanan's employment, § 24L(b)(ii) requires "fair and reasonable consideration independent of the continuation of employment." The $90,000 retention bonus alone is textbook independent consideration. It is paired with a promotion from Senior Director to VP, a $70,000 base-salary increase, and the enlargement of Narayanan's role to cover enterprise sales strategy — all bargained for in the same June 2024 package. *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797 (2020), applied the pre-MNAA common-law "material change" doctrine to conclude that promotions and related benefits can supply consideration for new covenants. *Automile* is not controlling for post-10/1/2018 agreements because § 24L(b)(ii) replaces the common-law test with an explicit statutory formulation, but its reasoning is suggestive and reinforces the conclusion. *This element is satisfied.*

**3. Garden-leave or "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" — § 24L(b)(vii).** Section 3.1 provides 40% of the highest annualized base salary during the two years preceding termination, paid monthly. The statutory default is garden-leave pay of at least 50% of the employee's highest annualized base salary during the two years preceding termination, paid on a pro-rata basis during the restricted period, "or other mutually-agreed-upon consideration." G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)(vii). The 40% figure is below the 50% default and therefore cannot qualify as garden-leave in the statute's technical sense. Halston's position must be that the 40% payment, coupled with the $90,000 retention bonus and the promotion itself, together constitute "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration." That is a colorable but untested position; post-MNAA caselaw gives limited guidance on what qualifies as "other" consideration, and the risk of a court reading the 40% monthly payment as a non-compliant attempt at garden-leave (rather than as "other" consideration) is real. *This element presents a substantial risk and should be the focus of further analysis and, if possible, a supplemental top-up payment before any PI hearing.*

The 24-month lookback used in § 3.1 tracks the statutory formulation and requires no further analysis.

**4. Duration — § 24L(b)(iv).** The 12-month duration is at the statutory cap and per se permissible. G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)(iv). *This element is satisfied.*

**5. Geographic scope — § 24L(b)(v).** The statute requires the geographic scope to be "reasonable in relation to the interests protected." Halston's client data confirms booked revenue in only 29 U.S. states and one Canadian province (Ontario) over the preceding 24 months. The "United States and Canada" formulation is facially broader than Halston's actual commercial footprint and therefore facially overbroad. However, Massachusetts courts prefer reformation ("blue-penciling") to voiding under *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974). A reformed scope limited to "U.S. states and Canadian provinces in which Halston has booked revenue during the 24 months preceding termination" is likely to be enforced. *This element presents a substantial reformation risk but is unlikely to be fatal.*

**6. Activity scope — § 24L(b)(vi).** "Demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers" (§ 2.2) is narrow and tied directly to the business line Halston actually operates. It does not capture, for example, healthcare or financial-services verticals. Kettlepoint's public positioning in retail demand forecasting places it squarely within the defined Competing Business. *This element is satisfied.*

**7. Choice-of-law and choice-of-forum — § 24L(e); *Oxford Global*.** Section 24L(e) renders a choice-of-law provision unenforceable if it would deprive a Massachusetts resident or Massachusetts-employed individual of the protections of the Act; the statute looks to whether the employee, during the 30 days preceding termination, was "primarily resident" or "primarily employed" in Massachusetts. Narayanan's Cambridge office of record, her Massachusetts residency (confirmed by her home address on her personnel file), and her three-days-per-week Cambridge presence all support the position that she is within the Act's coverage. The two-days-per-week Providence remote-work pattern is not negligible, however, and the 30-day lookback should be documented with timesheet, badge-swipe, and expense-report data before filing. *Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462 (2018), underscores that a Massachusetts choice-of-law clause cannot circumvent the more employee-protective law of a bona fide other state of residence or employment. Rhode Island's R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-59-1 et seq. is materially more restrictive than the MNAA (it generally bars non-competes against low-wage workers, imposes stricter consideration rules, and narrows protectable interests). Narayanan's $295,000 base salary likely exempts her from the low-wage prohibition, but the statute's other restrictions could apply if Rhode Island were treated as her state of primary employment. *This element presents a non-trivial risk that can likely be neutralized with factual documentation, but warrants confirmation before PI filing.*

### B. Common-Law Reasonableness: *Boulanger* and *Marine Contractors*

Satisfying § 24L is necessary but not sufficient; the Covenant must also be reasonable under the *Boulanger* framework (legitimate business interest, reasonable in time and space, consonant with the public interest). *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635 (2004).

**Legitimate business interest.** *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280 (1974), recognizes trade secrets, other confidential business information, and employer goodwill — but not protection from ordinary competition — as protectable. Halston has strong claims on all three prongs. Narayanan had access to the ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and training datasets, SSP allocation models, the win/loss database, and the forward-looking customer pipeline reports — each identified on Schedule A. She held sustained customer-facing responsibility for the three named accounts (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech), generating personal goodwill classically protectable under *Marine Contractors*. *This element is satisfied.*

**Time and space.** Twelve months tracks ordinary enterprise-sales cycles and forecast-model refresh cadences; as discussed above, the geographic scope requires reformation. *Time is reasonable; space requires blue-penciling.*

**Public interest.** Massachusetts recognizes competing interests in reasonable covenant enforcement and labor mobility; a narrowed injunction aligned with Halston's actual commercial footprint honors both.

### C. Preliminary Injunction Under *Packaging Industries*

Under *Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609, 617 (1980), a preliminary injunction requires (1) likelihood of success on the merits, (2) irreparable harm, (3) balance of harms favoring the movant, and (4) consonance with the public interest.

**1. Likelihood of success.** Two independent theories support the merits showing. On the covenant theory, the analysis above suggests enforcement in narrowed form is likely; the principal uncertainties are the garden-leave characterization and the reformation mechanics. On the trade-secret theory (addressed below), the March 28 USB-copy events to "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" provide direct evidence of misappropriation or, at minimum, threatened misappropriation. The combined likelihood is substantial.

**2. Irreparable harm.** The analysis must distinguish two distinct harms, which is often blurred in covenant-plus-trade-secret PI motions. First, *covenant-based harm*: loss of customer goodwill if Narayanan sells against Halston at Kettlepoint, which is difficult to quantify ex post. Second, *trade-secret harm*: disclosure or use of the ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and forward-looking pipeline reports, which cannot be undone once disclosed and which bear on the three named accounts (Brinkerhoff, Meridian, Quellstream) and the Q2 forecast. The trade-secret harm is independently sufficient to support a narrowly tailored injunction and does not depend on the Covenant's enforceability.

**3. Balance of harms.** Narayanan faces deferral of her Kettlepoint role; Halston faces permanent loss of customer relationships and trade-secret value. A narrowly tailored injunction — preventing Narayanan from working on retail-forecasting accounts at Kettlepoint, or requiring her return of the "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" materials — imposes limited burden while preventing substantial harm. The balance favors Halston.

**4. Public interest.** A narrowly tailored injunction reflecting Massachusetts's dual interest in reasonable covenant enforcement and labor mobility, paired with a clear trade-secret prohibition, serves the public interest.

*The four factors, taken together, favor preliminary relief, particularly if the motion is structured to permit narrowing.*

### D. Parallel Trade-Secret Theory — DTSA and MUTSA

The March 28, 2026 USB-copy events provide a factual predicate for a freestanding misappropriation claim under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq., and the Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G.

**Trade-secret status.** The contents of "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" presumptively qualify as trade secrets: they derive independent economic value from not being generally known, and Halston has taken reasonable measures to protect them (access controls, the Schedule A identification, the confidentiality provisions of the Covenant). Confirmation will require review of the copied content once recovered.

**Misappropriation.** Copying to a non-company USB device six days before resignation, without authorization and in directories specific to the accounts and algorithm at issue, supports a strong inference of misappropriation. 18 U.S.C. § 1839(5); G.L. c. 93, § 42.

**Remedies and seizure.** DTSA authorizes injunctive relief and, in "extraordinary circumstances," ex parte civil seizure under 18 U.S.C. § 1836(b)(2). The seizure remedy is rarely granted and likely overreaches on these facts (no evidence Narayanan will evade process; the USB devices are at present unknown in location); the better path is a conventional preliminary injunction with an expedited return order.

The trade-secret theory is viable independent of the Covenant and is the firm's single strongest near-term ground for injunctive relief.

## V. Recommendation

The partner should consider three actionable options, ranked by expected value:

**1. Combined PI motion — covenant enforcement plus DTSA/MUTSA claims (recommended).** File in Suffolk Superior Court, seeking enforcement of the Covenant as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business, with blue-pencil fallback on geographic scope, plus DTSA and MUTSA claims seeking return of the "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" materials and an injunction against use or disclosure. *Probability of meaningful relief: high (roughly 65–75%).* *Estimated cost: $75K–$90K through hearing.* *Client-relationship impact: assertive but measured.*

**2. Trade-secret-only TRO/PI.** File a narrower TRO grounded solely in DTSA/MUTSA, deferring the covenant question. *Probability of TRO entry: high.* *Scope of relief: narrower — likely barring use of the copied materials and requiring forensic remediation.* *Estimated cost: $45K–$55K.* *Recommended if the partner prefers to avoid the reformation fight at the PI stage.*

**3. Negotiated standstill toward a customer-specific non-solicit.** Open dialogue with Kettlepoint counsel, proposing a narrower non-solicit focused on Brinkerhoff, Meridian, and Quellstream, with a modest forensic-review protocol and a mutual release. *Probability of success: moderate, depending on Kettlepoint's posture.* *Estimated cost: $15K–$25K.* *Recommended if litigation cost exceeds protected value.*
