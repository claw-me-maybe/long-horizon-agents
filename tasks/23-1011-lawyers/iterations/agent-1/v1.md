## I. Question Presented

Whether the 12-month post-employment non-competition covenant signed by Priya Narayanan on June 1, 2024, in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales and a $90,000 retention bonus, is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, against her acceptance of the "Head of Revenue" role at Kettlepoint AI, and whether Halston Analytics can obtain a preliminary injunction in Suffolk Superior Court sufficient to halt her April 20, 2026 start.

## II. Brief Answer

Likely yes, in substantial part. The covenant appears to satisfy the threshold validity requirements of § 24L — the 10-business-day notice window was met (delivered 5/17/2024, signed 6/1/2024); the promotion and $90,000 retention bonus supply the independent consideration required for a mid-employment covenant; the 12-month duration is at the statutory cap but not beyond it; and the 40% garden-leave clause tracks the statutory floor. The principal vulnerability is the "United States and Canada" geographic scope, which is facially broader than Halston's actual footprint and presents a substantial reformation risk. Massachusetts courts strongly prefer blue-penciling to voiding, so the likelier outcome is a narrowed injunction rather than denial.

On the preliminary injunction question, Halston has a better-than-even chance of obtaining meaningful relief, particularly when the covenant theory is paired with a parallel trade-secret-misappropriation claim rooted in the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events. The recommended path is a combined motion — seeking enforcement of the covenant as to the retail-forecasting business, with a trade-secret-based alternative theory that can stand if the covenant is narrowed.

## III. Statement of Facts

Halston Analytics, Inc. ("Halston") is a Delaware-incorporated B2B demand-forecasting company headquartered in Cambridge, Massachusetts. Priya Narayanan was hired on February 1, 2022, as Senior Director, Enterprise Sales. On June 1, 2024, Halston promoted her to Vice President of Enterprise Sales. In connection with that promotion, her base salary was raised from $215,000 to $285,000 (subsequently to $295,000 effective January 1, 2026), and she received a one-time retention bonus of $90,000. The non-competition agreement at issue here was executed on the same date. The offer letter enclosing the covenant was delivered on May 17, 2024, and Narayanan signed the covenant on June 1, 2024 — ten business days later.

The covenant's key provisions are:

- **§ 2.1 — Restricted Period:** 12 months following termination for any reason.
- **§ 2.2 — Competing Business:** any business offering demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers.
- **§ 2.3 — Restricted Territory:** the United States and Canada.
- **§ 3.1 — Garden Leave:** 40% of Narayanan's highest annualized base salary during the two years preceding termination, paid monthly.
- **§ 7.2 — Choice of Law / Forum:** Massachusetts law; exclusive jurisdiction in Suffolk Superior Court or the District of Massachusetts.

Narayanan resigned on April 3, 2026, providing ten business days' notice; her last day was April 17, 2026. On April 15, 2026, Kettlepoint AI — a direct competitor in the retail-demand-forecasting vertical — announced via press release that Narayanan would join as "Head of Revenue" on April 20, 2026. Narayanan was a Massachusetts resident who worked from Halston's Cambridge office three days per week and from her home in Providence, Rhode Island two days per week; her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record.

On April 7, 2026, Halston's in-house IT Security team, acting under the direction of in-house counsel, forensically imaged Narayanan's returned laptop. The imaging identified two USB-copy events on March 28, 2026 — six days before her resignation — to directories named "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning." The USB device serial numbers do not correspond to any company-issued device. Two of Narayanan's former direct reports have stated that she held three "pipeline strategy" sessions in February and March 2026 focused on retail-vertical accounts that Kettlepoint now targets.

## IV. Discussion

### A. MNAA Validity (§ 24L)

Massachusetts enacted § 24L effective October 1, 2018. The statute sets threshold validity requirements; an agreement that fails any is unenforceable as written, though the statute permits reformation in some circumstances. G.L. c. 149, § 24L.

**1. Notice (§ 24L(b)(i)).** The MNAA requires that the agreement be presented to the employee at least 10 business days before the effective date of the agreement or before acceptance, whichever is earlier. G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)(i). Here, Halston delivered the offer letter attaching the non-compete on May 17, 2024, and Narayanan signed on June 1, 2024 — ten business days later. *The notice requirement is satisfied.*

**2. Independent consideration for a mid-employment agreement (§ 24L(b)(ii), (vii)).** Because the covenant was executed in 2024, after Narayanan's 2022 hire, § 24L(b)(ii) requires that it be supported either by "fair and reasonable consideration independent of the continuation of employment" or by garden-leave pay meeting the statutory floor. The $90,000 retention bonus and the promotion from Senior Director to VP, accompanied by a $70,000 base salary increase, together supply independent consideration well beyond what Massachusetts courts have historically required for mid-employment covenants. Pre-statute authority, *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797 (2020), recognized promotions as "material change" events supporting enforceability; though *Automile* applied the common-law doctrine and is not controlling for post-10/1/2018 agreements, its logic is informative. *The independent-consideration requirement is satisfied.*

**3. Garden-leave compliance (§ 24L(b)(vii)).** Section 3.1 of the covenant provides 40% of Narayanan's highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination, paid monthly. That tracks the statutory formula (at least 50% of highest annualized base salary paid on a pro-rata basis during the restricted period, unless the parties agree to "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration"). The 40% figure is below the 50% default but may qualify as "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" if the promotion bonus and mid-employment benefits are treated as part of the bundle. This is the element on which the covenant is most textually exposed; a court applying the statute strictly could read 40% as below the floor. *The garden-leave element presents a close question and warrants further briefing.*

**4. Duration (§ 24L(b)(iv)).** Twelve months is the statutory cap and is per se permitted. Whether the full 12 months is reasonable as applied depends on Halston's sales cycle and the shelf life of the confidential information at issue; given typical enterprise-sales cycles of 6–9 months and forecast-model refresh cadences, 12 months is defensible. *The duration requirement is satisfied.*

**5. Geographic scope (§ 24L(b)(v)).** "The United States and Canada" is facially broad. Client data shows Halston has booked revenue in only 29 U.S. states and one Canadian province over the preceding 24 months. A covenant's geographic scope must be "no broader than necessary to protect the employer's legitimate business interest." *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635 (2004). The "United States and Canada" formulation is likely to be challenged as overbroad. Massachusetts courts historically prefer reformation to voiding, *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974), and a court is likely to narrow this to jurisdictions in which Halston has booked revenue. *This element presents a substantial reformation risk.*

**6. Legitimate business interest (*Marine Contractors*).** Even if the covenant satisfies § 24L, it must rest on a legitimate business interest: trade secrets, other confidential information, or employer goodwill. *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280 (1974). Narayanan had access to Halston's customer pipeline reports, SSP allocation models, win/loss database, and the "ForecastIQ" algorithm's parameters and training data — all of which are enumerated on Schedule A to the covenant. She was the enterprise-sales face for at least three named accounts (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech), cultivating goodwill that is classically protectable. *The legitimate-business-interest requirement is satisfied.*

### B. Preliminary Injunction Under *Packaging Industries*

Massachusetts applies a four-factor test for preliminary injunctive relief: likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of harms, and public interest. *Packaging Indus. Grp., Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609, 617 (1980).

**1. Likelihood of success.** For the reasons above, Halston is likely to succeed on the merits of a covenant-enforcement claim in narrowed form, and has an additional independent theory under DTSA and MUTSA stemming from the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events. The parallel trade-secret theory strengthens the overall likelihood showing.

**2. Irreparable harm.** Halston has pled facts suggesting two distinct harms: (a) the loss of customer relationships and confidential pipeline information if Narayanan immediately sells against Halston at Kettlepoint; and (b) the risk of disclosure or use of the contents of the "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" directories. The second harm is independent of the covenant and is particularly difficult to redress through damages — misappropriated algorithm parameters cannot be un-disclosed.

**3. Balance of harms.** Narayanan faces loss of the Kettlepoint role for up to 12 months; Halston faces potentially permanent loss of customer relationships and trade-secret value. The balance tips toward Halston, particularly if the relief is narrowed in scope.

**4. Public interest.** Massachusetts has articulated a public interest both in reasonable covenant enforcement and in labor mobility. A narrowed injunction limited to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business would honor both interests.

*The four factors, taken together, favor preliminary relief, though likely in narrowed form.*

## V. Recommendation

In ranked order, the firm has three primary options:

**1. Combined PI motion: covenant enforcement plus trade-secret claim.** File a motion for preliminary injunction in Suffolk Superior Court enforcing the covenant as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business (with blue-pencil fallback to the geographic scope), and include parallel DTSA and MUTSA claims grounded in the March 28 USB-copy events. This gives the court two paths to relief. Estimated cost: $75,000–$90,000 through hearing. *Highest probability of meaningful relief.*

**2. Narrow TRO on trade-secret theory only.** File a narrower TRO grounded solely in DTSA/MUTSA misappropriation, leaving the covenant question for later plenary briefing. This is lower-risk procedurally — it does not require the court to reach the geographic-scope reformation question — but provides narrower relief. Estimated cost: $45,000–$55,000. *Recommended if the partner wishes to avoid the geographic-scope fight at the PI stage.*

**3. Negotiated standstill toward a customer-specific non-solicit.** Open a dialogue with Kettlepoint counsel proposing a narrower, customer-specific non-solicit covering the three named accounts, with a limited trade-secret forensic review. Lowest cost, lowest risk, but provides no judicial mechanism. *Recommended only if partner determines that litigation cost exceeds the protected value.*
