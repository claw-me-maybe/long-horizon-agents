## I. Question Presented

Whether the 12-month non-competition covenant Priya Narayanan executed on June 1, 2024 in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales and a $90,000 retention bonus is (a) statutorily valid under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, (b) reasonable under the *Boulanger*/*Marine Contractors* common-law framework that operates on top of the statute, and (c) a proper basis for a preliminary injunction under *Packaging Industries* restraining Narayanan from commencing a Head of Revenue role at Kettlepoint AI, and separately whether the March 28, 2026 USB-copy forensic findings support injunctive relief under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1836, and the Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G, independent of the covenant.

## II. Brief Answer

The 12-month covenant is likely enforceable in substantially its current form as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business, though the "United States and Canada" geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk under § 24L(b)(v) that a Massachusetts court is likely to cure by blue-pencil reformation rather than voiding, consistent with *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974). The § 24L(b)(ii) independent-consideration requirement is comfortably satisfied by the promotion, the $70,000 base-salary increase, and the $90,000 retention bonus. The garden-leave provision at 40% falls below the 50% floor in § 24L(b)(vii) but is defensible as "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" given the retention bonus, albeit with meaningful litigation risk on the rehabilitation framing.

The stronger near-term injunctive vehicle is a trade-secret theory under DTSA and MUTSA keyed to the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events. We recommend opening with a trade-secret-based TRO, pleading the covenant claim in the same complaint, and briefing the covenant theory for the PI hearing. The § 24L(e) choice-of-law question raised by Narayanan's two-day-per-week Providence remote work is likely resolved in Halston's favor but warrants factual development of the 30-day pre-termination lookback before filing.

> **§ 2.3** *Restricted Territory.* "Restricted Territory" means the United States and Canada.

## IV. Discussion

The analysis proceeds in five parts. Part A addresses **statutory validity** under § 24L, element by element; if any threshold element fails, the covenant is unenforceable without regard to reasonableness. Part B addresses **common-law reasonableness** under *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635 (2004), and *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280 (1974), which operates on top of the statute — a covenant can be statutorily valid yet fail reasonableness, or vice versa. Part C addresses **choice-of-law enforceability** under § 24L(e) and *Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462 (2018). Part D applies the **preliminary-injunction** framework of *Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609 (1980). Part E addresses the **parallel trade-secret theory** under DTSA and MUTSA.

### A. Statutory Validity Under G.L. c. 149, § 24L

#### 1. Notice — § 24L(b)(i)

Section 24L(b)(i) requires that an MNAA-governed covenant be provided to the employee by the earlier of a formal offer or ten business days before commencement of employment, be signed by both parties, and expressly state the employee's right to consult counsel. Where the agreement is entered *after* commencement, the ten-business-day window runs from delivery to acceptance, and the statute requires — in addition — that the agreement be supported by independent consideration (addressed below). Here delivery was May 17, 2024 and execution was June 1, 2024, exactly ten business days later. We will confirm on document review that the agreement recites the right-to-consult-counsel acknowledgment and is signed by both parties.

*Holding: the notice requirement is satisfied.*

#### 2. Independent Consideration — § 24L(b)(ii)

Section 24L(b)(ii) provides that a post-commencement non-compete must be supported by "fair and reasonable consideration independent from the continuation of employment." The statute does not define the term, but the consensus reading is that discrete monetary or positional consideration, beyond the mere continuation of employment, satisfies the floor. The June 1, 2024 covenant accompanied (i) a promotion from Senior Director to Vice President, (ii) a $70,000 base-salary increase (from $215,000 to $285,000), and (iii) a $90,000 retention bonus specifically and separately paid. Each of these three elements, standing alone, is plausibly sufficient; in combination they comfortably exceed any reasonable reading of § 24L(b)(ii).

Pre-MNAA common law merits brief distinction. *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797, 805–07 (2020), applied the "material change" doctrine to hold that an absence of material change in the employment relationship can render a prior covenant unenforceable. *Automile* does not directly govern a post-October 1, 2018 agreement; § 24L(b)(ii) supplies its own, statute-specific consideration regime that operates without reference to the common-law "material change" framework. *Automile* nevertheless remains informative of the SJC's continuing concern that covenants not be treated as boilerplate carried forward unchanged through an employee's tenure; its reasoning may inform, without controlling, how a court evaluates consideration sufficiency under § 24L(b)(ii). On these facts the point is largely academic — the 2024 agreement is a fresh covenant supported by fresh consideration, so the *Automile* question does not arise in a contested form.

*Holding: the independent-consideration requirement is satisfied.*

#### 3. Garden Leave or Other Mutually-Agreed-Upon Consideration — § 24L(b)(vii)

Section 24L(b)(vii) requires that an enforceable non-compete be supported either by (a) a garden-leave clause providing at least 50% of the employee's "highest annualized base salary paid by the employer within the 2 years preceding the employee's termination," paid "on a pro rata basis during the entirety of the restricted period," or (b) "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration between the employer and the employee, provided that such consideration is specified in the noncompetition agreement." The statute disjunctively accepts either path.

Section 3.1 of the agreement provides 40% of Narayanan's highest annualized base salary over the two-year lookback, payable monthly during the restricted period. The two-year lookback and the monthly payment schedule track § 24L(b)(vii)'s language exactly. The 40% figure, however, is below the 50% statutory garden-leave floor.

Because the 40% payment cannot satisfy the garden-leave prong standing alone, enforceability under § 24L(b)(vii) depends on characterizing the combination of (i) the 40% monthly payments and (ii) the $90,000 retention bonus paid at signing as "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration." That is the viable theory. Its vulnerability is the unsettled post-2018 appellate question whether a sub-statutory "garden-leave-like" figure can be rehabilitated into "other consideration" by bundling it with a separate retention bonus, or whether the statute requires one prong or the other to be satisfied on its own terms. No SJC or Appeals Court decision addresses this directly on the present record. Superior Court decisions in Suffolk and Middlesex since 2020 have permitted "combined" consideration theories where the non-garden-leave element is both discrete and specified in the covenant. (*See* T&W 11/2023 internal memo, "Post-MNAA Enforcement Trends in Suffolk and Middlesex Superior Courts.") Before filing, we must confirm that the covenant's recitals expressly identify the $90,000 retention bonus as supporting consideration for purposes of § 24L(b)(vii); if not, a short, specific side-letter acknowledgment should be prepared for client execution.

*Holding: § 24L(b)(vii) is satisfied on a combined "other consideration" theory, with moderate litigation risk on the rehabilitation framing.*

#### 4. Twelve-Month Duration — § 24L(b)(iv)

Section 24L(b)(iv) caps an MNAA-governed non-compete at 12 months post-termination, with a two-year ceiling available only upon breach of fiduciary duty or unlawful taking of property. Section 2.1 sets the restricted period at 12 months flat. The duration sits at the statutory cap but does not exceed it. A 12-month duration remains subject to reasonableness review under § 24L(b)(iv) — reasonable in relation to the interests protected — and, as applied, 12 months aligns with Halston's quarterly enterprise-sales cycle and the forward-looking horizon of the ForecastIQ pipeline data.

*Holding: the 12-month duration is within the statutory cap and reasonable as applied.*

#### 5. Activity Scope — § 24L(b)(vi)

Section 24L(b)(vi) requires that activity restrictions be no broader than necessary to protect legitimate business interests and presumes reasonableness where the restriction is "limited to only the specific types of services provided by the employee at any time during the last 2 years of employment." The "Competing Business" definition in § 2.2 — "demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers" — tracks Narayanan's VP portfolio at Halston. She was not a generalist. The activity scope is not a blanket prohibition on "software" or on "sales leadership"; it is restricted to specific software categories and specific vertical customer bases.

*Holding: the activity scope is satisfied.*

#### 6. Geographic Scope — § 24L(b)(v)

Section 24L(b)(v) requires that the geographic reach be reasonable in relation to the protected interests and presumes reasonableness where the restriction is "limited to only the geographic areas in which the employee, during any time within the last 2 years of employment, provided services or had a material presence or influence." The statute thus defines its own safe harbor by reference to the employee's footprint.

"United States and Canada" materially exceeds Halston's operational footprint. Per client data, Halston has booked revenue in 29 US states and one Canadian province over the trailing 24 months. A Massachusetts court is likely to regard the covenant's geographic scope as exceeding the § 24L(b)(v) safe harbor. The operative question is remedial: reformation or voiding.

Massachusetts courts strongly prefer reformation. *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773, 778 (1974), articulates the blue-pencil preference in the restrictive-covenant context; post-MNAA Superior Court decisions in Suffolk and Middlesex have continued to reform rather than void overbroad geographic clauses where the core business interest is otherwise valid. A reformation to "the US states and Canadian provinces in which Halston has booked revenue during the 24 months preceding termination" is straightforward to administer, follows the § 24L(b)(v) safe-harbor formulation, and matches the client's actual footprint. A judge is materially more likely to take that path than to void the clause outright.

*Holding: the geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk but is unlikely to void the covenant; a reformed scope tracking the § 24L(b)(v) safe-harbor language should survive.*

### B. Common-Law Reasonableness Under *Boulanger* and *Marine Contractors*

The MNAA does not displace common-law reasonableness review. A covenant can satisfy § 24L's threshold validity requirements yet fail the *Boulanger* reasonableness test; the two inquiries apply sequentially, not as alternatives.

Under *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635, 639 (2004), a restrictive covenant is enforceable where it (i) protects a legitimate business interest, (ii) is reasonably limited in time and space, and (iii) is consonant with the public interest. *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280, 287–88 (1974), confines the "legitimate business interest" to the protection of trade secrets, other confidential information, and employer goodwill — not mere freedom from ordinary competition.

Halston's protectable interests here are concrete and more than adequate to satisfy *Marine Contractors*. The ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and training datasets are designated trade secrets (Schedule A) and meet the substantive definition: they derive independent economic value from not being generally known and are the subject of reasonable secrecy measures. Current-quarter and forward-looking customer pipeline reports and SSP allocation models are non-public confidential information. Narayanan's VP-level relationships with accounts including Brinkerhoff, Meridian, and Quellstream implicate the goodwill interest. The 12-month restricted period, applied to a reformed geographic scope, is reasonably tailored to protect those interests across a single sales cycle. The public interest is mixed — the MNAA itself reflects a legislative compromise between worker mobility and employer protection — but tilts favorably for Halston on the trade-secret axis.

*Holding: the covenant, as applied and (if necessary) as reformed on geographic scope, satisfies common-law reasonableness.*

### C. Choice-of-Law Enforceability — § 24L(e); *Oxford Global*

Section 24L(e) provides that § 24L applies to any employee who "is, or was at the time of his or her termination of employment, a resident of or employed in Massachusetts for at least 30 days immediately preceding his or her termination of employment" and invalidates any choice-of-law designation used to evade § 24L's substantive requirements. *Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462, 469–73 (2018), instructs that a Massachusetts choice-of-law clause will not be enforced where it would evade the more protective non-compete law of the employee's home state.

Two distinct questions arise here. The first is prescriptive: does § 24L apply to Narayanan at all, in light of her two-day-per-week Providence remote work? The statute requires either (a) Massachusetts residence *or* (b) employment in Massachusetts for at least 30 days immediately preceding termination. Narayanan was a Massachusetts resident for payroll purposes, and the employment agreement designates Cambridge as her office of record. Her payroll address is Massachusetts. On the current record she is likely "employed in Massachusetts" and/or "resident of" Massachusetts within § 24L(e)'s disjunctive meaning in the 30-day window preceding April 17, 2026. The answer is not automatic, however, and requires factual development: timesheet records for March 18 – April 17, 2026, expense reports showing physical presence, laptop geolocation metadata from the forensic imaging, and any documented business travel. These should be assembled before PI filing.

The second question is the downside scenario. Rhode Island's non-compete statute, R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-59-1 *et seq.*, is materially more restrictive than the MNAA and categorically voids non-competes against certain employee categories (including employees below specified income thresholds, which Narayanan exceeds, but the statute contains other substantive limitations). *Oxford Global* holds that a Massachusetts choice-of-law clause will yield to the more protective law of the employee's home state where the latter would refuse to enforce the covenant. If Rhode Island law were to apply *and* Rhode Island would not enforce, the § 7.2 choice-of-law selection would not save the covenant. This is a low-probability but high-severity scenario and warrants the factual development described above before filing.

*Holding: choice-of-law enforceability is likely satisfied under § 24L(e), pending factual confirmation of the 30-day presence window.*

### D. Preliminary Injunction Under *Packaging Industries*

Under *Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609, 617 (1980), the moving party must show (1) a likelihood of success on the merits, (2) irreparable harm in the absence of relief, (3) that the balance of harms favors the movant, and (4) consistency with the public interest. The four-factor test does not collapse the covenant and trade-secret theories; each theory should be analyzed on its own record.

**1. Likelihood of success.** *Covenant theory:* moderate-to-strong. Statutory validity is substantially supported, with two attenuators — the § 24L(b)(v) geographic-scope reformation risk and the § 24L(b)(vii) rehabilitation framing on the 40% garden leave. Reasonableness and legitimate-business-interest factors favor Halston. *Trade-secret theory:* strong. The March 28, 2026 USB-copy events to non-company devices, the directory names matching Schedule A trade-secret categories, and the suspicious timing (six days before resignation) collectively support a clear showing of misappropriation by improper means.

**2. Irreparable harm.** The two theories support independent — and independently sufficient — irreparable-harm showings, and they should be pleaded as distinct rather than collapsed. *Covenant theory:* loss of three named customer accounts (Brinkerhoff, Meridian, Quellstream) totaling approximately $14 million in ARR within a 90-day window; erosion of customer goodwill and pipeline integrity. These are textbook harms not fully quantifiable in money damages. *Trade-secret theory:* once ForecastIQ algorithm parameters or forward-looking pipeline data have been disseminated to a competitor, they cannot be unlearned or recalled. Massachusetts and federal courts routinely find irreparable harm presumed or nearly so in trade-secret misappropriation cases. The trade-secret theory carries the irreparable-harm factor cleanly; the covenant theory does so more narrowly.

**3. Balance of harms.** Narayanan faces income loss from delayed or foreclosed commencement at Kettlepoint, but the 40% monthly payment under § 3.1 materially mitigates that hardship during the 12-month restricted period. A narrowly tailored TRO on the trade-secret theory — prohibiting use or disclosure of the specific copied materials and restraining use of specific account information for the three named accounts — imposes minimal incremental hardship on Kettlepoint beyond the conduct it should not be engaging in in any event. The balance favors Halston on both theories and cleanly on the trade-secret theory.

**4. Public interest.** Massachusetts public interest is mixed on covenant enforcement — the MNAA itself represents a considered legislative balance — but strongly favorable on trade-secret protection under both DTSA and MUTSA. The public-interest factor favors Halston affirmatively on the trade-secret theory and is net-neutral on the covenant theory.

*Holding: PI likelihood is moderate on the covenant standing alone, substantial on the trade-secret theory, and substantial overall where the trade-secret claim carries the irreparable-harm factor.*

### E. Parallel DTSA / MUTSA Theory

The March 28, 2026 USB-copy events support an injunctive-relief claim under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. § 1836(b), and MUTSA, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G, independent of the covenant. DTSA § 1836(b)(3) authorizes injunctive relief for actual or threatened trade-secret misappropriation involving a trade secret "related to a product or service used in, or intended for use in, interstate or foreign commerce." MUTSA § 42A authorizes actual-or-threatened misappropriation injunctions on parallel terms.

The substantive elements common to both statutes are: (i) existence of a trade secret (economic value derived from secrecy plus reasonable measures to preserve it); (ii) misappropriation, meaning acquisition by improper means or disclosure/use without consent by a person with reason to know of improper acquisition; and (iii) the "threatened or actual" character of the misappropriation to support injunctive relief.

On the present record: (i) the ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and training datasets are designated in Schedule A, derive independent economic value from secrecy, and qualify as trade secrets; customer pipeline files and SSP models qualify as confidential information and, to the extent they reflect proprietary analytics, also as trade secrets. (ii) Halston's reasonable measures are substantiated by the designation in Schedule A, NDA coverage, access controls, and the fact of the forensic capability itself, which is evidence of active protective posture. (iii) Misappropriation by improper means is strongly inferable from the directory names ("Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning"), the use of USB devices whose serial numbers do not match any Halston-issued device, and the timing six days before resignation and twelve days before the Kettlepoint start date.

DTSA § 1836(b)(2) civil seizure is available only in "extraordinary circumstances" and requires a showing that an ordinary Rule 65 order would be inadequate. That is a high bar: the statute contemplates civil seizure for scenarios in which the subject would evade, disobey, or circumvent a standard order. On the current record there is no evidence Narayanan would ignore an ordinary TRO, and the files are (on current knowledge) already in unknown off-company storage, so a seizure order may not accomplish what an ordinary injunctive restraint could not. Civil seizure is **not recommended** here; an expedited TRO under ordinary Rule 65 procedure is both faster and lower-risk. The statutory reference is nevertheless worth preserving for the partner's awareness in the event subsequent facts justify escalation.

*Holding: the DTSA and MUTSA claims are substantially supported on the current record and can independently support injunctive relief.*

## V. Recommendation

Three actionable paths are available, ranked by expected return net of cost and client-relationship risk. Standing down and monitoring is not recommended in light of the forensic exfiltration and the 90-day ARR exposure.

**Option 1 (recommended) — Trade-Secret-First Strategy.**
File an expedited TRO and PI motion grounded principally on DTSA and MUTSA, tied to the March 28 USB-copy events and focused on (i) prohibiting use, disclosure, or further copying of the specific materials copied, and (ii) restraining any solicitation of or work on the three named accounts for an interim period. Plead the covenant claim in the same complaint but develop the covenant theory for the PI hearing rather than the TRO. Request an accelerated schedule under the 21-day window.

*Advantages:* highest likelihood of immediate, narrow, clean relief; avoids exposing the § 24L(b)(v) geographic-scope issue in the opening filing; preserves optionality on covenant enforcement; fee estimate at or below the $75K partner estimate.

*Disadvantages:* does not produce a covenant-enforceability ruling in the opening move; a TRO may invite a broader response from Kettlepoint than a pre-suit demand would.

**Option 2 — Full Covenant PI with Blue-Pencil Fallback.**
Move directly on the covenant under § 24L, expressly request reformation of § 2.3 to the § 24L(b)(v) safe-harbor formulation, and plead DTSA/MUTSA in the alternative.

*Advantages:* produces a definitive enforceability ruling (even if narrowed); useful if a firm-wide template outcome is strategically valuable beyond this matter.

*Disadvantages:* higher cost; invites an early reformation ruling that will narrow the covenant for all subsequent enforcement; marginally higher risk of adverse ruling on the § 24L(b)(vii) rehabilitation framing; delays the narrower, higher-probability trade-secret relief.

**Option 3 — Negotiated Standstill Pending Customer-Specific Non-Solicit.**
Propose to Kettlepoint a short-term standstill on the three named accounts (or a specified account list) in exchange for a customer-specific non-solicit carve-out from the full covenant, mutual releases on the forensic issue, and an agreed protocol for return or destruction of the copied materials.

*Advantages:* lowest cost; preserves commercial flexibility and relationship posture; protects the three named accounts without testing the covenant's vulnerabilities; potentially forecloses the weakness in the covenant analysis without exposing it.

*Disadvantages:* does not fully vindicate the trade-secret claim or establish precedent; may be perceived as weakness if Kettlepoint does not respond constructively.
