## I. Question Presented

Whether the 12-month non-competition covenant signed by Priya Narayanan on June 1, 2024 in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, such that Halston Analytics, Inc. can obtain a preliminary injunction in Suffolk Superior Court restraining Narayanan from commencing her Head of Revenue role at Kettlepoint AI, a direct retail-demand-forecasting competitor.

## II. Brief Answer

Likely yes, in substantially current form, with one meaningful vulnerability. The covenant satisfies the MNAA's notice, duration, consideration, and garden-leave requirements on the face of the record. The single serious statutory-validity risk is the "United States and Canada" geographic scope, which is facially broader than Halston's actual operational footprint and will draw scrutiny under § 24L(b)(v); Massachusetts courts, however, prefer blue-pencil reformation to voiding. Halston has a plausible path to a preliminary injunction under the *Packaging Industries* four-factor test, but the stronger near-term vehicle for emergency relief is a trade-secret theory grounded in the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events. A narrower TRO on trade-secret grounds is likely to succeed more cleanly than a full covenant-based PI and is the recommended first filing if the partner elects to proceed.

## III. Statement of Facts

Narayanan was hired by Halston Analytics, Inc. on February 1, 2022 as Senior Director of Enterprise Sales. On June 1, 2024, she was promoted to VP of Enterprise Sales; in connection with that promotion, her base salary rose from $215,000 to $285,000 (and later to $295,000 on January 1, 2026), she received a $90,000 retention bonus, and she signed the non-competition agreement now at issue. The offer letter attaching the covenant was delivered on May 17, 2024; Narayanan executed it on June 1, 2024 — 10 business days later.

Section 2.1 of the agreement imposes a 12-month restricted period "following termination of Employee's employment for any reason," during which Narayanan may not "engage in, own, manage, operate, consult for, or be employed by any Competing Business within the Restricted Territory." Section 2.2 defines "Competing Business" as any business offering "demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers." Section 2.3 defines "Restricted Territory" as "the United States and Canada." Section 3.1 obligates Halston to pay Narayanan, "on a monthly basis," 40% of her highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination. Section 7.2 selects Massachusetts law and Suffolk Superior Court (or the District of Massachusetts) as exclusive forum.

Narayanan resigned on April 3, 2026, giving 10 business days' notice; her last day was April 17, 2026. She was a Massachusetts resident for payroll purposes; her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record; she worked in Cambridge three days per week and remotely from Providence, Rhode Island two days per week. On April 15, 2026, Kettlepoint AI — a direct competitor in the retail-demand-forecasting vertical — announced her hire as Head of Revenue via press release, with a start date of April 20, 2026.

Forensic imaging of Narayanan's returned laptop, performed April 7, 2026 by Halston's IT Security under in-house counsel direction, identified two USB-copy events on March 28, 2026 (six days before her resignation). The directories copied were named "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning." The USB device serial numbers do not match any company-issued device. Two of Narayanan's former direct reports have confirmed that she led three "pipeline strategy" sessions in February–March 2026 concentrated on the retail-vertical accounts that Kettlepoint now targets.

## IV. Discussion

### A. MNAA Timing and 10-Business-Day Notice — § 24L(b)(i)

Under § 24L(b)(i), a non-compete must be provided to the employee by the earlier of a formal offer or ten business days before the commencement of employment, and must be signed by both parties and expressly state that the employee has the right to consult counsel. Here the covenant was delivered on May 17, 2024 and signed June 1, 2024 — exactly 10 business days later — and Narayanan was already employed, so the relevant trigger is the later portion of § 24L(b)(i) applicable to post-employment agreements (see Part IV.B below on consideration). The face of the record satisfies the timing floor. *Holding: the notice requirement is satisfied.*

### B. Independent Consideration for a Promotion-Triggered Agreement — § 24L(b)(ii)

When a non-compete is entered after commencement of employment, § 24L(b)(ii) requires, in addition to the ten-day notice, "fair and reasonable consideration independent from the continuation of employment." The June 1, 2024 covenant was signed in connection with (i) a meaningful promotion (Senior Director to VP), (ii) a base-salary increase from $215,000 to $285,000, and (iii) a $90,000 retention bonus. Each standing alone is plausibly sufficient; in combination they comfortably satisfy the statutory standard. Pre-MNAA case law under *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797 (2020), applying the common-law "material change" doctrine, is informative but not controlling for a 2024 agreement governed by the MNAA. *Holding: the independent-consideration requirement is satisfied.*

### C. Garden-Leave Consideration — § 24L(b)(vii)

Section 24L(b)(vii) requires that an enforceable non-compete be supported either by a garden-leave clause providing at least 50% of the employee's highest annualized base salary during the two years preceding termination, "paid on a pro rata basis" during the restricted period, or by "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration." Section 3.1 of the agreement obligates Halston to pay 40% of Narayanan's highest base salary over that same two-year lookback, on a monthly basis during the restricted period. Forty percent is below the 50% statutory garden-leave floor, but § 24L(b)(vii) permits "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" as an alternative — and the $90,000 retention bonus at signing supplies that alternative consideration. The two-year lookback tracks the statute. The clause as drafted should satisfy § 24L(b)(vii) on an "other consideration" theory, though counsel should be prepared to defend it against an argument that the 40% figure is an ineffective attempt at a compliant garden-leave provision rather than "other" consideration. *Holding: garden-leave / other consideration is satisfied, with moderate litigation risk on framing.*

### D. Twelve-Month Duration — § 24L(b)(iv)

The MNAA caps non-compete duration at twelve months (§ 24L(b)(iv)), extendable to two years only for breach of fiduciary duty or unlawful taking. The 12-month period in § 2.1 sits at the statutory cap. Duration at the cap is permissible but attracts per-se-reasonableness scrutiny; here, the 12-month period is defensible in light of Halston's quarterly sales cycle and the forward-looking pipeline data Narayanan controlled. *Holding: the 12-month duration is within the statutory cap and, as applied, reasonable.*

### E. Geographic Scope — § 24L(b)(v)

Section 24L(b)(v) requires the geographic reach be reasonable in relation to the legitimate business interest protected; the statute presumes reasonableness where the covenant is "limited to only the geographic areas in which the employee, during any time within the last 2 years of employment, provided services or had a material presence or influence." The covenant defines the Restricted Territory as "the United States and Canada." Halston's operational footprint — per client data, booked revenue in 29 US states and one Canadian province over the last 24 months — is meaningfully narrower than that. A court is likely to regard "United States and Canada" as facially overbroad and to reform rather than void the provision; Massachusetts courts consistently prefer reformation, a posture confirmed as to restrictive covenants in *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974). *Holding: the geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk but is unlikely to void the covenant.*

### F. Legitimate Business Interest — *Marine Contractors*

Under *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280 (1974), and confirmed post-MNAA, a restrictive covenant must protect a legitimate business interest — trade secrets, other confidential information, or employer goodwill — not freedom from ordinary competition. Narayanan's portfolio included: proprietary ForecastIQ algorithm parameters; current-quarter and forward-looking customer pipeline reports; SSP allocation models; and the win/loss database. The identified trade secrets meet the *Marine Contractors* standard, and her customer-facing VP role also implicates the goodwill interest. *Holding: legitimate business interest is satisfied.*

### G. Preliminary Injunction Analysis — *Packaging Industries*

Under *Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609 (1980), a Massachusetts court weighs (1) likelihood of success on the merits; (2) irreparable harm to the moving party; (3) balance of harms; and (4) public interest.

**Likelihood of success.** Substantial as to the covenant's validity, subject to the geographic-scope reformation issue; strong as to misappropriation on a standalone trade-secret theory given the USB-copy events.

**Irreparable harm.** Halston has credible irreparable-harm arguments on both theories. On the covenant, loss of three named accounts (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech) covering ~$14M ARR in the next 90 days is concrete and largely unquantifiable pre-judgment. On trade secrets, the March 28 USB exfiltration of pipeline and ForecastIQ tuning data is, as a matter of settled law, presumptively irreparable.

**Balance of harms.** Narayanan would lose income from her new role, but the garden-leave / other consideration mitigates hardship during the restricted period. A narrower trade-secret-only TRO imposes minimal hardship on Kettlepoint beyond prohibiting use of specific files.

**Public interest.** Massachusetts public interest is ambivalent — the MNAA reflects a legislative balance between worker mobility and legitimate employer protection. The trade-secret theory cuts more cleanly in Halston's favor.

*Holding: likelihood of PI success is moderate on the covenant alone and substantial on a trade-secret theory or combined theory.*

## V. Recommendation

Three options are available to the partner, ranked by expected return net of cost and client-relationship risk:

**Option 1 — Narrow TRO on trade-secret misappropriation (DTSA/MUTSA), followed by PI on the covenant.** Highest probability of fast, clean relief. Uses the USB-copy forensic findings as the centerpiece, avoids the geographic-scope fight in the initial filing, and preserves the covenant claim for the full PI hearing. Estimated fee exposure at or below the $75K estimate for expedited briefing.

**Option 2 — Full PI enforcing the covenant, with blue-pencil fallback and trade-secret theory pleaded in the alternative.** Higher cost; higher likelihood of triggering a reformation ruling that narrows the covenant in any follow-on proceeding. Recommend only if the partner determines that a definitive enforceability ruling (even as narrowed) is strategically valuable.

**Option 3 — Negotiated standstill pending a customer-specific non-solicit.** Preserves the client relationship with Kettlepoint, protects the three named accounts, and avoids the geographic-scope litigation risk. Most conservative on cost; does not address the forensic exfiltration directly and may leave that remedy on the table.

Standing down and monitoring is not recommended given the concrete USB-copy evidence and the 90-day ARR exposure.
