## I. Question Presented

Whether Priya Narayanan's 12-month non-competition covenant, executed June 1, 2024 in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales and a $90,000 retention bonus, is (a) statutorily valid under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, (b) reasonable under the *Boulanger*/*Marine Contractors* common-law framework that survives on top of the statute, and (c) amenable to preliminary-injunctive enforcement under *Packaging Industries* in Suffolk Superior Court against Narayanan's commencement of a Head of Revenue role at Kettlepoint AI — and, alternatively, whether the March 28, 2026 USB-copy forensic findings support a parallel Defend Trade Secrets Act and Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act theory sufficient to support injunctive relief independent of the covenant.

## II. Brief Answer

The 12-month covenant is likely enforceable in substantially its current form as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business, though the "United States and Canada" geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk under § 24L(b)(v) that a Massachusetts court is likely to cure by blue-pencil rather than voiding. The independent-consideration requirement of § 24L(b)(ii) is comfortably satisfied by the promotion, the salary increase, and the $90,000 retention bonus; the garden-leave provision at 40% falls below the statutory 50% floor but is defensible as "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" under § 24L(b)(vii) given the retention bonus. A covenant-based preliminary injunction under *Packaging Industries* is a close question principally because of the geographic-scope defect.

The stronger near-term injunctive theory rests on trade-secret misappropriation under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq., and G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G (MUTSA), grounded in the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events. We recommend opening with a trade-secret-based TRO and preserving the covenant theory for the PI hearing. The choice-of-law question raised by Narayanan's two-day-per-week Providence remote work requires factual development of the 30-day pre-termination lookback under § 24L(e) before filing.

## III. Statement of Facts

On June 1, 2024, she was promoted to VP of Enterprise Sales. The offer letter attaching the covenant was delivered to Narayanan on May 17, 2024; she signed on June 1, 2024 — 10 business days later.

The agreement's operative provisions read:

>
> **§ 2.3** *Restricted Territory.* "Restricted Territory" means the United States and Canada.

> **§ 3.1** *Garden Leave Consideration.* During the Restricted Period, the Company shall pay Employee, on a monthly basis, an amount equal to forty percent (40%) of Employee's highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination.

Schedule A designates as trade secrets and confidential information, among other items, customer pipeline reports, SSP allocation models, the win/loss database, and the proprietary ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and training datasets.

Narayanan resigned on April 3, 2026, giving 10 business days' notice; her last day was April 17, 2026. Her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record. She was a Massachusetts resident for payroll purposes and worked in Cambridge three days per week; she worked remotely from her home in Providence, Rhode Island two days per week. ## IV. Discussion

The analysis proceeds in two tiers. Part A addresses **statutory validity** under § 24L: if any threshold element fails, the covenant is unenforceable regardless of reasonableness. Part B addresses **common-law reasonableness** under *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635 (2004), and *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280 (1974), which applies on top of the statute. Part C addresses **choice-of-law enforceability** under § 24L(e). Part D applies the *Packaging Industries* PI framework. Part E sets out the parallel DTSA/MUTSA theory.

### A. Statutory Validity Under § 24L

#### 1. Notice — § 24L(b)(i)

Section 24L(b)(i) requires that an MNAA-governed covenant be provided to the employee by the earlier of a formal offer of employment or ten business days before the commencement of employment, be signed by both parties, and expressly state the employee's right to consult counsel. For post-commencement agreements, the ten-business-day window runs from delivery to acceptance. Here the covenant was delivered May 17, 2024 and executed June 1, 2024 — exactly 10 business days later. *This element is satisfied.*

#### 2. Independent Consideration — § 24L(b)(ii)

When a non-compete post-dates the commencement of employment, § 24L(b)(ii) requires, in addition to the notice described in (b)(i), "fair and reasonable consideration independent from the continuation of employment." The statute is not further defined, but the consensus post-2018 reading is that a meaningful promotion plus discrete monetary consideration is comfortably sufficient. The June 1, 2024 covenant accompanied (i) a promotion from Senior Director to Vice President, (ii) a $70,000 base-salary increase, and (iii) a $90,000 retention bonus specifically and separately paid. Each of the three standing alone is plausibly sufficient; in combination they exceed any reasonable interpretation of § 24L(b)(ii)'s threshold.

It is worth distinguishing pre-MNAA authority here. *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797 (2020), applying the common-law "material change" doctrine, held that a material change in the employment relationship may be necessary to breathe new life into an earlier covenant. *Automile* does not directly govern a post-October 1, 2018 agreement; § 24L supplies its own consideration regime. *Automile* remains informative because it signals the SJC's continuing concern that covenants not become dead-letter boilerplate carried forward unchanged through promotions, but on the present facts the 2024 agreement is itself a fresh covenant supported by fresh consideration, so the *Automile* question does not really arise. *This element is satisfied.*

#### 3. Garden-Leave / Other Mutually-Agreed-Upon Consideration — § 24L(b)(vii)

Section 24L(b)(vii) requires that the covenant be supported either by (a) a garden-leave clause providing at least 50% of the employee's "highest annualized base salary paid by the employer within the 2 years preceding the employee's termination," paid on a pro-rata basis during the restricted period, or (b) "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" between employer and employee, provided such consideration is specified in the covenant.

The 40% figure in § 3.1 falls below the 50% statutory garden-leave floor. The agreement therefore cannot satisfy § 24L(b)(vii) as a "garden-leave" clause per se. The viable theory is that the combination of (i) the 40% monthly payment during the restricted period and (ii) the $90,000 retention bonus paid at signing constitutes "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration." The retention bonus is specified in the covenant's recitals (subject to our confirming this on document review before filing). This is defensible but not airtight: a plaintiff-side argument that a sub-statutory garden-leave figure cannot be rehabilitated by labeling it "other consideration" is foreseeable, and there is limited post-2018 appellate guidance on the point.

The two-year lookback in § 3.1 ("highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination") tracks the statutory language and requires no further analysis. *This element is satisfied, with moderate litigation risk on the rehabilitation framing.*

#### 4. Twelve-Month Cap — § 24L(b)(iv)

Section 24L(b)(iv) caps a non-compete at 12 months post-termination, subject to a two-year extension only for breach of fiduciary duty or unlawful taking of property. Section 2.1 sets the restricted period at 12 months flat. *This element is satisfied.*

#### 5. Activity Scope — § 24L(b)(vi)

Section 24L(b)(vi) requires that activity restrictions be no broader than necessary to protect the employer's legitimate business interest, and presumptively reasonable if "limited to only the specific types of services provided by the employee at any time during the last 2 years of employment." Section 2.2 restricts Narayanan from employment by any business offering "demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers." That scope maps directly onto Narayanan's VP portfolio at Halston; she was not a generalist. It is not a blanket prohibition on the software industry. *This element is satisfied.*

#### 6. Geographic Scope — § 24L(b)(v)

Section 24L(b)(v) requires the geographic reach be reasonable in relation to the interests protected, and provides a safe harbor where the covenant is "limited to only the geographic areas in which the employee, during any time within the last 2 years of employment, provided services or had a material presence or influence."

"United States and Canada" is facially broader than Halston's operational footprint. Per client data, Halston has booked revenue in 29 US states and one Canadian province over the last 24 months. A Massachusetts court is likely to regard "United States and Canada" as exceeding the § 24L(b)(v) safe harbor. The question is whether the court (a) reforms to a narrower geographic scope or (b) voids the clause.

Massachusetts courts prefer reformation to voiding for overbroad restrictive covenants. *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773, 778 (1974), articulates the blue-pencil preference, and post-MNAA Superior Court decisions in Suffolk and Middlesex have continued to reform rather than void. (*See* T&W 11/2023 memo, "Post-MNAA Enforcement Trends in Suffolk and Middlesex Superior Courts.") A reformation to "US states and Canadian provinces where Halston has booked revenue during the 24 months preceding termination" is both readily administrable and consistent with the § 24L(b)(v) safe harbor. *This element presents a substantial reformation risk but is unlikely to void the covenant; a narrowed scope should survive.*

### B. Common-Law Reasonableness — *Boulanger* / *Marine Contractors*

The MNAA does not displace common-law reasonableness analysis; a covenant can be statutorily valid yet fail under *Boulanger*'s reasonableness test (and vice versa), so the two analyses apply in sequence rather than as alternatives.

Under *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635, 639 (2004), a restrictive covenant is enforceable where it (i) is necessary to protect a legitimate business interest, (ii) is reasonably limited in time and space, and (iii) is consonant with the public interest. *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280, 287–88 (1974), defines the legitimate business interest as the protection of trade secrets, other confidential information, and employer goodwill — not mere freedom from ordinary competition.

Halston's protectable interests here are concrete: the ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and training datasets are trade secrets (Schedule A); current-quarter and forward-looking customer pipeline reports and SSP models qualify as confidential information; and Narayanan's VP-level customer relationships with accounts like Brinkerhoff, Meridian, and Quellstream implicate the goodwill interest. The 12-month/reformed-geographic restriction is reasonably tailored to protect those interests across a single sales cycle. The public interest is neutral-to-favorable: the MNAA itself reflects a considered legislative balance, and trade-secret protection cuts affirmatively for Halston.

*The covenant, as applied and (if necessary) as reformed on geographic scope, satisfies common-law reasonableness.*

### C. Choice-of-Law Enforceability — § 24L(e); *Oxford Global*

Section 24L(e) provides that an MNAA-governed covenant is unenforceable against an employee who is "a resident of or employed in Massachusetts for at least 30 days immediately preceding his or her termination of employment" only if Massachusetts law is selected, and further provides that no choice-of-law provision can be used to evade § 24L's substantive requirements. The converse implication — that § 24L does not apply to an employee neither resident nor employed in Massachusetts in the relevant 30-day window — is the vulnerability here.

Narayanan worked three days a week in Cambridge and two days a week remotely from Providence throughout her VP tenure. Her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record; her payroll address was Massachusetts. On the present record she is likely "employed in Massachusetts" for § 24L(e) purposes in the 30 days preceding April 17, 2026, notwithstanding the Rhode Island remote work. But the answer is not automatic. We should obtain and review: (i) timesheet records for March 18 – April 17, 2026; (ii) expense reports showing physical presence; (iii) laptop geolocation metadata from the forensic imaging; and (iv) any records of travel for business.

The downside scenario matters. Rhode Island's non-compete statute, R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-59-1 *et seq.*, is materially more restrictive than the MNAA and categorically voids non-competes against certain employee categories; *Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462 (2018), holds that a Massachusetts choice-of-law clause will not be enforced where it would evade the more protective law of the employee's home state. If Rhode Island law applied — and if it would not enforce the covenant — the choice-of-law clause itself falls. This is a low-probability but high-severity scenario warranting factual development before filing. *This element is likely satisfied pending factual confirmation.*

### D. Preliminary Injunction Under *Packaging Industries*

Under *Packaging Industries Group, Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609, 617 (1980), the moving party must show (1) likelihood of success on the merits, (2) irreparable harm, (3) a favorable balance of harms, and (4) consistency with the public interest. The analysis differs meaningfully between the covenant theory and the trade-secret theory and should be run separately for each.

**Likelihood of success.** On the covenant: moderate-to-strong, attenuated by the geographic-scope reformation risk and the § 24L(b)(vii) rehabilitation framing. On the trade-secret theory: strong, given the documented March 28, 2026 USB-copy events, the Schedule A designation of the files' apparent content, and the suspicious timing (six days before resignation) and non-company USB serial numbers.

**Irreparable harm.** The covenant theory supports an irreparable-harm showing on goodwill and pipeline-confidentiality grounds — a Q2 forecast turning on the retention of three named accounts (~$14M ARR) is the paradigm case of harm that cannot be made whole by money damages. The trade-secret theory supports an independent, and stronger, irreparable-harm showing: once ForecastIQ tuning parameters or pipeline data are disseminated to a competitor, they cannot be recalled. These two harm theories should be pleaded as distinct — they are independently sufficient, and the court should not be invited to collapse them.

**Balance of harms.** Narayanan would lose income from her Kettlepoint role, but the 40% monthly payment under § 3.1 materially mitigates that hardship during the restricted period. On the trade-secret theory, a narrowly tailored TRO prohibiting use of the specific copied files imposes minimal incremental hardship on Kettlepoint.

**Public interest.** Massachusetts public interest is mixed on covenant enforcement — the MNAA represents a legislative compromise — but strongly favorable on trade-secret protection under both DTSA and MUTSA.

*PI likelihood: moderate on the covenant alone; substantial on the trade-secret theory; and substantial on a combined theory where the trade-secret claim carries the irreparable-harm showing.*

### E. Parallel DTSA / MUTSA Theory

The March 28, 2026 USB-copy events independently support a claim under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 *et seq.*, and MUTSA, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G. DTSA § 1836(b)(3) authorizes injunctive relief for trade-secret misappropriation involving products or services used in interstate commerce. MUTSA § 42 authorizes actual or threatened misappropriation injunctions.

The elements are: (i) existence of a trade secret, (ii) reasonable measures to preserve secrecy, and (iii) misappropriation. On the present record: (i) the ForecastIQ parameters and training datasets are designated in Schedule A as trade secrets and derive actual economic value from secrecy; (ii) Halston's access controls, NDA coverage, and IT-security posture (evidenced by the forensic capability itself) support the reasonable-measures prong; (iii) the directory names "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning," the USB serial mismatch, and the timing six days before resignation support a strong inference of misappropriation by improper means under both statutes.

DTSA § 1836(b)(2) civil seizure is *not* recommended here; it is reserved for "extraordinary circumstances" and requires a showing that ordinary injunctive relief will be insufficient. The 21-day expedited-briefing track the partner is considering makes an ordinary TRO both faster and lower-risk than a civil-seizure application.

*The trade-secret claim is substantially supported on the current record and can support injunctive relief independent of the covenant.*

## V. Recommendation

Three actionable paths, ranked by expected return net of cost and client-relationship impact:

**Option 1 (recommended) — Trade-secret-first strategy.** File an expedited TRO and PI motion on DTSA and MUTSA theories, keyed to the March 28 USB-copy events and focused on use/disclosure of the specific copied materials and the three named accounts. Plead the covenant claim in the same complaint but brief it for the PI hearing rather than the TRO. Advantages: highest likelihood of immediate relief, narrowest tailoring, avoids exposing the geographic-scope issue in the initial filing. Estimated fee exposure at or below the $75K estimate.

**Option 2 — Full covenant PI with blue-pencil fallback.** Move directly on the covenant, request reformation of the geographic scope, and plead DTSA/MUTSA in the alternative. Advantages: produces a definitive enforceability ruling (even if narrowed); useful if the partner wants to establish a firm-wide template. Disadvantages: higher cost; invites reformation ruling that narrows the covenant across future matters; marginally higher risk of adverse ruling on the § 24L(b)(vii) rehabilitation framing.

**Option 3 — Negotiated standstill pending customer-specific non-solicit.** Propose to Kettlepoint a short-term standstill on the three named accounts (or a specified account list) in exchange for a carve-out from the full covenant and mutual releases on the forensic issue. Advantages: lowest cost; preserves commercial flexibility; potentially forecloses the weakness in the covenant analysis without testing it. Disadvantages: does not vindicate the trade-secret claim; may telegraph weakness if Kettlepoint perceives the offer as a retreat.

Standing down and monitoring is not recommended in light of the forensic exfiltration and the 90-day ARR exposure.
