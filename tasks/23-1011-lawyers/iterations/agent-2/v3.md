## I. Question Presented

Whether the 12-month noncompetition covenant that Priya Narayanan executed on June 1, 2024 in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales — purporting to restrict her for twelve months post-termination from working for any business offering demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers throughout the United States and Canada — is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, and the common-law reasonableness standard of *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635 (2004), so as to support a preliminary injunction in Suffolk Superior Court against her April 20, 2026 start at Kettlepoint AI; and whether, independent of the covenant, the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events support parallel injunctive relief under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq., and the Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G.

Sub-questions: (a) whether the covenant satisfies each threshold element of § 24L(b); (b) whether its geographic scope can be enforced as drafted or only as reformed under *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974); (c) whether Narayanan's two-day-a-week Providence remote work defeats § 24L(e) choice-of-law protection; (d) whether the *Packaging Industries* four-factor test supports preliminary relief; and (e) whether DTSA and MUTSA independently support injunctive relief on the forensic record.

## II. Brief Answer

The covenant is likely enforceable in substantially its current form as to Kettlepoint AI's retail-demand-forecasting business, though the "United States and Canada" geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk that Massachusetts courts will almost certainly resolve by blue-penciling to Halston's actual revenue footprint rather than voiding the covenant. The separate and, in the near term, stronger injunctive theory rests on trade-secret misappropriation under DTSA and MUTSA: the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events — involving the "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" directories transferred to non-company devices six days before resignation — support irreparable-harm findings and return-of-materials relief on a freestanding basis.

Recommended procedural path: a combined preliminary-injunction motion coupling covenant enforcement (with an express blue-pencil fallback) and DTSA/MUTSA relief, filed in Suffolk Superior Court on a three-week expedited track. Estimated probability of meaningful injunctive relief in some form: substantial (approximately 65–75%). Estimated probability of enforcement of the covenant exactly as drafted: modest (approximately 30–40%), driven principally by geographic overbreadth. Estimated probability of return-of-materials and use-restriction relief on the trade-secret theory: high (approximately 80%), assuming the USB-copied content is developed and the trade-secret status of specific files is corroborated pre-filing.

## IV. Discussion

Analysis proceeds in five parts. Part IV.A addresses statutory validity under § 24L(b)'s threshold requirements. Part IV.B addresses common-law reasonableness under *Boulanger* and *Marine Contractors* — an independent overlay that applies even where § 24L(b) is satisfied. Part IV.C addresses choice-of-law vulnerability under § 24L(e). Part IV.D applies the *Packaging Industries* preliminary-injunction test. Part IV.E addresses the parallel DTSA and MUTSA theory. The separation of Parts IV.A and IV.B is deliberate: MNAA threshold validity and common-law reasonableness operate in series, not in substitution. A covenant may be statutorily valid but unreasonable, or reasonable in principle but statutorily noncompliant. Both layers must hold for enforcement to issue.

### A. MNAA Threshold Validity — G.L. c. 149, § 24L(b)

The MNAA governs noncompetition agreements "entered into on or after October 1, 2018." G.L. c. 149, § 24L(a). The June 1, 2024 agreement falls squarely within its scope.

**1. Timing and Notice — § 24L(b)(i).** For a covenant entered after commencement of employment, § 24L(b)(i) requires delivery "at least 10 business days before the agreement is to be effective." Delivery was May 17, 2024 and execution was June 1, 2024. Counting business days between delivery and execution and excluding Memorial Day (Monday, May 27, 2024), the count is ten: May 20, 21, 22, 23, 28, 29, 30 plus June 2, 3, 4 — actually, correctly counted forward from delivery: May 20, 21, 22, 23 (4); skip Memorial Day; May 28, 29, 30 (3, running total 7); June 2, 3, 4 (3, running total 10); signature on June 1 falls on the tenth business day after delivery when counting exclusively. Because § 24L(b)(i) requires "at least 10 business days before," the count must be conservative — we should have an HR-custodian affidavit prepared confirming this count, and confirm that no other federal holiday (e.g., a locally observed day) fell within the window. On the facts as represented, the element is satisfied, and we should not file until the affidavit is in hand. **Holding: element satisfied, subject to HR-custodian affidavit confirming the 10-business-day count.**

**2. Independent Consideration — § 24L(b)(ii).** Section 24L(b)(ii) requires, for a covenant executed after the commencement of employment, that it be "supported by fair and reasonable consideration independent from the continuation of employment." This provision displaces the common-law "material change" analysis that once governed promotion-triggered agreements. Pre-MNAA authority, most notably *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797, 803–05 (2020), developed the "material change" doctrine to police agreements reassigned or imposed without fresh consideration in the middle of an employment relationship. *Automile* addressed a covenant executed before October 1, 2018 and therefore applied the common-law doctrine directly; the Supreme Judicial Court there relied on the common-law requirement that a post-hire covenant be supported by new consideration beyond continued employment. 483 Mass. at 803–05.

*Automile* remains informative for identifying what kinds of consideration Massachusetts courts have historically recognized, and pre-MNAA precedent is a reliable guide to how trial courts will evaluate "fair and reasonable consideration" under § 24L(b)(ii). But *Automile* is not controlling for agreements governed by the statute. For the 2024 agreement, the analytical path runs through § 24L(b)(ii) directly. By any measure, the June 2024 package — a promotion from Senior Director to VP of Enterprise Sales, a $70,000 (approximately 33%) base-salary increase, and a contemporaneous $90,000 retention bonus — comfortably satisfies the statutory standard. The retention bonus alone is independent consideration bargained specifically for execution of the covenant. See generally *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280, 287 (1974) (continued employment alone insufficient; "something of value" required). **Holding: element satisfied.**

**3. Garden Leave or Other Mutually-Agreed-Upon Consideration — § 24L(b)(vii).** Section 24L(b)(vii) requires either (i) a garden-leave payment, on a pro-rata monthly basis during the restricted period, of "at least 50 per cent of the employee's highest annualized base salary paid . . . within the 2 years preceding the employee's termination," or (ii) "other mutually-agreed upon consideration between the employer and the employee, provided that such consideration is specified in the noncompetition agreement."

Section 3.1 provides a 40% monthly payment, below the 50% floor. The statute's disjunctive "or other mutually-agreed upon consideration" contemplates alternatives to a 50%-or-more garden-leave formula. The question is whether the 40% payment plus the $90,000 retention bonus plus the promotion satisfies the statute's disjunctive alternative.

Two readings are plausible. On the employer-favorable reading, § 24L(b)(vii)'s disjunctive expressly sanctions structures that combine a reduced garden-leave payment with other bargained-for consideration, provided the consideration is specified in the agreement. The 40% payment is in § 3.1; the retention bonus is in the offer letter attached to and integrated with the agreement; and the promotion is the consideration cited in § 24L(b)(ii) for the separate independent-consideration requirement. On the employee-favorable reading, § 24L(b)(vii) sets a quantitative floor (50%) and the "other consideration" prong requires functionally equivalent value rather than permitting any reduction below 50%. Under this reading, a 40% payment plus a $90,000 one-time bonus would need to be shown to exceed — in present value terms — 50% of base over 12 months (which, on $295,000 base, is $147,500 annualized, or ~$12,292 monthly; 40% is ~$9,833 monthly; the $24,583 monthly shortfall over 12 months is $29,500 — the retention bonus of $90,000 exceeds this mathematically, though one can question whether a 2024 retention bonus "counts" toward 2026–2027 garden leave).

Appellate authority on § 24L(b)(vii) is sparse. The conservative assessment is that the 40% structure supplemented by the retention bonus and promotion is likely to be sustained, particularly given Massachusetts courts' preference for reformation over voiding (see Part IV.A.5 below), but this is Halston's second-most vulnerable MNAA issue. Crucially, § 24L(b)(vii)'s two-year lookback in § 3.1 tracks the statutory "within the 2 years preceding the employee's termination" language verbatim; that subsidiary point requires no further analysis. **Holding: element likely satisfied; modest litigation risk; strongest defense couples the 40% payment with the retention bonus and promotion as integrated consideration.**

**4. Twelve-Month Duration — § 24L(b)(iv).** Section 24L(b)(iv) caps noncompetition agreements at "not . . . more than 12 months from the date of cessation of employment," extendable to two years in cases of specified employee misconduct. The covenant's 12-month term sits precisely at the statutory cap. Duration at the cap is presumptively valid; reasonableness-as-applied can still be probed under *Boulanger*, but trial courts rarely void a cap-length covenant for duration alone when the role warrants protection. **Holding: element satisfied.**

**5. Geographic Scope — § 24L(b)(v).** Section 24L(b)(v) requires geographic scope "reasonable in relation to the interests protected." The scope is reasonable, the statute further provides, if it "is limited to only the geographic areas in which the employee, during any time within the last 2 years of employment, provided services or had a material presence or influence." The covenant's "United States and Canada" scope is facially broad.

Per client data, Halston has booked revenue in only twenty-nine US states and one Canadian province in the 24 months preceding termination. This leaves the covenant sweeping over twenty-four US states, twelve Canadian provinces/territories, and the District of Columbia in which Halston has no commercial footprint. On a straightforward application of § 24L(b)(v), this is overbroad.

Massachusetts courts, however, have long preferred reformation to voiding. *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773, 778 (1974), establishes that a court may "enforce the covenant to the extent that it is reasonable" and modify its terms accordingly. Although *All Stainless* predates the MNAA by over four decades, nothing in § 24L displaces the equitable reformation power, and post-MNAA Superior Court decisions have routinely applied reformation to MNAA-era agreements with overbroad scope language. The reformation rule is one of pragmatic equity rather than pro-employer bias: a court can narrow scope to protect legitimate interests without releasing the employee from all restraint.

Two caveats. First, a minority of post-MNAA decisions have signaled that egregiously overbroad drafting may weigh against equitable reformation as evidence of drafter bad faith or *in terrorem* overreach. A "US and Canada" restriction where the employer operates in 30 of 67 possible jurisdictions is common drafter-overreach, not facially egregious. Second, reformation typically narrows scope to the reasonable minimum rather than redrafting; a reviewing court is more likely to cut back "US and Canada" to "those US states and Canadian provinces in which Halston has booked revenue within the 24 months preceding termination" than to craft novel limitations.

The likely reformed scope: the 29 US states and one Canadian province of Halston's actual footprint, potentially further narrowed to the retail-forecasting vertical in which Narayanan operated. Kettlepoint AI appears to operate in the retail-forecasting vertical and to be seeking Narayanan for that specific purpose, so reformation to either geographic footprint or vertical likely still captures her proposed employment. **Holding: substantial reformation risk; highly unlikely to void outright; probable blue-pencil to Halston's actual footprint; reformation likely to capture Narayanan's proposed Kettlepoint role.**

**6. Activity Scope — § 24L(b)(vi).** Section 24L(b)(vi) requires scope of activities restricted to "be reasonable in relation to the interests protected." The covenant's activity scope — "demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers" — directly tracks Halston's product line and Narayanan's VP-level portfolio. It is narrower than a blanket "competitor" restriction and maps to specific verticals in which Narayanan operated. **Holding: element satisfied.**

### B. Common-Law Reasonableness Overlay — Boulanger, Marine Contractors

Even where a covenant clears MNAA threshold validity, it must satisfy the common-law reasonableness standard that *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635, 639 (2004), imported into modern Massachusetts practice from *All Stainless*, 364 Mass. at 778. The covenant must (i) protect a legitimate business interest, (ii) be reasonable in time, space, and the scope of activities prohibited, and (iii) be consonant with the public interest.

**1. Legitimate Business Interest.** *Marine Contractors*, 365 Mass. at 287–88, recognizes three categories of protectable interest: trade secrets, confidential information, and customer goodwill. It excludes protection from "ordinary competition." Halston's Schedule A identifies paradigmatic protectable interests: customer pipeline reports (a textbook species of confidential information), SSP allocation models (proprietary methodology), win/loss database (competitive intelligence), and ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and training data (quintessential trade secret). Narayanan, as VP of Enterprise Sales, had daily access to and helped cultivate Halston's goodwill with customers including the three named Q2-at-risk accounts. Her role squarely fits the *Marine Contractors* paradigm; this is not a case of protecting against ordinary competition from a fungible employee. **Holding: strong legitimate business interest.**

**2. Time.** Twelve months is reasonable in relation to the interests protected, particularly given the roughly 90-day competitive half-life of current-quarter pipeline data and the demonstrably longer competitive half-life of ForecastIQ algorithm parameters. **Holding: reasonable.**

**3. Space.** Reasonable only as reformed. See Part IV.A.5 above. **Holding: reasonable as reformed; unreasonable as drafted.**

**4. Activity Scope.** Tracks Halston's business; narrower than blanket "competitor" restriction; reasonable. **Holding: reasonable.**

**5. Public Interest.** Massachusetts policy after the MNAA favors enforcement of properly-noticed, garden-leave-compensated, and reasonably-tailored covenants. *See* *Boulanger*, 442 Mass. at 639. Public interest is neutral to mildly favorable to Halston on the covenant theory; it is more clearly favorable on the trade-secret theory (Part IV.E below), where Massachusetts and federal policy uniformly disfavor misappropriation. **Holding: consonant.**

### C. Choice of Law and the § 24L(e) Vulnerability

Section 24L(e) voids any choice-of-law or forum-selection provision "that would have the effect of avoiding the requirements of this section, if the employee has been, for at least 30 days immediately preceding the employee's cessation of employment, a resident of or employed in the commonwealth at the time of the employee's cessation of employment." (Emphasis added.) The statutory disjunction is decisive: an employee need only be *either* a Massachusetts resident *or* employed in Massachusetts during the 30-day window.

Narayanan is a Providence, Rhode Island resident, so the residency prong is not satisfied. The question is whether she was "employed in the commonwealth" during the 30-day pre-termination window (March 18 – April 17, 2026). On the representations made, Narayanan worked three days per week at Halston's Cambridge office — that is, approximately 60% of her working time over any arbitrary 30-day window. Her employment agreement designates Cambridge as her office of record. The three-day-per-week Cambridge presence is more than sufficient to sustain "employed in the commonwealth" under any reasonable reading of § 24L(e).

Factual development is nonetheless required pre-filing. We should collect: (i) badge-in logs or equivalent physical-presence records for March 18 – April 17, 2026; (ii) expense reports reflecting Cambridge-based activity; (iii) timesheets or Slack/email metadata showing working-hour location; and (iv) a representative sample of calendar entries showing in-office meetings. A brief declaration from Narayanan's former manager confirming the three-day schedule would also be valuable.

*Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462 (2018), warns Massachusetts courts against applying Massachusetts law (or holding a Massachusetts forum) where doing so would circumvent the more protective law of the employee's home jurisdiction. 480 Mass. at 469–71. Here, however, Rhode Island's non-compete statute, R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-59-3, does not apply to Narayanan in any event. That statute prohibits non-competes only against (i) non-exempt employees under the FLSA, (ii) undergraduate or graduate students in internships, (iii) employees 18 or younger, and (iv) low-wage employees (defined as earning 250% of the federal poverty guideline or less). Narayanan, a VP earning $295,000, falls into none of these protected classes. A Rhode Island court applying R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-59-3 would therefore permit the covenant's enforcement — which means *Oxford Global*'s defensive invocation of a "more protective" home-state law is not available to Narayanan.

The combination of these facts — three-day-per-week Cambridge presence, Cambridge office of record, and a Rhode Island statute that does not protect Narayanan — makes § 24L(e) applicability robust. The choice-of-law and forum-selection provisions of § 7.2 should hold. **Holding: § 24L(e) likely supports application of Massachusetts law and a Massachusetts forum; factual development of the 30-day pre-termination location record required pre-filing, principally as a prophylactic against rebuttal.**

### D. Preliminary Injunction Under Packaging Industries

*Packaging Indus. Grp., Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609, 617 (1980), governs the Massachusetts state-court preliminary-injunction analysis. The movant must show: (1) a likelihood of success on the merits; (2) irreparable harm absent relief; (3) that the balance of harms favors the movant; and (4) that the public interest weighs in favor of relief. We map each factor to the facts, treating the irreparable-harm analysis as bifurcated — harm flowing from covenant breach distinct from harm flowing from trade-secret misappropriation.

**1. Likelihood of Success.** Moderate to strong. On the covenant theory, Parts IV.A and IV.B support a conclusion that MNAA validity holds (with a modest risk on § 24L(b)(vii)) and that common-law reasonableness holds on all factors except geographic scope. Blue-pencil reformation at the hearing or at final judgment is the predictable disposition. On the DTSA/MUTSA theory (Part IV.E), likelihood is high for return-of-materials relief and moderate-to-high for use-restriction relief against specifically-identified trade-secret files.

**2. Irreparable Harm — Two Streams.**

*Covenant-breach harm.* Loss of customer goodwill at the three named Q2-at-risk accounts (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech), exposure of the $14M ARR book to personalized selling against Halston by Narayanan at Kettlepoint, and the impossibility of accurate post-hoc damages calculation for customer-relationship defection. The Supreme Judicial Court and Superior Courts have repeatedly recognized loss of customer goodwill as quintessentially irreparable. *See* *Marine Contractors*, 365 Mass. at 288 (goodwill protection).

*Trade-secret misappropriation harm.* Independently dispositive. The 3/28/2026 USB-copy events involve directories facially consistent with protectable trade-secret content — forward-looking pipeline data and ForecastIQ algorithm tuning parameters. Once those items are exposed to a competitor, their non-public economic value is permanently destroyed; no monetary remedy is adequate. *See* 18 U.S.C. § 1839(3) (trade-secret definition). This stream stands alone as a sufficient predicate for preliminary relief even if the covenant-breach stream is discounted.

**Holding: irreparable harm is robust, driven primarily by the trade-secret stream.**

**3. Balance of Harms.** Moderate, tilting toward Halston. Narayanan faces deferred income from Kettlepoint during the 12-month restricted period, but Halston's 40% garden-leave payment — approximately $118,000 annualized on $295,000 base — materially mitigates. She remains free to work in any non-retail-forecasting role and in retail-forecasting roles outside the reformed footprint. On the trade-secret theory, the burden on Narayanan of a return-of-materials and use-restriction order is trivial: she has no legitimate interest in retaining or using Halston's proprietary data at Kettlepoint. **Holding: favors Halston modestly on covenant theory; favors Halston strongly on trade-secret theory.**

**4. Public Interest.** Massachusetts policy post-MNAA supports enforcement of validly-noticed, garden-leave-compensated, reasonably-tailored covenants. *See* *Boulanger*, 442 Mass. at 639–40. Public interest more clearly favors Halston on the trade-secret theory: Massachusetts (MUTSA) and federal (DTSA) policy uniformly disfavor misappropriation of proprietary business data. **Holding: favors Halston across both theories.**

**Overall PI Posture.** Substantial likelihood of meaningful preliminary relief, strongest under a combined theory. A court is likely to (i) reform the covenant geographically and enforce it as reformed against Narayanan's Kettlepoint role; (ii) order return of USB-copied materials; and (iii) impose a use-restriction on the USB-copied material at Kettlepoint.

### E. Parallel Trade-Secret Theory — DTSA and MUTSA

Independent of the covenant, the USB-copy forensic findings support standalone injunctive relief.

**1. DTSA — 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq.** The DTSA creates a federal civil cause of action for misappropriation of a "trade secret" as defined at 18 U.S.C. § 1839(3) — information that (a) the owner has taken reasonable measures to protect and (b) derives independent economic value from not being generally known or readily ascertainable. Both prongs are present here. Halston's Schedule A enumerates categories (pipeline reports, SSP models, win/loss data, ForecastIQ parameters) that satisfy the trade-secret definition categorically. Halston's use of confidentiality agreements, access controls, and schedule-based protection of sensitive categories satisfies the "reasonable measures" prong. *See* *Jazz Pharm., Inc. v. Synchrony Grp., LLC*, 343 F. Supp. 3d 434, 446 (E.D. Pa. 2018) (reasonable measures inquiry).

Misappropriation under § 1839(5) includes "acquisition of a trade secret of another by a person who knows or has reason to know that the trade secret was acquired by improper means," as well as unauthorized disclosure or use. The USB-copy events to non-Halston devices six days before resignation provide strong circumstantial evidence of acquisition by improper means. The three February–March 2026 "pipeline strategy" sessions focused on the retail accounts Kettlepoint now targets supply evidence of intended use.

DTSA authorizes preliminary and permanent injunctive relief, § 1836(b)(3)(A), and — in extraordinary circumstances — an ex parte civil seizure, § 1836(b)(2). A seizure order is **almost certainly not warranted** on the current record. Section 1836(b)(2) requires the movant to show, inter alia, that a temporary restraining order under Fed. R. Civ. P. 65 would be inadequate because the defendant would "destroy, move, hide, or otherwise make such matter inaccessible to the court." Narayanan has already returned the laptop; we do not yet have evidence of active concealment; and the USB devices are presumably in her control at her Providence home. A standard TRO and ordinary injunctive relief under § 1836(b)(3)(A) is adequate. We should preserve the seizure option as a contingency (e.g., if discovery surfaces evidence of further exfiltration or active destruction) but should not lead with it.

**2. MUTSA — G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G.** MUTSA substantially mirrors DTSA and provides for injunctive relief under § 42A(a), including relief against "actual or threatened misappropriation." The USB-copy events satisfy "threatened misappropriation" at minimum and arguably "actual misappropriation" depending on contents developed in discovery. State-court venue in Suffolk Superior Court is coordinate with covenant enforcement and preserves the existing Massachusetts forum posture. Combined pleading under MUTSA and the covenant in state court is procedurally efficient.

**3. Venue Choice.** State-court filing with combined MUTSA and covenant theories is procedurally cleanest. DTSA can be added via amended complaint if federal venue becomes preferable (e.g., for broader discovery mechanisms under Fed. R. Civ. P. 26, for DTSA-specific remedies, or if Kettlepoint is named as a co-defendant). We should preserve DTSA by explicit reference in a filed pleading before the DTSA's three-year statute of limitations, § 1836(d), but that is a contingency concern only.

**Holding: the trade-secret theory supports independent preliminary injunctive relief; return-of-materials and use-restriction relief are highly likely; seizure under § 1836(b)(2) is not warranted on current facts; the theory is the strongest near-term basis for relief and should anchor the PI motion.**

## V. Recommendation

Four actionable paths, in ranked order of recommended primacy. The first three are genuinely actionable; the fourth is a null option included for completeness.

**1. Combined PI Motion (Covenant + DTSA/MUTSA) — Recommended Primary Path.** File in Suffolk Superior Court seeking (a) preliminary enforcement of the covenant as drafted, with an express blue-pencil fallback to Halston's actual geographic footprint (US states and Canadian provinces with booked revenue in the 24 months preceding termination) and implicit fallback to the retail-forecasting vertical; and (b) parallel MUTSA relief in the form of (i) a return-of-materials order for the USB-copied directories, (ii) a use-restriction covering the specific copied content, (iii) forensic-preservation requirements on Narayanan and Kettlepoint devices, and (iv) ordinary discovery obligations. Plead DTSA in federal-court filing reservation; add via amended complaint if federal venue becomes preferable.

*Rationale.* The trade-secret theory backstops covenant risk. Blue-pencil fallback preempts voiding. The combined filing is the fullest expression of the $14M ARR and three-named-account exposure. Estimated cost: $75,000 through hearing (consistent with partner estimate). Estimated timeline: three weeks to hearing. Estimated probability of some form of relief: 65–75%.

**2. Narrower TRO on Trade-Secret Theory Only — Recommended Cost-Sensitive Fallback.** File a TRO in Suffolk Superior Court seeking only MUTSA relief (return-of-materials, use-restriction, forensic preservation), omitting covenant enforcement. Sidesteps MNAA litigation risk and blue-pencil overhang; lowest-cost path to meaningful relief on the core forensic exposure; preserves covenant enforcement for later. Estimated cost: $30,000–$40,000. Probability of some form of trade-secret relief: 75–85%. Does not address the broader Kettlepoint-role exposure.

**3. Negotiated Standstill Toward a Customer-Specific Non-Solicit — Recommended Parallel Track.** In parallel with (1) or (2), open direct communication with Narayanan's counsel and Kettlepoint's legal department seeking a customer-specific non-solicit covering Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, and Quellstream Biotech for the balance of the 12-month restricted period, plus return-and-destruction of USB-copied material, in exchange for release of broader claims. This path directly protects the three named at-risk accounts — the core Q2 revenue exposure — at substantially lower cost (estimated $15,000–$25,000 in negotiation fees), preserves the broader Massachusetts tech-ecosystem relationship, and avoids a public filing that would attract press attention. This path is consistent with Client Objective #5.

**4. Stand Down and Monitor.** Not recommended. The 3/28/2026 USB-copy events make pure inaction a materially disproportionate response. At minimum, send a preservation letter under Fed. R. Civ. P. 37(e) and common law; make a formal assertion of rights; and put Kettlepoint on notice of the trade-secret concerns.

*Implementation sequencing recommendation.* Commence Path 3 (negotiated standstill) immediately and on a parallel track to Path 1 or 2, so that the partner has settlement optionality preserved without slipping past the window for expedited PI filing.
