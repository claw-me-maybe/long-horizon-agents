## I. Question Presented

Whether the 12-month non-competition covenant that Priya Narayanan executed on June 1, 2024 in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales — restricting her for twelve months post-termination from working for any business offering demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers anywhere in the United States and Canada — is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, and the common-law reasonableness standard, such that Halston Analytics can obtain a preliminary injunction in Suffolk Superior Court barring her April 20, 2026 start at Kettlepoint AI; and whether, independent of the covenant, the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events support parallel injunctive relief under the Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq., and the Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G.

## II. Brief Answer

The 12-month covenant is likely enforceable in substantially its current form as to Kettlepoint AI's retail-forecasting business, though the "United States and Canada" geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk that Massachusetts courts will almost certainly resolve by blue-penciling rather than voiding. The stronger near-term injunctive theory, however, rests on trade-secret misappropriation under DTSA and MUTSA: the March 28, 2026 USB-copy events — involving "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning" directories transferred to non-company devices six days before resignation — support irreparable-harm findings and return-of-materials relief independent of any MNAA analysis.

Recommended path: a combined preliminary-injunction motion coupling covenant enforcement (with an express blue-pencil fallback) and DTSA/MUTSA relief, filed on a three-week expedited track. Probability of some meaningful injunctive relief: substantial (estimated 65–75%); probability of enforcement of the covenant exactly as drafted: modest (estimated 30–40%) given the geographic overbreadth.

## III. Statement of Facts

Narayanan joined Halston Analytics, Inc. ("Halston"), a Delaware-incorporated B2B forecasting company headquartered in Cambridge, Massachusetts, on February 1, 2022 as Senior Director, Enterprise Sales. Effective June 1, 2024 she was promoted to VP of Enterprise Sales; base salary increased from $215,000 to $285,000 and later (effective January 1, 2026) to $295,000. The promotion was accompanied by a $90,000 retention bonus.

The non-compete at issue was presented by offer letter dated May 17, 2024 and executed on June 1, 2024 — ten business days later. The operative provisions, verbatim, are as follows.

> §2.1 *Restricted Period.* For a period of twelve (12) months following termination of Employee's employment for any reason, Employee shall not, directly or indirectly, engage in, own, manage, operate, consult for, or be employed by any Competing Business within the Restricted Territory.
>
> §2.2 *Competing Business.* "Competing Business" means any business that offers demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers.
>
> §2.3 *Restricted Territory.* "Restricted Territory" means the United States and Canada.
>
> §3.1 *Garden Leave Consideration.* During the Restricted Period, the Company shall pay Employee, on a monthly basis, an amount equal to forty percent (40%) of Employee's highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination.
>
> §7.2 *Choice of Law / Forum.* This Agreement shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the Commonwealth of Massachusetts. The parties consent to exclusive jurisdiction and venue in the Suffolk County Superior Court or the U.S. District Court for the District of Massachusetts.

Narayanan resigned on April 3, 2026 with ten business days' notice; her last day of employment was April 17, 2026. Kettlepoint AI — a direct competitor in the retail-demand-forecasting vertical — announced Narayanan's hire as "Head of Revenue" by press release on April 15, 2026, with a start date of April 20, 2026.

Narayanan is a Providence, Rhode Island resident. She worked three days per week from Halston's Cambridge office and two days per week remotely from her Providence home. Her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record.

On April 7, 2026, Halston's IT Security, acting under in-house counsel direction, forensically imaged Narayanan's returned laptop. The imaging revealed two USB-copy events on March 28, 2026 (six days before resignation) involving directories named "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning." The USB device serial numbers do not match any company-issued device. Two of Narayanan's former direct reports have separately reported that she led three "pipeline strategy" sessions in February–March 2026 focused specifically on the retail-vertical accounts that Kettlepoint now targets. Halston's Schedule A to the agreement identifies as protected trade-secret or confidential information: customer pipeline reports (current and forward-looking), SSP allocation models, win/loss and competitive-intelligence data, and proprietary "ForecastIQ" algorithm parameters and training datasets.

## IV. Discussion

This analysis separates MNAA threshold statutory validity (Part IV.A) from the common-law reasonableness overlay of *Boulanger* and *Marine Contractors* (Part IV.B). A covenant can be statutorily valid yet unreasonable, or reasonable in principle yet statutorily non-compliant; both layers must hold. Part IV.C addresses choice of law under § 24L(e). Part IV.D applies the *Packaging Industries* PI test. Part IV.E addresses the parallel DTSA/MUTSA theory.

### A. MNAA Statutory Validity (G.L. c. 149, § 24L)

**1. Ten-Business-Day Notice (§ 24L(b)(i)).** For covenants entered after the commencement of employment, § 24L(b)(i) requires the employer to provide the agreement "at least 10 business days before the agreement is to be effective." Here, delivery was May 17, 2024 and execution was June 1, 2024 — exactly ten business days (excluding Memorial Day, Monday May 27, 2024). The statute's "at least 10 business days before" wording is exactingly counted; we should confirm the calendar count in an affidavit of the GC or HR custodian before filing. On the facts represented, the requirement is satisfied. **Holding: element satisfied, subject to calendar verification.**

**2. Independent Consideration for a Post-Hire Agreement (§ 24L(b)(ii)).** Because the agreement was executed on June 1, 2024 — after Narayanan's February 1, 2022 hire — § 24L(b)(ii) requires that the covenant be "supported by fair and reasonable consideration independent from the continuation of employment." The June 2024 promotion elevated Narayanan from Senior Director to VP of Enterprise Sales, raised her base salary by $70,000 (roughly 33%), and was accompanied by a $90,000 retention bonus paid contemporaneously with execution. Independently of salary, the VP title carried expanded authority, pipeline access, and the Schedule A data rights that are the predicate of the covenant.

Pre-MNAA Massachusetts law, culminating in *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797, 803–05 (2020), applied a common-law "material change" doctrine requiring new consideration when the employment relationship changed materially. *Automile* is informative but not controlling here: the doctrine it refined is now largely subsumed and displaced by § 24L(b)(ii), which supplies a statutory independent-consideration requirement rather than relying on the material-change rubric. For our 2024 agreement, we analyze under § 24L(b)(ii) directly; *Automile* reasoning is persuasive chiefly to the extent it illuminates what sorts of promotion-linked consideration Massachusetts has historically treated as sufficient — and by any measure, a title promotion plus $70,000 salary increase plus a $90,000 retention bonus clear that bar. **Holding: element satisfied.**

**3. Garden Leave or Other Mutually-Agreed-Upon Consideration (§ 24L(b)(vii)).** Section 24L(b)(vii) requires either (i) a garden-leave clause paying at least 50% of the employee's highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination, pro rata on a monthly schedule, or (ii) "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" specified in the agreement. Section 3.1 provides 40% monthly — below the 50% statutory floor standing alone. The question is whether the 40% payment combined with the $90,000 retention bonus and the promotion itself qualifies as "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration."

The statute does not quantify what "other" consideration suffices, and there is limited appellate authority. Massachusetts practitioners (including our firm's 11/2023 memo on post-MNAA enforcement trends) have generally read § 24L(b)(vii) disjunctively: either the 50% payment or some other specified consideration bargained for and recited. Here, the retention bonus and the promotion were plainly bargained for and documented, and Section 3.1 supplements with a 40% monthly payment. A court could nonetheless read § 24L(b)(vii) as setting a 50% floor that "other consideration" supplements rather than displaces. The conservative view is that this element is satisfied but not unassailable; it is our second-most vulnerable MNAA issue after geographic scope.

The two-year lookback in Section 3.1 tracks the statute's reference to "the two-year period preceding termination" and is not a separate issue. **Holding: element likely satisfied; modest litigation risk.**

**4. Twelve-Month Duration (§ 24L(b)(iv)).** The covenant's 12-month term sits at the statutory cap. Duration at the cap is presumptively valid. Reasonableness as applied can still be probed, but is rarely an independent ground for voiding a covenant at the statutory ceiling where the role warrants protection. **Holding: element satisfied.**

**5. Geographic Scope (§ 24L(b)(v)).** Section 24L(b)(v) requires geographic scope "reasonable in relation to the interests protected." Section 2.3 restricts Narayanan throughout "the United States and Canada." Per client data, Halston has booked revenue in only twenty-nine US states and one Canadian province over the trailing 24 months. A "US and Canada" restriction sweeps far beyond Halston's actual commercial footprint and is facially overbroad as applied.

Massachusetts courts prefer reformation to voiding. *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773, 778 (1974), establishes that a court may modify an overbroad covenant to the extent necessary to protect legitimate interests without penalizing employees beyond those interests. Although *All Stainless* predates the MNAA, its reformation rule survives and is routinely applied to MNAA-era agreements; nothing in § 24L displaces the equitable reformation power. We should nonetheless not take reformation for granted: a minority of post-MNAA decisions have suggested that egregiously overbroad drafting can evidence bad-faith overreach, weighing against equitable modification. A 54-jurisdiction restriction on behalf of a firm with a presence in 30 is not plausibly egregious; it is common drafter-overreach and is the paradigmatic candidate for reformation.

The likely reformed scope: the US states and Canadian provinces in which Halston has booked revenue in the 24 months preceding termination, further narrowed (at least effectively) to the retail-forecasting vertical in which Narayanan operated and in which Kettlepoint competes. **Holding: substantial reformation risk; highly unlikely to void; probable blue-pencil to Halston's actual footprint.**

**6. Activity Scope (§ 24L(b)(vi)).** "Demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers" closely tracks Halston's business and Narayanan's VP portfolio. The restriction is narrower than a blanket "competitor" prohibition and maps to specific verticals. **Holding: element satisfied.**

### B. Common-Law Reasonableness (Boulanger, Marine Contractors)

Even after clearing MNAA validity, a covenant must satisfy the common-law standard articulated in *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635, 639 (2004), and *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280, 287–88 (1974): the covenant must protect a legitimate business interest; be reasonable in time, space, and activity; and be consonant with the public interest.

**Legitimate Business Interest.** *Marine Contractors* recognizes trade secrets, confidential information, and customer goodwill, not protection from ordinary competition. Halston's Schedule A identifies paradigmatic protectable interests — customer pipeline data, SSP models, win/loss and competitive intelligence, and proprietary ForecastIQ parameters and training data. Narayanan, as VP of Enterprise Sales, had direct access to and developed goodwill against customer relationships, squarely fitting the *Marine Contractors* paradigm. **Holding: strong.**

**Time.** Twelve months at the MNAA cap is reasonable in relation to the interests protected; the competitive half-life of current-quarter pipeline and forecast-tuning parameters is plausibly within that window. **Holding: reasonable.**

**Space.** Reasonable as reformed (see Part IV.A.5); unreasonable as drafted. **Holding: reasonable only after reformation.**

**Public Interest.** Massachusetts policy after the MNAA favors enforcement of properly-tailored, garden-leave-compensated covenants. The public interest is neutral or mildly favorable to Halston; it is not hostile to enforcement of the covenant as reformed. **Holding: consonant with public interest.**

### C. Choice of Law and § 24L(e) Vulnerability

Section 24L(e) voids choice-of-law provisions that would avoid application of the MNAA where the employee is, "for at least 30 days immediately preceding termination of employment, a resident of or employed in the commonwealth." Narayanan is a Rhode Island resident but worked three days per week at Halston's Cambridge office. The statutory disjunction ("resident of or employed in") means she need only be "employed in" Massachusetts for the 30-day window — and on the representations made, she was, three days per week.

The factual record is not yet ironclad. We should develop, before filing: timesheet records showing in-office presence for March 18 – April 17, 2026; expense reports or badge-in logs; and email metadata or similar markers of work location. *Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462, 469–71 (2018), cautions that Massachusetts courts will not apply Massachusetts law (or a Massachusetts forum) where doing so would circumvent the more protective law of the employee's "home" jurisdiction. Rhode Island's own non-compete statute, R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-59-1 et seq., is materially more restrictive — it prohibits non-competes against non-exempt employees, undergraduate/graduate students, employees under 18, and certain low-wage workers. Narayanan (a VP earning $295,000) is not within the Rhode Island statute's protected classes, so the *Oxford Global* defensive invocation is weak. Nonetheless, the factual predicate for § 24L(e) applicability should be firmed up pre-filing. **Holding: § 24L(e) likely supports application of Massachusetts law; factual development required.**

### D. Preliminary Injunction (Packaging Industries)

*Packaging Indus. Grp., Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609, 617 (1980), applies a four-factor test: (1) likelihood of success on the merits; (2) risk of irreparable harm absent relief; (3) balance of harms; (4) public interest.

**1. Likelihood of Success.** Moderate to strong. On the covenant theory, we expect MNAA validity to hold, reasonableness to hold on all dimensions except geographic scope, and a reformed covenant to issue. On the DTSA/MUTSA theory (see Part IV.E), likelihood is strong for return-of-materials relief and at least moderate for activity-based injunctive relief tied to the USB-copied material.

**2. Irreparable Harm.** Strong and bifurcated. Two distinct irreparable-harm streams:

- *Covenant breach harm.* Loss of customer goodwill, inability to monetize post-hoc the defection of the three at-risk accounts (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech), and the $14M ARR book exposed to Narayanan's personal selling against Halston at Kettlepoint. Damages calculation for lost customer relationships is notoriously difficult — the paradigmatic irreparable-harm case.
- *Trade-secret misappropriation harm.* The March 28, 2026 USB-copy events create a distinct, more acute, and more quantifiable irreparable-harm basis. Once ForecastIQ algorithm parameters and current-quarter pipeline data are exposed, the competitive value cannot be restored; monetary damages are inadequate. This prong is independently dispositive of the irreparable-harm factor.

**3. Balance of Harms.** Moderate, tilting toward Halston. Narayanan faces lost compensation from Kettlepoint, but the 40% garden-leave payment (approximately $118,000 annualized on $295,000) materially mitigates. She retains freedom to work in any non-retail-forecasting role and to work in retail-forecasting roles outside Halston's footprint. The balance tilts modestly toward Halston and more strongly toward Halston on the trade-secret theory, which implicates only return-of-materials relief that imposes trivial burden.

**4. Public Interest.** Neutral to favorable. Massachusetts policy post-MNAA favors enforcement of validly-noticed, garden-leave-compensated, and reasonably-tailored covenants. Public interest more clearly favors Halston on the trade-secret theory: Massachusetts and federal policy disfavor trade-secret misappropriation without qualification.

**Holding: substantial likelihood of some form of injunctive relief; strongest under a combined covenant-plus-trade-secret theory.**

### E. Parallel DTSA and MUTSA Theory

Independent of the covenant, the 3/28/2026 USB-copy events support standalone injunctive relief.

**DTSA (18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq.).** The statute creates a federal civil cause of action for misappropriation of a "trade secret" — information the owner has taken reasonable measures to protect and that derives independent economic value from non-public status. 18 U.S.C. § 1839(3). The Schedule A information (ForecastIQ parameters, pipeline reports, SSP models, win/loss data) satisfies the trade-secret definition on its face; Halston's access controls and the agreement's confidentiality regime satisfy the reasonable-measures prong. Misappropriation requires "acquisition . . . by a person who knows or has reason to know that the trade secret was acquired by improper means," § 1839(5), or disclosure/use after acquisition. The USB-copy events to non-company devices six days before resignation provide strong circumstantial evidence of acquisition by improper means.

DTSA authorizes injunctive relief, § 1836(b)(3)(A), and in extraordinary circumstances an ex parte civil seizure, § 1836(b)(2). A seizure motion is almost certainly **not** warranted here — the statute requires a showing that ordinary injunctive relief would be inadequate, which is difficult given that Narayanan has already returned the laptop. We should preserve the seizure option for contingencies (e.g., evidence of further exfiltration) rather than lead with it.

**MUTSA (G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G).** MUTSA mirrors DTSA substantially and provides for injunctive relief under § 42A, including actual or threatened misappropriation. The USB-copy events support "threatened misappropriation" at minimum. State-court venue may be preferable for coordinate pursuit of covenant enforcement; federal venue permits DTSA-specific remedies.

**Holding: trade-secret theory supports independent preliminary injunctive relief; return-of-materials and use-restriction relief available; seizure under § 1836(b)(2) likely not warranted on current facts but should be preserved.**

## V. Recommendation

Three actionable paths, in ranked order of recommended primacy:

**1. Combined PI Motion (Covenant + DTSA/MUTSA) — Recommended Primary Path.** File in Suffolk Superior Court seeking (a) preliminary enforcement of the covenant as written, with an express blue-pencil fallback to Halston's actual geographic footprint and the retail-forecasting vertical; and (b) parallel DTSA and MUTSA relief in the form of a return-of-materials order, a use-restriction covering the USB-copied material, and standard discovery-preservation requirements. Rationale: trade-secret theory backstops covenant risk; blue-pencil fallback preempts voiding; the combined filing is the strongest expression of the $14M ARR and three-named-account exposure. Estimated cost: $75,000 through hearing. Estimated timeline: three weeks to hearing.

**2. Narrower TRO on Trade-Secret Theory Only — Recommended Fallback.** If the partner or client prefers a lower-cost, faster, and narrower posture — or wishes to preserve the covenant-enforcement option for later — file a TRO in Suffolk Superior Court seeking only DTSA and MUTSA relief (return of materials, use-restriction, forensic preservation). This avoids MNAA litigation risk and the blue-pencil overhang, and can be combined later with a fuller covenant-enforcement motion. Estimated cost: $30,000–$40,000. Leaves covenant claim available for later pleading.

**3. Negotiated Standstill Toward a Customer-Specific Non-Solicit — Recommended Settlement Posture.** In parallel with (1) or (2), open a channel to Narayanan's counsel and Kettlepoint's legal department seeking a customer-specific non-solicit covering Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, and Quellstream Biotech for the balance of the 12-month period, plus return-and-destruction of USB-copied material, in exchange for release of broader claims. This path protects the three named at-risk accounts — the core Q2 revenue exposure — at substantially lower cost and with materially less relationship risk with the broader Massachusetts tech ecosystem.

**4. Stand Down and Monitor.** Not recommended on present facts. The USB-copy forensic findings make standing down a disproportionate response; at minimum, a preservation letter and an assertion of rights are warranted.
