## I. Question Presented

Whether the 12-month non-competition covenant executed by Priya Narayanan on June 1, 2024 in connection with her promotion to VP of Enterprise Sales — purporting to bar her, throughout the United States and Canada, from working for any business offering demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers — is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, so as to support a preliminary injunction in Suffolk Superior Court against her April 20, 2026 start at Kettlepoint AI.

## II. Brief Answer

Likely yes, in substantial part. The 6/1/2024 agreement appears to satisfy the threshold validity requirements of § 24L — 10-business-day notice, independent consideration (promotion plus $90,000 retention bonus), garden leave at 40% of the two-year-lookback base salary, and a 12-month term at the statutory cap. The legitimate business interest (trade secrets, customer goodwill, ForecastIQ algorithm) is well-supported. The principal vulnerability is the "United States and Canada" geographic scope, which is facially broad and probably will be reformed (blue-penciled) rather than voided. On the recommended procedural path, a preliminary injunction is feasible but the stronger near-term vehicle — given the 3/28/2026 USB-copy events — is a combined covenant-plus-trade-secret motion. Probability of some form of injunctive relief: substantial.

## III. Statement of Facts

Narayanan was hired by Halston Analytics, Inc. ("Halston") on February 1, 2022 as Senior Director, Enterprise Sales. She was promoted to VP of Enterprise Sales effective June 1, 2024, with her base salary raised from $215,000 to $285,000 (subsequently increased to $295,000 on January 1, 2026). In connection with the promotion, Halston paid a $90,000 retention bonus and required execution of the non-compete at issue. The offer letter attaching the non-compete was delivered on May 17, 2024; Narayanan signed on June 1, 2024 — 10 business days after delivery.

Section 2.1 of the agreement prohibits Narayanan, "[f]or a period of twelve (12) months following termination of Employee's employment for any reason, . . . directly or indirectly, [from] engag[ing] in, own[ing], manag[ing], operat[ing], consult[ing] for, or be[ing] employed by any Competing Business within the Restricted Territory." Section 2.2 defines "Competing Business" as "any business that offers demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers." Section 2.3 defines "Restricted Territory" as "the United States and Canada." Section 3.1 requires Halston to pay, during the Restricted Period, a monthly amount equal to 40% of Narayanan's highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination. Section 7.2 selects Massachusetts law and Suffolk County courts.

Narayanan resigned on April 3, 2026, providing ten business days' notice. Her last day was April 17, 2026. Kettlepoint AI — a direct competitor in the retail-demand-forecasting vertical — publicly announced her hire as "Head of Revenue" on April 15, 2026, with a start date of April 20, 2026.

Narayanan is a Rhode Island resident (Providence). She worked three days per week from Halston's Cambridge office and two days per week remotely from Providence. Her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record.

On April 7, 2026, Halston's IT Security, acting under in-house counsel direction, conducted forensic imaging of Narayanan's returned laptop. The imaging revealed two USB-copy events on March 28, 2026 — six days before her resignation — involving directories labeled "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning." The USB device serial numbers do not match any Halston-issued device. Two of Narayanan's former direct reports have stated that she led three "pipeline strategy" sessions in February and March 2026 focused on the retail-vertical accounts Kettlepoint now targets.

## IV. Discussion

### A. MNAA Threshold Validity

The MNAA, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, governs agreements entered on or after October 1, 2018 and imposes several threshold requirements. The 6/1/2024 agreement is squarely within its scope.

**1. Notice and 10-Business-Day Rule (§ 24L(b)(i)).** For covenants entered after commencement of employment, § 24L(b)(i) requires that the agreement be provided "at least 10 business days before the agreement is to be effective." The offer letter attaching the covenant was delivered May 17, 2024; Narayanan executed it June 1, 2024, exactly 10 business days later. This is a close call on the face of the statute, because the statutory floor is "at least 10 business days before" — the execution itself must fall on or after the tenth business day. On the facts as represented, the count appears to satisfy the rule, but we should verify by calendar count (including federal holidays falling between 5/17 and 6/1/2024). **Conclusion: likely satisfied, subject to calendar confirmation.**

**2. Independent Consideration for a Promotion-Triggered Agreement (§ 24L(b)(ii)).** Because the 6/1/2024 agreement was entered after Narayanan's initial hire, § 24L(b)(ii) requires that it be "supported by fair and reasonable consideration independent from the continuation of employment." The promotion from Senior Director to VP — accompanied by a $70,000 base-salary increase and a $90,000 retention bonus — easily satisfies this requirement on its face. Pre-MNAA case law, most notably *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797 (2020), developed a "material change" doctrine requiring new consideration when the employment relationship materially changed; *Automile* is instructive but not controlling for agreements post-dating 10/1/2018, which are governed directly by § 24L(b)(ii). **Conclusion: satisfied.**

**3. Garden Leave / Other Mutually-Agreed-Upon Consideration (§ 24L(b)(vii)).** The statute requires a garden-leave clause paying at least 50% of the employee's highest annualized base salary during the two years preceding termination, or "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration." Section 3.1 of the agreement provides for 40% — below the 50% floor, but expressly framed as negotiated consideration. Massachusetts practitioners have generally read § 24L(b)(vii) to allow below-50% payment where the agreement reflects bargained-for consideration; here, the $90,000 retention bonus and the promotion itself arguably constitute the "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" contemplated by the statute, and the 40% monthly payment supplements rather than substitutes for that consideration. The two-year lookback tracks the statutory language. **Conclusion: satisfied, though a court could scrutinize the 40% figure; the retention bonus and promotion are the stronger support.**

**4. Twelve-Month Duration (§ 24L(b)(iv)).** The covenant's 12-month term sits at the statutory cap. At the cap, duration is presumptively valid; reasonableness as applied still matters but is rarely a standalone ground for voiding. **Conclusion: satisfied.**

### B. Reasonableness (Common Law Overlay: Boulanger, Marine Contractors)

Even where a covenant clears MNAA thresholds, it must satisfy the common-law reasonableness standard articulated in *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635, 639 (2004), and *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280, 287–88 (1974): the covenant must protect a legitimate business interest, be reasonable in time and space, and consonant with the public interest.

**1. Legitimate Business Interest.** *Marine Contractors* recognizes protection of trade secrets, confidential information, and customer goodwill — not protection from ordinary competition. Halston's Schedule A identifies customer pipeline reports, SSP allocation models, win/loss data, and ForecastIQ algorithm parameters as protectable. Narayanan held a revenue-leading role with direct pipeline access; her accumulated customer goodwill in the retail vertical is precisely the kind of interest *Marine Contractors* protects. **Conclusion: satisfied.**

**2. Geographic Scope: "United States and Canada."** This is the covenant's principal vulnerability. On the facts presented, Halston has booked revenue in only a subset of US states over the relevant period. A "US and Canada" restriction is likely facially overbroad as applied to a firm with a narrower footprint. Under *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974), Massachusetts courts prefer reformation (blue-penciling) to voiding. A court will likely narrow the territory to the states and provinces in which Halston has booked revenue, or to the retail-forecasting vertical where Narayanan directly operated. **Conclusion: substantial reformation risk; unlikely to void outright.**

**3. Activity Scope.** "Demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers" tracks Halston's business and Narayanan's portfolio. Reasonable as applied. **Conclusion: satisfied.**

### C. Likelihood of Success on PI Motion (Packaging Industries)

*Packaging Indus. Grp., Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609, 617 (1980), sets the Massachusetts PI standard: (1) likelihood of success on the merits; (2) irreparable harm absent relief; (3) balance of harms; (4) public interest.

**1. Likelihood of Success.** Moderate to strong. The covenant likely clears MNAA validity; reasonableness is strong on legitimate interest and activity scope but vulnerable on geography. A court is more likely to enforce a reformed covenant than the instrument as drafted.

**2. Irreparable Harm.** Strong. The $14M ARR book, the three named at-risk accounts (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech), and the 3/28/2026 USB-copy events together present irreparable harm both from covenant breach (loss of customer goodwill, inability to monetarily remedy lost contracts) and from potential trade-secret misappropriation. The forensic evidence materially strengthens this prong.

**3. Balance of Harms.** Moderate. Narayanan faces income loss, but Halston's garden-leave payment (40% base) materially mitigates. The balance tilts toward Halston.

**4. Public Interest.** Neutral to favoring Halston. Massachusetts policy, post-MNAA, favors enforcement of properly-tailored covenants with garden-leave compensation; it disfavors overbroad restraints.

**Conclusion: substantial likelihood of success on a PI motion, particularly if combined with a trade-secret theory addressing the USB-copy events.**

## V. Recommendation

Three actionable paths, ranked by cost and expected recovery:

1. **Combined PI Motion (covenant + trade-secret).** File in Suffolk Superior Court seeking enforcement of the covenant (with blue-pencil fallback on geographic scope) and parallel injunctive relief under DTSA and MUTSA based on the USB-copy events. Strongest leverage. Cost: ~$75K through hearing. Probability of some form of relief: substantial.

2. **Narrower TRO on Trade-Secret Theory Only.** File a narrower TRO relying solely on DTSA/MUTSA misappropriation, seeking immediate forensic-preservation and return-of-materials relief without the covenant-enforcement overlay. Lower-cost, faster, and sidesteps MNAA litigation risk. Preserves covenant-enforcement option for later.

3. **Negotiated Standstill.** Open a channel to Kettlepoint and Narayanan's counsel seeking a negotiated customer-specific non-solicit covering Brinkerhoff, Meridian, and Quellstream for the remaining covenant period, in exchange for a release of broader claims. Lowest cost; preserves client relationship optionality.
