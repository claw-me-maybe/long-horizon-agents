# Office Memorandum — Enforceability of Non-Compete Against Former VP Under Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act

## Occupation
- **SOC:** 23-1011
- **Title:** Lawyers

## Scenario
You are a fourth-year associate at Thorne & Whitfield LLP (Boston, MA) representing Halston Analytics, Inc., a Delaware-incorporated B2B forecasting company headquartered in Cambridge. On April 3, 2026, Halston's former VP of Enterprise Sales, Priya Narayanan, resigned and — twelve days later — accepted a "Head of Revenue" role at Kettlepoint AI, a direct competitor in the retail-demand-forecasting vertical. Halston's General Counsel, Dana Shaughnessy, has asked for an internal office memorandum by Monday 4/27/2026 analyzing whether Narayanan's 12-month non-compete is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, so that the litigation partner (Marisol Reyes) can decide by week's end whether to file for a preliminary injunction in Suffolk Superior Court. The stakes: Narayanan reports to manage a book of roughly $14M ARR, and Halston's Q2 forecast assumes at least three named accounts (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech) do not churn in the next 90 days.

## Inputs

**Key facts (from client intake call 4/16/26 and document review):**
- Narayanan was hired 2/1/2022 as Senior Director, Enterprise Sales.
- Promoted to VP of Enterprise Sales on 6/1/2024, with base salary raised from $215,000 to $285,000 (subsequently to $295,000 on 1/1/2026).
- The non-compete at issue was executed on **6/1/2024 in connection with the promotion and a $90,000 retention bonus** — not her original hire. The offer letter attaching the non-compete was delivered 5/17/2024; the non-compete was signed 6/1/2024 (10 business days after delivery).
- Narayanan resigned on 4/3/2026 with 10 business days' notice; last day was 4/17/2026.
- Kettlepoint AI announced her hire via press release on 4/15/2026; start date 4/20/2026.
- Narayanan was a Massachusetts resident and worked out of the Cambridge office 3 days/week; remote from her Providence, RI home 2 days/week. Her employment agreement lists Cambridge as her office of record.
- At exit, Narayanan returned her laptop; forensic imaging (performed 4/7/2026 by Halston's IT Security under in-house counsel direction) shows two USB-copy events on 3/28/2026 (6 days before resignation): directory names "Pipeline_Q2" and "ForecastIQ_tuning." USB device serial numbers do not match any company-issued device.
- Halston has spoken to two of Narayanan's former direct reports, who state she held three "pipeline strategy" sessions in February–March 2026 focused specifically on the retail-vertical accounts that Kettlepoint now targets.

**Controlling authority and standards (partial list — not exhaustive):**
- **G.L. c. 149, § 24L** (Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act) — effective for agreements entered on or after 10/1/2018. Governs: notice requirements (§ 24L(b)(i)), independent consideration for agreements post-dating employment (§ 24L(b)(ii)), 12-month duration cap (§ 24L(b)(iv)), reasonableness of geographic and activity scope (§ 24L(b)(v)–(vi)), garden-leave or "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" (§ 24L(b)(vii)), and choice-of-law / forum restrictions (§ 24L(e)).
- *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797 (2020) — pre-MNAA "material change" doctrine; still informs promotion-triggered agreement analysis though directly inapplicable to post-10/1/2018 agreements.
- *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635 (2004) — reasonableness framework for restrictive covenants (legitimate business interest, reasonable in time and space, consonant with public interest).
- *Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462 (2018) — choice-of-law and forum-selection limits where a covenant would violate the law of the state of the employee's residence.
- *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280 (1974) — legitimate business interest standard: trade secrets, other confidential information, and employer goodwill (not protection from ordinary competition).
- *Packaging Indus. Grp., Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609 (1980) — Massachusetts preliminary injunction standard: likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of harms, public interest.
- Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq. (DTSA) — federal civil cause of action for trade-secret misappropriation.
- G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G (Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, "MUTSA") — state trade-secret regime.
- Practitioner treatise: *Massachusetts Employment Law* (MCLE, 2024 ed.), ch. 11 [illustrative secondary source, internal use only].

**Client objectives (ranked by Shaughnessy in 4/16/2026 intake):**
1. Determine whether the non-compete is enforceable in its current form against Narayanan's employment at Kettlepoint AI.
2. If enforceable only in part, identify which specific provisions are vulnerable and whether Massachusetts courts would blue-pencil rather than void.
3. Assess likelihood of success on a preliminary-injunction motion sufficient to justify the ~$75K fee estimate for expedited briefing and a hearing within 21 days.
4. Flag exposure and leverage from the USB-copy forensic findings (DTSA and MUTSA theories as alternative or parallel claims independent of the covenant).
5. Preserve the option to settle via a modified, narrower restriction (e.g., customer-specific non-solicit for the three named accounts) rather than litigate through judgment.
6. Do not take a position on prospective fee-shifting (§ 24L(c)) at this stage — partner will address separately.

## Artifact specification
Format: traditional office memorandum (not a brief). Required sections, in order:

1. **Heading block** — TO / FROM / DATE / RE / PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL — ATTORNEY WORK PRODUCT.
2. **Question Presented** — single sentence framing, with sub-questions permissible.
3. **Brief Answer** — 1–2 paragraphs. Predictive, not advocative. Probability language calibrated ("likely," "substantial risk," "close question").
4. **Statement of Facts** — neutral recitation in narrative form; no argument. Include dates, roles, documents, forensic findings.
5. **Discussion / Analysis** — organized by issue using IRAC or CRAC sub-structure. Must analyze under the MNAA **each of**: (a) timing and notice requirement (§ 24L(b)(i)); (b) independent consideration requirement given this was a promotion, not initial hire; (c) garden-leave or "mutually-agreed-upon consideration" (§ 24L(b)(vii)); (d) reasonableness of 12-month duration; (e) geographic scope ("US and Canada"); (f) activity scope; (g) legitimate business interest (trade secrets/goodwill); (h) choice-of-law enforceability against a Massachusetts resident. Separately analyze (i) likelihood of success under *Packaging Industries* four-factor PI test; and (j) parallel trade-secret theory under DTSA and G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G (MUTSA) given the USB-copy events.
6. **Recommendation** — actionable options ranked; include blue-pencil scenarios, PI v. TRO tradeoff, and settlement posture.
7. **Open issues / further investigation needed** — what we do not yet know that materially affects the analysis.

Citation style: **Bluebook (21st ed.)**, full citations on first use, short-form thereafter. Massachusetts practitioners' conventions (e.g., "G.L. c. 149, § 24L") acceptable. If a fictional authority is used for pedagogical completeness, flag it with "[illustrative]" in a footnote.

Tone: formal, precise, predictive (not adversarial). Associate-to-partner register. Hedge calibrated — avoid both overconfidence and mush. No rhetorical flourishes; no second-person voice.

## Output format
Single Markdown file. Target length: 2,200–3,000 words of substantive analysis (not counting heading block or citations). Inline citations; a short reference list at the end is optional.

## Iteration targets
1. **Separate the MNAA statutory analysis from the common-law reasonableness analysis.** v1 will likely conflate §24L elements (which are threshold validity requirements) with *Boulanger*/*Marine Contractors* reasonableness (which applies on top). Restructure so the reader sees validity first, then reasonableness.
2. **Sharpen the independent-consideration analysis for the 2024 promotion-triggered agreement.** The $90,000 retention bonus is likely sufficient, but v1 often hand-waves; cite § 24L(b)(vii) and distinguish from pre-MNAA *Automile* reasoning, which is suggestive but not controlling post-10/1/2018.
3. **Quantify the geographic-scope problem.** "US and Canada" is facially overbroad; analyze whether blue-penciling to "US states where Halston has booked revenue in the last 24 months" survives and whether Massachusetts courts will reform rather than void.
4. **Integrate the USB-copy forensic findings into both the PI irreparable-harm prong AND a standalone DTSA/MUTSA theory** — currently likely treated as a sidebar; it is probably the strongest ground for injunctive relief independent of the covenant.
5. **Calibrate the Brief Answer.** v1 often either hedges into uselessness ("depends on many factors") or overstates ("clearly enforceable"). Target: "The 12-month covenant is likely enforceable in substantially its current form as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business, though the US-and-Canada geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk; the stronger near-term injunctive theory rests on trade-secret misappropriation."
6. **Flag the choice-of-law vulnerability** raised by Narayanan's two-day-a-week Providence remote work — is she "primarily resident or employed" in Massachusetts within § 24L(e)? Requires more factual development before PI filing.

## Success criteria
- Question Presented and Brief Answer are predictive and specifically tied to the MNAA framework, not generic.
- Every § 24L sub-element (notice, consideration, garden leave, 12-month cap, reasonableness, legitimate interest, choice-of-law) is addressed with a holding-level conclusion, not skipped.
- Citation form is consistent Bluebook 21st; any fictional authority is flagged.
- Analysis differentiates *Automile* (pre-statute) from post-MNAA law rather than treating them interchangeably.
- PI analysis applies *Packaging Industries* four-factor test explicitly, with facts mapped to each factor.
- Recommendation gives the partner at least three distinct actionable paths (full PI, narrowed TRO on trade-secret theory, settlement toward customer-specific non-solicit) with tradeoffs.
- No advocacy tone creeps in; the memo reads as neutral predictive analysis, not a draft brief.

## Scope target

Likely deferred to iteration: the choice-of-law analysis under § 24L(e) given the Providence remote-work fact, the full DTSA/MUTSA parallel theory, blue-pencil reformation mechanics, and Open Issues section. Expect approximately 1,400–1,700 words on first pass.
