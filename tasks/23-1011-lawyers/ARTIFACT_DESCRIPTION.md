# Office Memorandum — Enforceability of Non-Compete Against Former VP Under Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act

## Occupation
- **SOC:** 23-1011
- **Title:** Lawyers

## Scenario
You are a fourth-year associate at Thorne & Whitfield LLP (Boston, MA) representing Halston Analytics, Inc., a Delaware-incorporated B2B forecasting company headquartered in Cambridge. On April 3, 2026, Halston's former VP of Enterprise Sales, Priya Narayanan, resigned and — twelve days later — accepted a "Head of Revenue" role at Kettlepoint AI, a direct competitor in the retail-demand-forecasting vertical. Halston's General Counsel, Dana Shaughnessy, has asked for an internal office memorandum by Monday 4/27/2026 analyzing whether Narayanan's 12-month non-compete is enforceable under the Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act, G.L. c. 149, § 24L, so that the litigation partner (Marisol Reyes) can decide by week's end whether to file for a preliminary injunction in Suffolk Superior Court. The stakes: Narayanan reports to manage a book of roughly $14M ARR, and Halston's Q2 forecast assumes at least three named accounts (Brinkerhoff Foods, Meridian Logistics, Quellstream Biotech) do not churn in the next 90 days.

**Controlling authority and standards (partial list — not exhaustive):**
- **G.L. c. 149, § 24L** (Massachusetts Noncompetition Agreement Act) — effective for agreements entered on or after 10/1/2018. Governs: notice requirements (§ 24L(b)(i)), independent consideration for agreements post-dating employment (§ 24L(b)(ii)), 12-month duration cap (§ 24L(b)(iv)), reasonableness of geographic and activity scope (§ 24L(b)(v)–(vi)), garden-leave or "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" (§ 24L(b)(vii)), and choice-of-law / forum restrictions (§ 24L(e)).
- *Automile Holdings, LLC v. McGovern*, 483 Mass. 797 (2020) — pre-MNAA "material change" doctrine; still informs promotion-triggered agreement analysis though directly inapplicable to post-10/1/2018 agreements.
- *Boulanger v. Dunkin' Donuts Inc.*, 442 Mass. 635 (2004) — reasonableness framework for restrictive covenants (legitimate business interest, reasonable in time and space, consonant with public interest).
- *Oxford Global Resources, LLC v. Hernandez*, 480 Mass. 462 (2018) — choice-of-law and forum-selection limits where a covenant would violate the law of the state of the employee's residence.
- *Marine Contractors Co. v. Hurley*, 365 Mass. 280 (1974) — legitimate business interest standard: trade secrets, other confidential information, and employer goodwill (not protection from ordinary competition).
- *Packaging Indus. Grp., Inc. v. Cheney*, 380 Mass. 609 (1980) — Massachusetts preliminary injunction standard: likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of harms, public interest.
- Defend Trade Secrets Act, 18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq. (DTSA) — federal civil cause of action for trade-secret misappropriation.
- G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G (Massachusetts Uniform Trade Secrets Act, "MUTSA") — state trade-secret regime.
- Practitioner treatise: *Massachusetts Employment Law* (MCLE, 2024 ed.), ch. 11 [illustrative secondary source, internal use only].

## Artifact specification
Format: traditional office memorandum (not a brief). Required sections, in order:

1. **Heading block** — TO / FROM / DATE / RE / PRIVILEGED AND CONFIDENTIAL — ATTORNEY WORK PRODUCT.

2. **Question Presented** — single sentence framing, with sub-questions permissible. Should name the covenant, the statute, the act complained of, and the remedy sought.

3. **Brief Answer** — 1–2 paragraphs. Predictive, not advocative. Probability language calibrated ("likely," "substantial risk," "close question," "unlikely"). Should signal the bottom-line enforceability conclusion and the recommended procedural path without fully explaining reasoning.

4. **Statement of Facts** — neutral recitation in narrative form; no argument. Include: dates of hire, promotion, and signing of the covenant; contract terms verbatim where they matter to the analysis; the Providence remote-work facts; the USB-copy forensic findings; and Kettlepoint's public announcement.

5. **Discussion / Analysis** — organized by issue using IRAC or CRAC sub-structure. Must analyze under the MNAA **each of**:
   - (a) timing and notice requirement (§ 24L(b)(i)) — was the covenant presented at least 10 business days before acceptance or before the start of the new role?
   - (b) independent consideration requirement given this was a promotion, not initial hire — does § 24L(b)(ii) apply, and does the $90,000 retention bonus plus promotion satisfy it?
   - (c) garden-leave or "other mutually-agreed-upon consideration" (§ 24L(b)(vii)) — does 40% of highest annualized base salary meet the statutory floor, and is it paid pro-rata on the required schedule?
   - (d) reasonableness of 12-month duration (§ 24L(b)(iv)) — at or below the statutory cap, but reasonableness as applied;
   - (e) geographic scope ("United States and Canada") — (§ 24L(b)(v));
   - (f) activity scope ("demand-forecasting or revenue-intelligence software to retail, CPG, or logistics customers") — (§ 24L(b)(vi));
   - (g) legitimate business interest (trade secrets and goodwill under *Marine Contractors*);
   - (h) choice-of-law enforceability against a Massachusetts resident with Rhode Island residence (§ 24L(e); *Oxford Global*).

   Separately analyze:
   - (i) likelihood of success on a PI motion under *Packaging Industries* four-factor test (likelihood of success, irreparable harm, balance of harms, public interest), mapping facts to each factor;
   - (j) parallel trade-secret theory under DTSA (18 U.S.C. §§ 1836 et seq.) and G.L. c. 93, §§ 42–42G (MUTSA), given the USB-copy events — whether the misappropriation claim could support injunctive relief independent of the covenant.

6. **Recommendation** — actionable options ranked. Include at minimum:
   - full preliminary injunction enforcing the covenant as written, with blue-pencil fallback;
   - narrower TRO grounded in trade-secret misappropriation only (no reliance on covenant);
   - negotiated standstill pending a customer-specific non-solicit agreement;
   - decision to stand down and monitor.

7. **Open issues / further investigation needed** — what we do not yet know that materially affects the analysis (e.g., Rhode Island work-site facts, content of USB-copied files, Kettlepoint's internal policies on receipt of information from new hires, whether Narayanan has returned all physical and cloud-hosted materials).

Citation style: **Bluebook (21st ed.)**, full citations on first use, short-form thereafter. Massachusetts practitioners' conventions (e.g., "G.L. c. 149, § 24L") acceptable. If a fictional authority is used for pedagogical completeness, flag it with "[illustrative]" in a footnote.

Tone: formal, precise, predictive (not adversarial). Associate-to-partner register. Hedge calibrated — avoid both overconfidence and mush. No rhetorical flourishes; no second-person voice.

## Output format
Single Markdown file. Target length: 2,200–3,000 words of substantive analysis (not counting heading block or citations). Inline citations; a short reference list at the end is optional.

## Iteration targets
1. **Separate the MNAA statutory analysis from the common-law reasonableness analysis.** v1 will likely conflate § 24L elements (which are threshold validity requirements) with *Boulanger*/*Marine Contractors* reasonableness (which applies on top). Restructure so the reader sees validity first, then reasonableness — a covenant can be statutorily valid yet fail reasonableness, or vice versa.
2. **Sharpen the independent-consideration analysis for the 2024 promotion-triggered agreement.** The $90,000 retention bonus and the promotion itself are likely sufficient, but v1 often hand-waves by citing "adequate consideration" generically. Cite § 24L(b)(vii) specifically, distinguish the separate garden-leave requirement, and explain why pre-MNAA *Automile* reasoning is suggestive but not controlling post-10/1/2018.
3. **Quantify the geographic-scope problem.** "United States and Canada" is facially overbroad for a business that (per client data) has booked revenue in only 29 US states and one Canadian province over the last 24 months. Analyze whether blue-penciling to "US states and Canadian provinces where Halston has booked revenue in the 24 months preceding termination" survives, and whether Massachusetts courts prefer reformation to voiding (they do, under *All Stainless, Inc. v. Colby*, 364 Mass. 773 (1974), but the reader should not take this for granted).
4. **Integrate the USB-copy forensic findings into both the PI irreparable-harm prong AND a standalone DTSA/MUTSA theory.** v1 often treats forensic findings as a sidebar; they are likely the strongest near-term ground for injunctive relief independent of the covenant, and independently support a seizure motion under DTSA § 1836(b)(2) in extraordinary circumstances (probably not here, but worth addressing).
5. **Calibrate the Brief Answer.** v1 often either hedges into uselessness ("depends on many factors") or overstates ("clearly enforceable"). Target register: "The 12-month covenant is likely enforceable in substantially its current form as to Kettlepoint's retail-forecasting business, though the US-and-Canada geographic scope presents a substantial reformation risk; the stronger near-term injunctive theory rests on trade-secret misappropriation under DTSA and MUTSA."
6. **Flag the choice-of-law vulnerability** raised by Narayanan's two-day-a-week Providence remote work — is she "primarily resident or employed" in Massachusetts within § 24L(e) at the time of termination? The statute looks to the preceding 30 days; the facts suggest yes, but the answer is not automatic and requires factual development (timesheet records, expense reports) before PI filing. Note also that Rhode Island's own non-compete statute, R.I. Gen. Laws § 28-59-1 et seq., would be materially more restrictive if it applied.
7. **Address the "24-month" consideration lookback** in § 24L(b)(vii) for garden leave. The agreement uses "highest annualized base salary during the two-year period preceding termination" — tracks the statute. Confirm and do not over-analyze; one paragraph suffices.

## Success criteria
- Question Presented and Brief Answer are predictive and specifically tied to the MNAA framework, not generic — a reader should see the bottom-line enforceability conclusion and recommended path from the first page.
- Every § 24L sub-element (notice, consideration, garden leave, 12-month cap, geographic reasonableness, activity-scope reasonableness, legitimate interest, choice-of-law) is addressed with a holding-level conclusion ("This element is satisfied" / "This element presents a substantial risk"), not skipped or buried.
- Citation form is consistent Bluebook 21st; any fictional authority is flagged "[illustrative]" in a footnote rather than presented as real.
- Analysis differentiates *Automile* (pre-statute, applying the common-law "material change" doctrine) from post-MNAA law rather than treating them interchangeably — the memo should make clear that *Automile* is informative but not controlling for this 2024 agreement.
- PI analysis applies the *Packaging Industries* four-factor test explicitly, with facts mapped to each factor; irreparable-harm analysis should distinguish between harm flowing from breach of the covenant and harm flowing from trade-secret misappropriation.
- Recommendation gives the partner at least three distinct actionable paths (e.g., full PI enforcing the covenant; narrowed TRO on trade-secret theory only; negotiated standstill toward a customer-specific non-solicit) with tradeoffs on cost, probability of success, and client relationship impact.
- No advocacy tone creeps in; the memo reads as neutral predictive analysis for an internal audience, not a draft brief or a client-facing advocacy piece.
- The forensic USB-copy evidence is treated as a discrete factual input with its own weight, not as a rhetorical flourish attached to the covenant analysis.
